# Southern Hip-Hop

Southern hip-hop is a broad regional genre encompassing the diverse hip-hop scenes that emerged across the American South from the late 1980s onward, unified by a shared emphasis on heavy bass, slower tempos, melodic hooks, call-and-response chant structures, and a sonic aesthetic rooted in funk, soul, gospel, and blues traditions distinct from the boom-bap of New York and the G-funk of Los Angeles. The sound varies significantly by city — Houston's chopped-and-screwed syrup-slow grooves, Atlanta's crisp snap music and later trap production built on Roland TR-808 drum machines, Memphis's dark lo-fi horror-influenced beats, New Orleans's uptempo bounce rhythms, and Miami's bass-heavy party music — but is consistently characterized by trunk-rattling sub-bass frequencies, synthesizer-driven production, and vocal deliveries that range from drawling storytelling to rapid-fire triplet flows. Pioneered by artists like the Geto Boys, OutKast, UGK, Three 6 Mafia, and Master P, Southern hip-hop fought for recognition against coastal dominance throughout the 1990s before achieving total commercial supremacy in the 2000s through the rise of crunk, snap, and ultimately trap music. The genre distinguishes itself from East Coast hip-hop through its emphasis on groove, melody, and sonic weight over lyrical density, and from West Coast hip-hop through its deeper roots in Southern Black musical traditions and its more diverse array of regional sub-styles. Southern hip-hop's trap subgenre has become the dominant sound in global popular music, making the South the most commercially influential region in hip-hop history.
