# Industrial Hip-Hop

Industrial Hip-Hop fuses the rhythmic structures and vocal delivery of hip-hop with the abrasive textures, mechanical repetition, and confrontational aesthetics of industrial music, creating a sound defined by distorted beats, noise layers, and an atmosphere of urban dystopia and psychological extremity. The genre's sonic signature centers on heavily processed drum machines, harsh synthesizer tones, feedback, tape manipulation, and production techniques that deliberately degrade audio fidelity to create a sense of claustrophobic menace. Pioneered by acts like Dalek in the early 2000s and brought to mainstream visibility by Death Grips in the 2010s, the genre occupies a space where boom-bap rhythmic foundations collide with shoegaze walls of noise, power electronics, and punk-rock aggression. Vocal approaches range from Dalek's monotone, buried-in-the-mix delivery to Death Grips' MC Ride's frantic screaming and JPEGMAFIA's fragmented, glitch-hop cadences, united by a rejection of conventional hip-hop clarity and flow in favor of visceral, confrontational expression. Industrial Hip-Hop distinguishes itself from experimental hip-hop through its specific debt to the industrial and noise music lineage of Throbbing Gristle, Einsturzende Neubauten, and Godflesh, and from noise rap through its retention of structured beats and identifiable hip-hop rhythmic patterns beneath the abrasive surface.

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Dälek | Absence | 2005 | This album is a cornerstone of the genre, blending dense, shoegaze-influenced wall-of-sound textures with gritty, boom-bap rhythms and bleak lyrical themes. It prioritizes atmosphere and noise over traditional hip-hop clarity, a sound also explored in their other essential works like From Filth It Shall Rise and Abandoned Language. |
| Death Grips | The Money Store | 2012 | A landmark release that defined the modern era of industrial hip-hop through its use of glitchy electronics, punk-rock energy, and frantic, shouted vocals. The production is abrasive and unpredictable, traits that are further pushed to extremes on their albums Exmilitary and No Love Deep Web. |
| JPEGMAFIA | Veteran | 2018 | Characterized by its fragmented, "online-era" production, this album uses harsh samples and sudden sonic shifts to create a disorienting, high-tension listening experience. JPEGMAFIA's confrontational delivery and experimental structure are also central to his other notable releases, All My Heroes Are Cornballs and LP!. |
| Clipping. | Splendor & Misery | 2016 | This Hugo Award-nominated concept album utilizes musique concrète and harsh noise as the backdrop for intricate, fast-paced storytelling set in deep space. The trio's commitment to using non-musical sounds as percussion is also masterfully displayed on their self-titled debut and There Existed an Addiction to Blood. |
| Techno Animal | The Brotherhood of the Bomb | 2001 | A collaboration between Kevin Martin (The Bug) and Justin Broadrick (Godflesh), this album features crushing, distorted beats and "dirty" basslines that bridge the gap between industrial metal and hip-hop. Their influence on the genre's darker, mechanical side is also evident in the Radioactive EP. |
| Bavu Blakes (as part of The Point) | The Point | 2002 | While lesser-known, this project is a masterclass in combining minimalist, mechanical rhythmic patterns with raw, unpolished vocal takes. It captures the "factory-floor" aesthetic of industrial music through its repetitive, grinding loops and sparse arrangements. |
| B L A C K I E | Spittin' My Shit | 2008 | A pioneer of "American Grime" and noise-rap, B L A C K I E uses extreme distortion and blown-out speakers to create a visceral, physical sound. This lo-fi, high-decibel approach is a recurring theme in his discography, including the influential Wildness and True Spirit and Not Giving a Fuck. |
| Ho99o9 | United States of Horror | 2017 | This album fuses the aggression of hardcore punk with the rhythmic intensity of industrial hip-hop, featuring distorted synthesizers and chaotic vocal performances. Their cross-genre experimentation is also highlighted on the Dead Bodies in the Lake mixtape and Skin. |
| Moodie Black | Lucas Acid | 2018 | A prominent voice in "noise-rap," Moodie Black crafts a claustrophobic atmosphere using dense layers of static, feedback, and slowed-down beats. The album's focus on trans identity and emotional rawness is echoed in their other essential release, Fuzz. |
| Shabazz Palaces | Black Up | 2011 | This album redefined the genre's boundaries by incorporating avant-garde jazz sensibilities and complex, futuristic percussion into a dark, industrial framework. Their penchant for cryptic lyricism and abstract soundscapes is further developed on Lese Majesty. |
| El-P | I'll Sleep When You're Dead | 2007 | Featuring paranoid, sci-fi-inflected production, this album uses dissonant synths and aggressive percussion to mirror the anxiety of urban life. El-P's signature "overcast" sound is also fundamental to his debut Fantastic Damage and his later work with Run The Jewels. |
| Consolidated | The Myth of Rock | 1990 | Early pioneers who blended political activism with hard-hitting industrial beats and sampled speech. Their sound is defined by a heavy reliance on metallic percussion and funk-influenced basslines, as also heard on Friendly Fa$cism. |
| Meat Beat Manifesto | 99% | 1990 | Though often associated with big beat, this album's heavy use of hip-hop breaks layered with industrial samples and dub techniques was foundational for the genre. Jack Dangers' innovative use of the sampler is also showcased on Storm the Studio. |
| Scarlxrd | Dxxm | 2018 | A key figure in the "trap metal" scene, Scarlxrd uses industrial-grade distortion and screaming vocals over traditional trap 808s to create an overwhelming wall of sound. His high-energy, abrasive style is also prominent on Lxrdszn and Immortalisation. |
| The Bug | London Zoo | 2008 | While rooted in dancehall and dub, the sheer weight of the industrial distortion and the aggressive delivery of the guest emcees make this a genre definitive. Kevin Martin's obsession with "sonic warfare" is equally present on Angels & Devils and Fire. |
| Injury Reserve | By the Time I Get to Phoenix | 2021 | Released following the death of member Stepa J. Groggs, this album is a haunting exploration of grief set against glitchy, industrial, and post-rap production. Its deconstructed approach to song structure is a shift from their earlier work like Live from the Dentist Office. |
| Backxwash | I Lie Here Buried with My Rings and My Dresses | 2021 | This Polaris Prize-winning artist blends industrial noise with horrorcore and metal influences, featuring harrowing lyrics and crushing production. Her dark, occult-themed aesthetic is also central to the album God Has Nothing to Do with This Leave Him Out of It. |
| Ghostmane | Anti-Icon | 2020 | This project represents a peak in the fusion of industrial metal and hip-hop, utilizing mechanical rhythms, high-pitched shrieks, and deep, distorted bass. Ghostmane's evolution into a more industrial sound is also visible on his N/O/I/S/E album. |
| Cities Aviv | Black Pleasure | 2012 | A masterpiece of lo-fi, industrial-tinged rap, this album uses obscured samples and cavernous reverb to create a sense of urban isolation. His experimental approach to the genre is further explored on the albums Digital Lows and Come to Life. |
| Denzel Curry | 13 (EP) | 2017 | While Curry is a versatile artist, this EP captures his most aggressive, industrial-leaning phase with distorted production and high-octane delivery. This dark, high-pressure sound also appears on tracks from TA13OO and ZUU. |
| Lil Ugly Mane | Mista Thug Isolation | 2012 | Though heavily influenced by Memphis rap, the album's grimy, industrial-tinged production and lo-fi aesthetic made it a cult classic in the underground noise-rap scene. His more overtly experimental work can be found on Oblivion Access. |
| Show Me the Body | Dog Whistle | 2019 | Fusing New York hardcore with hip-hop cadences and industrial electronics, this album features a unique "sludge-rap" sound driven by distorted banjo and heavy percussion. Their raw, live energy is also captured on Corpus I. |
| Bob Vylan | We Live Here | 2020 | Combining the sociopolitical bite of grime with the raw aggression of industrial punk-rock, this duo creates a confrontational and rhythmic sound. Their focus on systemic issues and high-energy production continues on Bob Vylan Presents the Price of Life. |
| ZelooperZ | Dyn-O-Mite | 2019 | As a member of the Bruiser Brigade, ZelooperZ utilizes eccentric vocal delivery and glitchy, industrial-leaning production to create a jagged, unpredictable sound. His experimental tendencies are also on display on Van Gogh's Left Ear. |
| Prurient | Unknown Room | 2018 | Dominick Fernow (Prurient) brings his power-electronics background into a hip-hop context on certain projects, using cold, metallic synths and rhythmic noise. This "industrial-rap" crossover is a niche but vital part of his expansive discography, including Frozen Niagara Falls. |
| Author & Punisher | Beastland | 2018 | Known for playing custom-built "drone machines," Tristan Shone creates mechanical, industrial rhythms that occasionally lean into hip-hop structures. The heavy, rhythmic crushing sound is a hallmark of his albums like Krüller and Women & Children. |
| NAH | Mortal To The Core | 2018 | A percussion-heavy project that uses live drums, found-sound samples, and distorted vocals to create a chaotic, industrial hip-hop hybrid. The DIY, high-energy aesthetic is consistent across his prolific output, including Garbage Man. |
| Ratking | So It Goes | 2014 | This album captures a modern New York sound using busy, industrial-influenced production and complex multi-syllabic rapping. The interplay between Wiki's vocals and Sporting Life's gritty, sample-heavy beats is also evident on their Wiki93 EP. |
| Saul Williams | MartyrLoserKing | 2016 | Williams blends his background in spoken word with aggressive industrial beats and electronic textures to explore themes of global hacking and digital resistance. His self-titled 2004 album also features significant industrial and rock-rap influences. |
| 3640 (as part of Tackhead) | Friendly Fa$cism | 1991 | A collaboration involving Adrian Sherwood, this project utilized heavy dub processing and industrial-strength hip-hop rhythms to create a dense, politically charged sound. Their influence is also felt in the production work for artists like Mark Stewart on As the Veneer of Democracy Begins to Fade. |
