# Lo-Fi Hip-Hop

Lo-fi hip-hop is a subgenre of instrumental hip-hop that emerged in the early 2010s from online communities, characterized by its deliberately degraded audio quality — vinyl crackle, tape hiss, muffled filtering, and low-bitrate warmth — layered over mellow boom-bap drum patterns and jazzy, soul-sampled melodic loops that create a nostalgic, contemplative atmosphere designed for relaxation, study, and introspection. The sound is built around MPC-style chopped samples of jazz piano, bossa nova guitar, and soul vocals, paired with soft kick drums, understated hi-hats, and sub-bass frequencies that give the music a gentle, hypnotic pulse, often enhanced by ambient field recordings of rain, city sounds, or domestic atmospheres. Rooted in the production legacies of Nujabes, J Dilla, and Madlib — whose unquantized drum programming, dusty crate-digging aesthetic, and jazz-soul sampling techniques form the genre's foundational DNA — lo-fi hip-hop translated these underground hip-hop traditions into an ambient, loop-based format optimized for the streaming era. The genre distinguishes itself from standard instrumental hip-hop through its emphasis on mood and atmosphere over rhythmic complexity, and from chillhop through its more deliberately lo-fidelity production aesthetic and shorter, loop-centric track structures. Lo-fi hip-hop became a global cultural phenomenon through YouTube livestreams and curated playlists, establishing itself as the defining background music genre of the digital age.
