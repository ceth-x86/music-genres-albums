# Drill

Drill is an aggressive subgenre of hip-hop that originated in Chicago's South Side around 2011-2012, characterized by dark, nihilistic lyricism and cold, mechanical production. The sound is built on ominous synthesizer melodies, rolling hi-hat patterns, heavy 808 bass, and sharp snare drums, creating an atmosphere of tension and menace. Vocal delivery tends toward deadpan, monotone, or raw and confrontational, reflecting the genre's unflinching depiction of street violence and neighborhood loyalty. Chicago drill's original wave, driven by producers like Young Chop, inspired a major UK mutation in the mid-2010s that introduced sliding 808 basslines, darker atmospheres, and more complex rhythmic patterns over the distinctive UK "drill beat." This British variant then looped back across the Atlantic to birth Brooklyn drill, popularized by Pop Smoke, which fused UK production elements with New York's aggressive energy. The genre has since spread globally, spawning regional variants including Bronx sample drill and European adaptations, making it one of hip-hop's most geographically diverse and rapidly evolving subgenres.

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Chief Keef | Finally Rich | 2012 | This landmark release popularized the Chicago drill sound globally, featuring Young Chop's cold, mechanical production and Keef's nihilistic, deadpan delivery. It established the genre's focus on neighborhood pride and bleak street realities; other essential works include Back from the Dead. |
| Pop Smoke | Meet the Woo | 2019 | This mixtape defined the Brooklyn drill aesthetic by merging UK-style sliding 808 basslines with Pop Smoke's gravelly, baritone vocals and aggressive energy. It cemented the "Woo" sound as a dominant force in modern hip-hop; Meet the Woo 2 is also a cornerstone of his discography. |
| King Louie | Showtime | 2012 | Often credited with coining the term "drill," King Louie brought a more rhythmic, flow-heavy approach to the genre's early skeletal beats. This album showcases the transition from Chicago's trap roots into the distinct, repetitive drill hypnotic style; Drilluminati is another pivotal release. |
| Lil Durk | Signed to the Streets | 2013 | Durk introduced a melodic, auto-tuned vulnerability to the harsh Chicago landscape, proving that drill could be both gritty and tuneful. This project balanced aggressive anthems with emotive storytelling; he continued this evolution on Just Cause Y'all Waited. |
| 67 | Let's Lurk | 2016 | As pioneers of UK drill, 67 moved the sound away from US influences by incorporating darker, road-rap sensibilities and haunting, cinematic production. The title track remains one of the most influential anthems in the London scene; The 67 Project is another vital listen. |
| G Herbo | Welcome to Fazoland | 2014 | Released under the name Lil Herb, this mixtape is a masterclass in "no-limit" drill, featuring breathless, off-beat flows and vivid, harrowing lyricism. It captures the frantic intensity of Chicago's South Side better than almost any other record; Pistol P Project is also highly regarded. |
| Fredo Santana | Trappin Ain't Dead | 2013 | Representing the darker, "scary" side of the movement, Fredo's debut studio album utilized horror-inspired atmospheres and slow, menacing tempos. His presence defined the "savage" persona central to early drill; It's a Scary Site is an equally definitive mixtape. |
| Headie One | Music x Road | 2019 | Headie One is celebrated for his unique "flow-state" delivery and ability to adapt drill to more complex, artful structures. This album bridged the gap between underground UK drill and mainstream success without losing its raw edge; The State of Mind is also essential. |
| L'A Capone | Separate Myself | 2014 | Released posthumously, this project showcased Capone's technical rapping ability and star potential within the Chicago scene. His crisp delivery and infectious energy provided a blueprint for many technical drill rappers who followed; he is often cited as one of the genre's greatest "what ifs." |
| Loski | Call Me Loose | 2018 | A leading figure in the Harlem Spartans, Loski brought a lighter, more rhythmic "skipping" flow to the typically heavy UK drill sound. This mixtape influenced a shift toward more danceable yet lyrically sharp drill tracks; Mad Move is another standout in his catalog. |
| Digga D | Double Tap Diaries | 2019 | Digga D is known for his intricate wordplay and the ability to turn street narratives into viral hits despite heavy censorship. This project features the hallmark "sliding" bass and sharp percussion that define the West London drill sound; Made in the Pyrex is his other major work. |
| Young Chop | Precious | 2013 | While primarily a producer, Chop's solo album highlights the "Chop Squad" sound: heavy snares, bright synth brass, and rolling hi-hats that defined the first wave of drill. It serves as a definitive sonic template for the genre's instrumentation; his work on Still further solidified this style. |
| Fivio Foreign | 800 BC | 2020 | This EP helped propel Brooklyn drill into the "Big Drip" era, characterized by high-energy ad-libs and glossy, percussion-heavy production. It refined the party-ready side of the subgenre; he expanded on this sound with his debut studio album, B.I.B.L.E. |
| Dutchavelli | Dutch From The 5th | 2020 | Known for his powerful, raspy voice, Dutchavelli brought a sense of physical weight and grit to the UK drill scene. This project emphasizes booming bass and direct, intimidating lyricism; he is also noted for his high-profile collaborations across Europe. |
| Lil Reese | Don't Like | 2012 | This mixtape includes the original version of the genre's most famous track, "I Don't Like," and exemplifies the sparse, cold production of early Chicago. Reese's whispery, menacing delivery became a staple of the "Savage" aesthetic; Supa Savage is another key release. |
| Unknown T | Rise Above Hate | 2020 | Unknown T utilized his deep voice and precise, percussive flow to create a distinct pocket within the UK scene. This album demonstrates how drill can incorporate orchestral elements and complex internal rhyme schemes; Homerton 2 Holly is another essential project. |
| 22Gz | The Blixky Tape | 2019 | Widely considered the pioneer of the Brooklyn drill sound, 22Gz brought the UK's production style to New York, creating the "Blixky" movement. His rapid-fire delivery over chaotic, sliding basslines set the standard for the region; Growth & Development is also a core text. |
| MizOrMac | Return of the Mac (Singles) | 2020 | Though he lacks a traditional "album" due to legal issues, his collected 2020 output represents the peak of UK drill lyricism and flow complexity. His sharp, erratic delivery over haunting melodies influenced an entire generation of London rappers; his work with Harlem Spartans is foundational. |
| SD | Life Of A Savage 3 | 2013 | A member of the GBE collective, SD brought a more polished, melodic approach to the raw Chicago sound. This project features the genre's typical dark themes but with a higher focus on song structure and hook-writing; Truly Blessed is another notable title. |
| CB | A Drillers Perspective | 2019 | Often cited as the "purest" UK drill project, this album is defined by its relentlessly dark atmosphere and authentic, uncompromising street reporting. The production is minimal and eerie, focusing entirely on the rapper's grit; A Drillers Perspective 2 followed in a similar vein. |
| Sheff G | The Unluccy Luccy Kid | 2019 | Sheff G was instrumental in shifting Brooklyn drill toward a more low-tempo, menacing vibe inspired by UK "dark" drill. This album features his signature calm, baritone flow over heavy, distorted 808s; One and Only also defines his influential style. |
| K Trap | The Last Whip | 2017 | K Trap combined the "trappy" elements of London rap with the emerging drill structure, characterized by his "balaclava" persona and muffled, gritty delivery. This mixtape is a bridge between the road rap era and the drill explosion; Trapo is another essential release. |
| Bis | The Product | 2020 | A member of Harlem Spartans, Bis was known for his unique, high-pitched vocal inflections and creative metaphors. This posthumous release captures the "Blue Borough" sound of South London with its mix of aggression and melancholy; his contributions to Kennington Where It Started are legendary. |
| V9 | Yudoku | 2019 | V9 brought a "supervillain" aesthetic to the genre, using anime-inspired themes and a high-energy, chaotic rapping style. The production on this project is fast-paced and aggressive, mirroring the intensity of the Homerton scene; Murda is another key project. |
| Kay Flock | The D.O.A. Tape | 2021 | This project defined the Bronx "Sample Drill" era, utilizing sped-up soul and pop samples over frantic, high-bpm drill drums. Kay Flock's raw, screaming delivery perfectly matched the chaotic energy of this new regional variant; he is the face of the "D.O.A." movement. |
| Skengdo & AM | 2 Bunny | 2017 | This duo perfected the "back-and-forth" drill style, with AM providing deep, rhythmic verses and Skengdo offering higher-energy counterpoints. The mixtape is a masterclass in chemistry and mood-setting within the South London drill landscape; Back Like We Never Left is also vital. |
| Buni | Gulag | 2021 | Buni represents the aggressive, high-octane side of the Russ Millions-led "plug" drill sound. This project features fast, repetitive flows and production designed for clubs and viral challenges; it showcases the genre's transition into a more commercial, upbeat space. |
| Central Cee | Wild West | 2021 | While often veering into "Melodic Drill," this mixtape solidified the genre's global commercial viability using sleek production and a more accessible, refined flow. It retains the essential drill percussion but adds a polished, radio-friendly sheen; 23 is his other major success. |
| Zone 2 | Known Zoo | 2017 | This collective project from Peckham's Zone 2 is a foundational text for "rude" drill, emphasizing direct confrontation and haunting, minimal beats. It showcases the raw, unfiltered energy that initially made the genre a controversial lightning rod in the UK; Hillside Zoo is equally important. |
| Rah Swish | Mayor of the Streets | 2021 | A close associate of Pop Smoke, Rah Swish carries the "Woo" torch with a more technical, rapid-fire flow. This album epitomizes the late-stage Brooklyn drill sound: professional, aggressive, and highly rhythmic; BK Belly is another definitive project. |
