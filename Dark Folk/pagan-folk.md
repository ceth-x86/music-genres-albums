# Pagan Folk

Pagan Folk is a contemporary folk genre rooted in pre-Christian European spiritual traditions, combining traditional and historical acoustic instrumentation with themes drawn from Norse, Celtic, Slavic, and broader Indo-European mythologies. Unlike the broader Dark Folk movement, Pagan Folk emphasizes a direct, often celebratory connection to nature worship, seasonal cycles, and polytheistic cosmologies, manifested through music that ranges from the solemn and ritualistic to the festive and communal. Key instrumentation includes hurdy-gurdy, nyckelharpa, lute, bagpipes, various flutes, frame drums, and bodhr\u00e1n, frequently played on handmade or historically reconstructed instruments alongside group vocals that employ techniques such as polyphonic singing and kulning. The genre gained momentum through the early 2000s European medieval folk festival circuit, with groups like Faun, Omnia, and Wardruna establishing distinct regional approaches ranging from German medieval revivalism to Scandinavian runic interpretation. Production values vary widely, from the raw field-recording aesthetic of underground acts to the cinematic polish of internationally successful ensembles. Pagan Folk distinguishes itself from Ritual Folk through its greater emphasis on musicality and song structure, and from traditional folk revival through its explicit engagement with spiritual practice and mythological content rather than purely secular cultural preservation.

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Wardruna | Runaljod - Gap Var Ginnunga | 2009 | This album pioneered the use of historical Nordic instruments like the taglharpa and goat horn alongside ambient field recordings of nature. It focuses on the proto-Norse runes, creating a primal, ritualistic atmosphere that avoids metal tropes entirely; Runaljod - Yggdrasil and Kvitravn are also essential works. |
| Faun | Licht | 2003 | This release epitomizes the "Pagan Folk" sound through the masterly blend of hurdy-gurdy, nyckelharpa, and lute with polyphonic chants. It emphasizes medieval European melodies and forest-themed mysticism before the band moved toward more polished pop-folk sounds; Eden and Renaissance are other key entries. |
| Omnia | PaganFolk | 2006 | As the album that literally named the genre's modern movement, it features "Musick" played on handmade acoustic instruments like the sliding didgeridoo and Celtic harp. The sound is high-energy, earth-centered, and strictly acoustic, celebrating Druidic and nature-based themes; Alive! and Wolf Love are also significant. |
| Heilung | Ofnir | 2015 | Using "amplified history," this album utilizes human bones, throat singing, and rhythmic skin drums to recreate a pre-Christian Iron Age atmosphere. It is purely percussive and vocal-driven, stripping away modern song structures for a deep, trance-like ritual experience; Futha is their other monumental recording. |
| Forndom | Dauðra Dura | 2016 | This project focuses on the darker, more melancholic side of Scandinavian folklore using minimalist ambient textures and bowed lyre. The atmosphere is thick with the feeling of ancient burial mounds and Norse cosmology, represented further in the album Faþir. |
| Valravn | Valravn | 2007 | This Danish ensemble revitalized Faroese and Danish traditional ballads by adding a heavy, organic pulse and ethereal female vocals. The instrumentation remains rooted in the acoustic world, creating a bridge between medieval folk and modern pagan sensibilities; Koder på snor is also highly regarded. |
| Garmarna | Vittrad | 1993 | This album set the standard for Nordic folk revival, focusing on dark Swedish folklore and legends with a drone-heavy, acoustic-driven sound. The use of the fiddle and moraharpa creates a haunting, ancient aesthetic that defines the darker edge of the genre; Guds spelemän is another masterpiece. |
| Sowulo | SOL | 2016 | Built around the concept of the wheel of the year, this album uses strings and percussion to create a cinematic, atmospheric folk sound dedicated to the sun. It is entirely acoustic and focuses on the cyclical nature of the seasons, a theme also explored in MANN. |
| Danheim | Munarvágr | 2017 | This album specializes in a dark, brooding style of Viking-era folk that utilizes bone whistles and ambient forest sounds to evoke a sense of historical realism. It is deeply rhythmic and atmospheric, often used as a benchmark for the "dark pagan" sound; Herja is another notable title. |
| Dargaard | Eternity Rites | 1998 | While leaning toward the neoclassical, this album's focus on medieval ritualism and archaic atmospheres makes it a cornerstone of early pagan folk-ambient. It uses mournful melodies and synthetic orchestral textures to evoke ruined temples and forgotten gods; In Nomine Aeternitatis is equally essential. |
| The Moon and the Nightspirit | Regő Rejtem | 2007 | This Hungarian duo creates a lush, mystical landscape using violins, dulcimers, and dual male/female vocals singing in their native tongue. The music feels like a tribal incantation, deeply rooted in forest worship and Shamanic tradition; Őstükör and Holdrejtek are also vital. |
| Tenhi | Kauan | 1999 | This Finnish collective creates a somber, earthy folk sound that utilizes piano, flute, and acoustic guitar to mirror the desolate beauty of the Northern wilderness. It avoids the "tavern" style of folk in favor of a quiet, introspective paganism; Väre and Saivo are other definitive works. |
| Kati Ran | Lys | 2015 | Centered on the nyckelharpa and Kati's evocative vocals, this album explores the 13th-century Icelandic sagas and feminine energy within paganism. The production is organic and haunting, capturing a very specific Northern European folk spirit. |
| Sangre de Muerdago | Noite | 2018 | This Galician project focuses on "forest folk" using classical guitars, flutes, and hurdy-gurdies to create a soft, nostalgic atmosphere. The lyrics and melodies are deeply tied to the woods and ancient spirits of Iberia; O Camiño das Mans Valeiras is also essential. |
| Irfan | Irfan | 2003 | This Bulgarian group fuses Balkan folk with medieval and oriental influences, using instruments like the oud, kaval, and bodhrán. The ethereal vocals and sacred geometry of the rhythms create a "World Pagan" sound that is both ancient and timeless; Seraphim is another key release. |
| Nebala | Lust | 2021 | Founded by Jonas Lorentzen (formerly of Heilung), this project focuses on the reinterpretation of Old Norse poetry through a more melodic, yet still ritualistic, lens. It emphasizes the human voice and archaic strings to invoke the spirits of the Eddas. |
| Skáld | Le Chant des Vikings | 2019 | This French project utilizes multiple vocalists and traditional instruments to bring the oral traditions of the skalds to life. The sound is rhythmic and accessible while maintaining a strict focus on Old Norse lyrics and acoustic instrumentation. |
| Eihwar | Viking War Trance | 2023 | Although it incorporates electronic pulses, the core of the sound is driven by ritual drums and Nordic chants, representing the "trance" aspect of pagan rituals. It captures the high-energy, ecstatic side of the genre better than most traditional acts. |
| Nox Arcana | Winter's Knight | 2005 | While primarily known for gothic ambient, this specific album captures the haunting, snowy atmosphere of pagan midwinter rituals through harps and bells. It defines the "Winter Folk" aesthetic that is crucial to the genre's seasonal cycles; Grimm's Tales also touches on these themes. |
| Hedningarna | Trä | 1994 | This Swedish-Finnish group pioneered the use of "loud folk," using electrified versions of ancient instruments like the moraharpa and bagpipes. The energy is wild and untamed, perfectly capturing the chaotic side of pagan nature spirits; Kaksi! is another mandatory listen. |
| Laboratorium Pieśni | Rusalčyky | 2017 | This all-female vocal ensemble uses polyphonic "white voice" singing and shamanic drums to perform traditional Slavic ritual songs. The focus is entirely on the power of the human voice to connect with earth and ancestral spirits; Ouroboros is also significant. |
| Draugurinn | Í dvala | 2015 | This project explores the darker, chthonic side of paganism, focusing on themes of death and the underworld through deep drums and ritual chanting. It is more minimalist and drone-oriented, representing the "Occult Folk" overlap within the genre. |
| Osi and the Jupiter | Halls of the Slain | 2017 | Utilizing cello and acoustic guitar, this American project crafts a tribute to the vastness of the mountains and the old gods of the North. The sound is atmospheric and expansive, focusing on the spiritual connection between man and nature; Uthulingadagur is another standout. |
| Perneht | Sæ | 2021 | This project focuses on the Baltic tradition, specifically the pagan roots of Estonia, using the kannel (zither) and bowed lyre. The music is repetitive and meditative, designed to induce a state of ritual focus. |
| Trobar de Morte | Fairies' Wind | 2004 | This Spanish band focuses on the more "light" and "elemental" side of pagan folk, featuring flutes, harps, and ethereal female vocals. The music is centered on nature spirits, mythology, and the medieval Mediterranean landscape; Legends of Ithemar is also a core work. |
| Hagalaz' Runedance | The Winds That Sang of Fisherette's Song | 1998 | A seminal project by Andrea Haugen, this album focused on the feminine mysteries and Northern tradition through acoustic ballads and field recordings. It was one of the first modern projects to explicitly link folk music with neo-pagan spirituality; Volven is another essential title. |
| Nytt Land | Oðinn | 2018 | Hailing from Siberia, this duo uses Tuvan throat singing and traditional Nordic instruments to find the common ground between shamanism and Norse myth. The sound is raw, dusty, and incredibly rhythmic, evoking the vast steppes and ancient forests; Ritual is also a key album. |
| Fejd | Storm | 2009 | While the members have roots in metal, Fejd strictly uses traditional acoustic instruments like the bouzouki and willow flute to create a heavy, rhythmic folk sound. They represent the "battle-folk" energy without the use of distorted guitars; Eifur is another notable release. |
| Gealdýr | Sól | 2019 | This project emphasizes the melodic and soaring side of Old Norse chants, focusing on the poetic Eddas and the warmth of the sun. The production is clean and evocative, highlighting the clarity of the vocals over layers of strings. |
| Eivør | Slør | 2015 | While her career is varied, this album captures the Faroese "Gøta" pagan spirit through its use of hand drums and lyrics that feel like ancient sea-charms. It represents the "Oceanic" branch of pagan folk, where the elements of wind and water are the primary deities. |
