# Witch House

Witch House is a dark, occult-tinged electronic music microgenre that emerged around 2009-2010 from online communities and the DIY underground, blending elements of chopped and screwed hip-hop, industrial, darkwave, and shoegaze into a haunting, lo-fi aesthetic. The genre is defined by heavily pitch-shifted and reverb-drenched vocals, dragging tempos typically slowed to 100 BPM or below, crushing sub-bass, and an atmosphere steeped in horror film imagery, occult symbolism, and digital decay. Production techniques center on extreme tempo manipulation of pop and R&B samples, distorted drum machines, cold synthesizer pads, and a deliberately degraded, "VHS-damaged" sonic quality that evokes both nostalgia and unease. Key early artists such as Salem, oOoOO, and White Ring established the template on platforms like Tumblr and Last.fm, often using Unicode symbols and unpronounceable names as part of the genre's cryptic visual identity. The movement gained a significant second life in Russia and Eastern Europe during the mid-2010s, where artists like Ic3peak and Crossparty expanded the sound with trance-influenced melodies and more emotionally expressive approaches. Witch House sits at the crossroads of industrial, goth, and hip-hop culture, distinguished from its parent genres by its internet-native origins, its specific use of tempo manipulation as a primary compositional tool, and its fusion of club-music structures with dark ambient atmospheres.

| Salem | King Night | 2010 | As the definitive architects of the sound, Salem blended slowed-down southern hip-hop "chopped and screwed" textures with haunting synth-pop and shoegaze. This album established the genre's hallmarks: dragging tempos, distorted low-end, and a heavy, nihilistic atmosphere; their EPs Yes I Glow and Water are also foundational. |
| oOoOO | oOoOO | 2010 | This self-titled EP helped codify the "drag" aesthetic through the use of eerie, pitch-shifted female vocals and a cold, minimalist production style. It balances dark pop sensibilities with a funeral-procession pace, showcasing the genre's fascination with ghost-like melodies; the EP Our Loving is Hurting Us is also a key release. |
| White Ring | Black-Heart-Bled-White | 2010 | A pioneer of the aggressive, industrial side of the movement, White Ring utilized harsh noise, high-pitched vocal screams, and punishing bass lines. This release defines the genre's tendency toward abrasive, lo-fi textures and occult themes; the single IXC999 remains a genre anthem. |
| Ritualz | † | 2010 | Often styled as †‡†, Ritualz focused on a dark, occult-driven electronic sound that leaned heavily into EBM and darkwave influences. This album is a masterclass in the use of "witchy" iconography and cold, synthetic percussion; the later album Hologram further refined this dark electronic fusion. |
| Balam Acab | See Birds | 2010 | This project emphasized the aquatic, ethereal side of Witch House, using heavily processed vocal snippets and deep, resonant bass to create an underwater atmosphere. It represents the genre's capacity for emotional depth and haunting beauty; his full-length Wander / Wonder is also considered an essential, more experimental listen. |
| Holy Other | With U | 2011 | Holy Other brought a soulful, melancholic "ghost R&B" sensibility to the genre, focusing on space, reverb, and pitch-shifted vocal sighs. This EP defined the more introspective and "vibe-centric" end of the spectrum; the subsequent album Held is also a critical landmark. |
| Clams Casino | Instrumentals | 2011 | While primarily a hip-hop producer, Clams Casino's early work was pivotal in bridging the gap between "cloud rap" and the Witch House aesthetic through hazy, ethereal sampling. His use of distant, washed-out vocal loops and heavy reverb influenced the entire production style of the genre; Rainforest is another essential EP from this era. |
| Crim3s | Stay Ugly | 2013 | Formerly known as Story of Isaac, Crim3s captured the raw, punk-infused energy of the London squat rave scene with distorted synths and frantic, yearning vocals. This EP is an archetypal example of the genre's "high-energy" side, blending aggression with a sense of urban isolation; their self-titled EP is equally vital. |
| Crystal Castles | (III) | 2012 | While their earlier work was more 8-bit/electroclash, their third album fully leaned into the grim, gothic textures of Witch House with tracks like "Plague." The album's oppressive atmosphere and distorted, shrieking vocals by Alice Glass made it the most mainstream-accessible version of the genre's core sound; II also flirted with these dark elements. |
| Sidewalks and Skeletons | White Light | 2015 | This artist refined the genre for the mid-2010s, emphasizing a clean but massive production style with heavy emphasis on melancholic melodies and crushing bass. It captures the "vortex" feeling of the genre — immersive and emotionally heavy; the albums The Void and Entity are also highly influential in the modern scene. |
| Mater Suspiria Vision | Second Coming | 2010 | A project deeply rooted in surrealism and horror film aesthetics, this album utilizes avant-garde collage techniques and slowed-down pop samples. It defines the genre's obsession with occult cinema and "hauntology"; the massive Inverted Renaissance also explores these dark, psychedelic terrains. |
| Glass Teeth | Graceless | 2014 | This project highlights the "death-pop" aspect of the genre, featuring heavy, rhythmic industrial beats paired with ghostly, longing vocals. The atmosphere is consistently bleak and nocturnal, representing the genre's evolution into more structured, song-oriented territory; the EP O My Heart is another strong example. |
| GuMMy Bear | Night Drive | 2011 | This artist leaned into the synthwave-adjacent side of the genre, creating a "sludge-pop" sound that felt like a VHS tape of a 1980s horror movie melting. It exemplifies the genre's use of nostalgia and analog decay to create a sense of unease; Haterz is another notable release from this period. |
| Fraunhofer Diffraction | Fenris | 2016 | A staple of the later "wave" of Witch House, this album focuses on cinematic, dark-ambient textures combined with aggressive trap-style percussion. It represents the genre's transition into a more polished, "dark trap" aesthetic; the album Wait is also an essential listen for fans of this colder sound. |
| Modern Witch | Modern Witch | 2010 | This group embodied the lo-fi, DIY art-school origins of the genre, featuring distorted vocals and primitive drum machine patterns. Their sound is raw and unpolished, capturing the movement's early rejection of high-fidelity production; the EP Disko Etiquette is another classic of their minimal style. |
| MASCARA | MASCARA | 2011 | This self-titled release is a bridge between darkwave and Witch House, utilizing cold, repetitive synth lines and distant, ethereal vocals. It defines the genre's "gothic" DNA, focusing on a sense of romantic doom; their collaborations and EPs further explored this haunting, minimal space. |
| Blvck Ceiling | Teenage | 2014 | Blvck Ceiling is known for a massive discography of high-energy, "club-ready" Witch House that blends pop-sensibilities with dark, heavy distortion. This album is a prime example of his ability to create "stadium-sized" dark electronic music; the Alone series is also highly regarded. |
| Crossparty | Dreaming | 2014 | Representing the genre's expansion into Russia (where it gained a massive second life), Crossparty combines hyper-melancholic trance melodies with Witch House's dragging beats. This album defines the "emotional" and "epic" side of the Russian scene; their single "Kola" is a genre-defining track. |
| Summer of Haze | Pacific Oasis | 2015 | Another key figure in the Russian "voodoo" scene, this artist blends tropical and vaporwave-like textures with the heavy, dark bass of Witch House. This album shows the genre's versatility in incorporating "lighter" sounds only to drench them in darkness and reverb; Holy Jungle is another essential release. |
| Ic3peak | Ic3peak | 2015 | Before they became a political pop-horror duo, Ic3peak's early work was purely experimental Witch House characterized by high-pitched, glitchy vocals and jagged synths. This self-titled album captures the "digital-stress" side of the genre; the album Fallal further pushed these boundaries. |
| Unison | Unison | 2011 | Unison focused on a rhythmic, percussion-heavy version of the genre that felt closer to industrial and EBM but maintained the signature hazy, occult atmosphere. This album is noted for its ritualistic feel and mechanical precision; their single "Outer Limits" is a standout example. |
| Gvcci Hvcci | Underworld | 2011 | This artist combined the aesthetics of high-fashion and street culture with the dark, dragging sounds of the Witch House underground. The music is characterized by aggressive, boastful vocals over distorted, slowed-down electronic beats; the track "Vampire" remains a cult favorite. |
| AIMON | AIMON | 2011 | This duo created a very thick, wall-of-sound style of Witch House that incorporated elements of industrial noise and funeral doom. Their self-titled EP is essential for its oppressive, claustrophobic atmosphere and use of religious and occult imagery; the album Flatliner is also a key work. |
| Purity Ring | Shrines | 2012 | While they eventually moved toward "future pop," Purity Ring's debut is heavily indebted to Witch House's production techniques: pitch-shifted vocals, heavy sub-bass, and macabre lyrical themes. It represents the genre's "brightest" and most melodic iteration; the early single "Lofticries" is an archetypal track. |
| Lake R3dio | Grimm | 2011 | Lake R3dio specialized in a very "VHS-damaged" and lo-fi version of the sound, focusing on eerie melodies and muffled percussion. This album defines the "haunted basement" aesthetic that was prevalent in the genre's early Tumblr-driven days; the release Visions is also notable. |
| Mooqee | Mooqee | 2011 | This project focused on the "slowed and throwed" aspect of the genre, taking pop and R&B samples and stretching them into unrecognizable, dark soundscapes. It is a pure representation of the genre's origins as a remix culture based on tempo manipulation; various singles from this era define the "drag" sound. |
| Grimes | Halfaxa | 2010 | Before her mainstream pop success, Grimes' early work like Halfaxa was deeply connected to the ethereal, experimental Witch House scene with its layered, unintelligible vocals and dark electronic loops. Tracks like "Weregild" showcase the genre's "dream-pop-gone-wrong" aesthetic; her debut Geidi Primes also shares this DNA. |
| Bathaus | The Ritual | 2011 | This project was known for its performance-art approach and very harsh, dissonant take on the Witch House sound. It emphasizes the "noise" and "experimental" side of the classification, often utilizing ritualistic vocal chants and industrial textures; their live recordings are legendary within the underground. |
| Horse Head | Kevlar | 2012 | Not to be confused with the GothBoiClique member, this Horse Head was a significant contributor to the dark, instrumental "drag" sound. This album is characterized by its heavy use of hip-hop drum patterns slowed to a crawl and layered with menacing, minor-key synths; the EP Shed is another great example. |
| Dead Virgin | Amnesia | 2013 | This release leans into the "coldwave" side of Witch House, featuring driving, mechanical beats and detached, ghostly female vocals. It defines the genre's intersection with 1980s darkwave, updated with 21st-century digital decay and heavy bass; the EP Transience is also a standout. |
