# Third Stream

Third stream is a compositional movement that emerged in the late 1950s, coined by composer Gunther Schuller to describe music that deliberately fuses the improvisational traditions of jazz with the formal structures, orchestration techniques, and harmonic language of Western classical music. The sound is characterized by the integration of jazz soloists — typically trumpet, saxophone, or piano — within large-scale orchestral or chamber music settings featuring strings, woodwinds, and brass sections performing through-composed scores. Third stream compositions often employ classical forms such as fugues, canons, suites, and concertos while leaving space for jazz improvisation, creating a dialogue between notated precision and spontaneous expression. The genre distinguishes itself from jazz fusion through its engagement with the European classical tradition rather than rock, and from orchestral jazz through its commitment to formal compositional rigor rather than merely arranging jazz standards for large ensembles. Third stream attracted some of jazz's most intellectually ambitious artists, from the Modern Jazz Quartet's chamber elegance to Miles Davis and Gil Evans's orchestral reimaginings, and its influence persists in the work of contemporary composers who continue to bridge the two traditions.
