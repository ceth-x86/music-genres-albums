# Dixieland Jazz

Dixieland jazz is the earliest recorded form of jazz, originating in New Orleans in the early twentieth century and characterized by collective polyphonic improvisation where a frontline of cornet (or trumpet), clarinet, and trombone simultaneously improvise interlocking melodic lines over a rhythm section of piano, banjo, tuba (or string bass), and drums. The sound is defined by its "hot" rhythmic energy, syncopated two-beat or four-beat pulse, "tailgate" trombone slides, piercing clarinet runs weaving around the lead horn, and an overall feeling of joyous, communal musical conversation rooted in the marching band and brass band traditions of New Orleans. Pioneered by figures like Louis Armstrong, King Oliver, Sidney Bechet, and Jelly Roll Morton, Dixieland jazz drew from ragtime, blues, gospel, and the parade music of the Crescent City, establishing the foundational vocabulary of jazz improvisation and ensemble interaction. The genre distinguishes itself from the swing era that followed through its emphasis on collective rather than solo improvisation, its smaller ensemble format, and its rougher, more folk-rooted rhythmic feel. Dixieland experienced significant revival movements in the 1940s-1960s on both the American West Coast and in Britain, ensuring its survival as a living tradition performed worldwide from Preservation Hall to international jazz festivals.
