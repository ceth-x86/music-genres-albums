# Spiritual Jazz

Spiritual jazz is a devotional and transcendence-seeking strand of jazz that emerged in the early 1960s, characterized by its use of modal harmonies, mantra-like repetitive motifs, extended improvisations, and non-Western instrumentation to create music explicitly oriented toward spiritual experience, cosmic consciousness, and the divine. The sound is built around sustained modal vamps and drone-based harmonic foundations that allow musicians to explore meditative, trance-inducing improvisations, often incorporating instruments like harp, oud, sitar, kalimba, bells, and chimes alongside the standard jazz ensemble, creating textures that draw from African, Indian, Middle Eastern, and indigenous spiritual traditions. Pioneered by John Coltrane's devotional works, Alice Coltrane's Indo-jazz explorations, Pharoah Sanders' ecstatic overblowing techniques, and Sun Ra's Afrofuturist cosmic philosophy, spiritual jazz represented a conscious turn away from jazz-as-entertainment toward jazz-as-prayer, deeply intertwined with the Black consciousness movements, Pan-Africanism, and the search for spiritual liberation. The genre distinguishes itself from free jazz through its emphasis on devotion, beauty, and transcendence rather than pure deconstruction, and from jazz fusion through its rejection of commercial considerations in favor of sacred intent. Spiritual jazz has experienced a significant global revival in the 21st century through artists like Kamasi Washington, Shabaka Hutchings, and the South African jazz scene, ensuring the tradition's continuing relevance.
