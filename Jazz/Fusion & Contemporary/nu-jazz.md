| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| St Germain | Tourist | 2000 | This masterpiece seamlessly blends live flute and trumpet improvisations with deep house beats and bluesy samples. It remains the gold standard for high-fidelity lounge jazz, with other notable works like Boulevard and Self Titled further exploring the jazz-house hybrid. |
| Bugge Wesseltoft | New Conception of Jazz | 1996 | A cornerstone of the Norwegian scene, this album integrates programmed rhythms and synthesizers with traditional piano jazz structures. Wesseltoft's work, including Moving and Sharing, pioneered the "future jazz" aesthetic that defined the late 90s. |
| The Cinematic Orchestra | Every Day | 2002 | Known for sweeping, cinematic arrangements, this album uses turntablism and electronics to frame live jazz instrumentation and soulful vocals. Motion and Ma Fleur are also essential for understanding their fusion of atmospheric jazz and downbeat electronics. |
| Koop | Waltz for Koop | 2001 | This Swedish duo specialized in a "fake" jazz sound, meticulously sampling live recordings to create a breezy, 1960s-inspired swing infused with modern electronic precision. Their follow-up, Koop Islands, further refined this sophisticated, vocal-led approach. |
| Nils Petter Molvaer | Khmer | 1997 | This album redefined the jazz trumpet by placing it within a landscape of industrial beats, ambient textures, and rock-influenced rhythms. Molvaer's subsequent releases, such as Solid Ether and ER, continued to push the boundaries of "electro-acoustic" jazz. |
| Jazzanova | In Between | 2002 | As leaders of the Berlin scene, Jazzanova combined broken beat, Latin jazz, and soul into a complex, polished electronic tapestry. Their compilation The Remixes 1997-2000 is equally definitive in showing how they reshaped traditional jazz through a DJ's lens. |
| Matthew Herbert | Goodbye Swingtime | 2003 | Herbert recorded a full big band and then digitally manipulated the audio to create a glitchy, avant-garde version of classic swing. This record, along with his work as The Matthew Herbert Big Band, is a technical feat of jazz-concrete. |
| Nicola Conte | Other Directions | 2004 | Conte brings an Italian "Bossa Nova" and 60s film score aesthetic to modern club culture, utilizing acoustic instruments and sophisticated lounge arrangements. His albums Jet Sounds and Rituals are also pillars of the stylish, European Nu Jazz sound. |
| Quantic | The 5th Exotic | 2001 | Will Holland's debut blends jazz-funk, downtempo, and breaks with a distinctively dusty, sampled feel that bridges the gap between trip-hop and jazz. Mishaps Happening and Magnetica show his evolution into more organic, global jazz fusions. |
| Erik Truffaz | The Dawn | 1998 | Combining Miles Davis-inspired trumpet lyricism with drum and bass rhythms, this album brought an urban, edgy energy to the genre. Truffaz continued this successful exploration of jazz and hip-hop/jungle textures on Bending New Corners. |
| Jaga Jazzist | A Livingroom Hush | 2001 | This Norwegian ensemble utilizes a large-scale band to play intricate, programmed-sounding compositions that blend post-rock, electronica, and jazz. The Stix and What We Must are also vital examples of their complex, multi-layered "maximalist" jazz. |
| Bonobo | Animal Magic | 2000 | While leaning toward downtempo, this debut is an archetypal example of using jazz instrumentation and textures (vibraphones, upright bass) within an electronic framework. Dial 'M' for Monkey and Days to Come further solidified his status as a Nu Jazz pioneer. |
| Kyoto Jazz Massive | Spirit of the Sun | 2002 | This Japanese duo perfected the "crossover" sound, mixing acid jazz roots with high-energy broken beat and polished electronic production. Their various compilations and the album Message from a New Dawn represent the peak of Tokyo's Nu Jazz influence. |
| United Future Organization | United Future Organization | 1993 | One of the earliest examples of the genre, this album uses heavy sampling and club-oriented beats to revitalize bebop and cool jazz for the dancefloor. No Sound Is Too Taboo and 3rd Perspective are also essential listens for historical context. |
| Gilles Peterson | INCredible Sound of Gilles Peterson | 1999 | As a tastemaker, Peterson's curated selections on this compilation defined the "worldwide" sound that mixed jazz, soul, and electronics. His BBC Sessions and Digs America series are equally important in documenting the genre's evolution. |
| Azymuth | Antes Que Esqueca | 1996 | Though legends of 70s jazz-funk, Azymuth's 90s output embraced modern synthesizers and production, creating a "Samba Doido" (crazy samba) that fits perfectly into the Nu Jazz canon. Carnival and Lifeline are other great examples of their electronic-MPB fusion. |
| Xploding Plastix | Amateur Girlfriends Go Pro Skirt Lust | 2001 | This album features frantic, complex "drill and bass" rhythms overlaid with moody, noir-inspired jazz melodies and live instrumentation. Their later work like Treated Squad continues this high-velocity, cinematic approach to electronic jazz. |
| Laya Project | Laya Project | 2007 | A unique entry that fuses regional folk and jazz elements from post-tsunami communities with modern electronic production. It represents the "World Music" branch of Nu Jazz, where atmospheric jazz serves as a bridge between traditional and modern sounds. |
| Nostalgia 77 | Songs for My Father | 2004 | This project brings a raw, "spiritual jazz" sensibility to the modern era, using analog recording techniques and hip-hop-influenced arrangements. The Garden and Everything Under the Sun are other key works that lean into a more organic, soul-jazz sound. |
| Hidden Orchestra | Night Walks | 2010 | Combining two drummers with classical and jazz elements, this album creates a dark, brooding, and highly cinematic atmosphere. Archipelago and Dawn Chorus further explore their "imaginary soundtrack" style that blends field recordings with jazz. |
| Beady Belle | Home | 2001 | This Norwegian duo focuses on a "vocal jazz" approach, embedding soulful singing within glitchy electronic beats and jazz-pop arrangements. Cewbeagappic and Closer are equally definitive for their sleek, sophisticated production style. |
| Flanger | Templates | 1999 | A collaboration between Burnt Friedman and Atom TM, this project uses software to "re-compose" jazz, resulting in a hyper-technical, slightly surreal version of the genre. Midnight Sound and Outer Space / Inner Space are also essential for fans of technical Nu Jazz. |
| Skalpel | Skalpel | 2004 | This Polish duo famously used samples exclusively from 1960s and 70s Polish jazz records to create a unique, atmospheric, and hip-hop-inflected Nu Jazz sound. Their follow-up Konfusion continues this masterful "cut-and-paste" jazz aesthetic. |
| De-Phazz | Death by Chocolate | 2001 | A quintessential "lounge" Nu Jazz album, blending easy listening, trip-hop, and jazz with a playful, kitschy sensibility. Detunized Gravity and Godsdog are also hallmark records of the German "Phazz-a-delic" sound. |
| Portico Quartet | Knee-Deep in the North Sea | 2007 | Known for their use of the "Hang" (drum), this group created a minimalist, melodic jazz sound that felt contemporary and electronic despite being largely acoustic. Isla and their Self-Titled album show their further drift into electronic textures. |
| Flying Lotus | Cosmogramma | 2010 | While often categorized as "Brainfeeder" or experimental hip-hop, this album is a modern Nu Jazz landmark that incorporates harp, live bass, and complex jazz fusion into a digital framework. You're Dead! and Until the Quiet Comes are equally pivotal. |
| BadBadNotGood | BBNG2 | 2012 | This trio brought a punk-rock energy to jazz, interpreting hip-hop tracks with a raw, improvisational jazz style that revitalized the genre for a younger generation. III and IV show their evolution into sophisticated, original jazz composition. |
| GoGo Penguin | v2.0 | 2014 | This piano trio mimics the structures of electronic dance music (like breakbeat and techno) using purely acoustic instruments. Man Made Object and Fanfares are other essential examples of their "acoustic-electronica" jazz style. |
| Mouse on the Keys | An Anxious Object | 2009 | This Japanese group focuses on two pianos and drums to create a high-speed, math-rock-influenced version of Nu Jazz. Their EP Sezession is also vital for its aggressive yet melodic approach to the genre's boundaries. |
| Mammal Hands | Animalia | 2014 | Drawing from spiritual jazz and folk, this trio uses repetitive, looping structures that feel electronic in nature but are performed with saxophone, piano, and drums. Floa and Shadow Work further define their hypnotic, atmospheric sound. |
