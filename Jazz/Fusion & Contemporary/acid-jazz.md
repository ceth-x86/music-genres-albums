# Acid Jazz

Acid jazz is a genre that emerged in the late 1980s London club scene, blending jazz improvisation with funk grooves, soul vocals, and hip-hop-influenced breakbeats. Coined by DJ Gilles Peterson and Eddie Piller, the term initially described a "rare groove" movement that recontextualized classic soul-jazz records for dancefloor audiences before evolving into a full-band live aesthetic. The sound is characterized by Hammond B3 organs, tight horn sections, syncopated funk basslines, and soulful vocal harmonies layered over organic, live-instrumented rhythms. Acid jazz distinguishes itself from traditional jazz through its emphasis on groove and danceability over complex improvisation, and from electronic dance music through its commitment to live instrumentation and jazz harmony. The genre spread internationally through scenes in Japan, France, and the United States, each adding regional flavors from boogaloo and Latin jazz to Shibuya-kei aesthetics, while maintaining the core fusion of jazz sophistication with club-ready energy.
