# Ethio / World Jazz

Ethio-Jazz is a genre that emerged in the 1960s and 1970s from Addis Ababa, Ethiopia, fusing traditional Ethiopian pentatonic scales and Amharic vocal traditions with Western jazz instrumentation, Latin percussion, and funk rhythms to create a hypnotic, melancholic, and deeply atmospheric sound. The music is characterized by the prominent use of vibraphone, organ, and analog synthesizers playing within the distinctive Ethiopian five-note modal system, accompanied by tight brass sections, walking basslines, and Afro-Latin rhythmic patterns that give the music its characteristic swaying groove. Pioneered by Mulatu Astatke — widely regarded as the father of Ethio-Jazz — and flourishing during Ethiopia's musical golden age alongside vocalists like Mahmoud Ahmed and Tlahoun Gèssèssè, the genre was nurtured by state-sponsored big bands and the vibrant Addis Ababa nightclub scene before being disrupted by the Derg military regime. Ethio-Jazz distinguishes itself from American jazz through its use of Ethiopian modal scales that produce an eerie, otherworldly quality foreign to Western harmony, and from traditional Ethiopian music through its incorporation of jazz improvisation, funk rhythms, and electric instrumentation. The genre experienced a global revival through the Éthiopiques compilation series and has inspired contemporary ensembles worldwide who continue to explore the intersection of Ethiopian modes and jazz improvisation.
