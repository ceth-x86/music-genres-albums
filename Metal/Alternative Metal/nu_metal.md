# Nu Metal

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Korn | Korn | 1994 | Often cited as the genre's blueprint, it introduced the signature down-tuned seven-string guitar riffs and raw, vulnerable vocal delivery that became industry standards. This debut established the dark, rhythmic groove and psychological lyrical themes central to the movement, seen also in their seminal albums Life is Peachy and Follow the Leader. |
| Deftones | Adrenaline | 1995 | This record pioneered the contrast between aggressive, percussive rhythms and atmospheric, melodic textures that would define the genre's more artistic side. Chino Moreno's dynamic vocal shifts from whispers to screams set a high bar for emotional intensity, later refined on their masterpiece Around the Fur. |
| Limp Bizkit | Significant Other | 1999 | This album perfected the fusion of hip-hop cadence with heavy rock riffs, featuring high-energy turntablism and massive, stadium-sized hooks. It remains the commercial peak of the "rap-metal" crossover, alongside their debut Three Dollar Bill, Yall$ and the follow-up Chocolate Starfish and the Hot Dog Flavored Water. |
| Slipknot | Slipknot | 1999 | Bringing an unprecedented level of aggression and chaos, this album utilized a nine-member lineup to create a wall of percussion and dissonant electronics. Its blend of death metal intensity with nu metal's rhythmic structure redefined the genre's extremity, a sound further explored on Iowa. |
| Linkin Park | Hybrid Theory | 2000 | A masterclass in production, this album seamlessly integrated electronic elements, precise scratching, and the interplay between rapping and soaring melodic choruses. It is the definitive "polished" nu metal record, with Meteora serving as its essential companion piece. |
| System of a Down | System of a Down | 1998 | This debut introduced a unique, eccentric energy characterized by rapid-fire vocal shifts, Middle Eastern melodic influences, and jagged, staccato riffing. Their sound is both politically charged and musically unpredictable, reaching further heights on the iconic Toxicity. |
| Coal Chamber | Coal Chamber | 1997 | Highlighting the "spooky" aesthetic of the genre, this album features simplistic, driving grooves and a focus on gothic atmosphere and eccentric vocal delivery. It helped popularize the "bounce" riff style, also present on their follow-up Chamber Music. |
| Sevendust | Sevendust | 1997 | This record stands out for its soulful, powerful lead vocals paired with intricate, syncopated heavy drumming and chugging guitar work. They brought a level of technicality and melodic depth to the genre, also heard on Home and Animosity. |
| Staind | Dysfunction | 1999 | This album leaned into the brooding, melancholic side of the genre, utilizing heavy, sludgy riffs to underscore themes of angst and depression. It bridged the gap between raw aggression and the post-grunge melodicism found on their massive hit Break Cycle. |
| Static-X | Wisconsin Deathtrip | 1999 | Coining the term "evil disco," this album combined industrial loops and techno-influenced synths with repetitive, high-speed power riffs. Wayne Static's rhythmic vocal delivery made it one of the most unique dance-metal hybrids of the era, a style continued on Machine. |
| Incubus | S.C.I.E.N.C.E. | 1997 | Before shifting to alternative rock, this album was a cornerstone of the genre's funk-infused, experimental side, featuring heavy slap-bass and prominent DJ scratching. It showcased a high level of musicianship and genre-blending, also found on their earlier EP Enjoy Incubus. |
| P.O.D. | The Fundamental Elements of Southtown | 1999 | Integrating elements of reggae and punk into the nu metal framework, this album brought a spiritual and positive perspective to a largely angst-driven scene. Their rhythmic, groove-heavy sound peaked commercially with the multi-platinum Satellite. |
| Papa Roach | Infest | 2000 | This record focused on the high-energy intersection of rap-inspired verses and anthemic punk-rock choruses. It captured the suburban angst of the time with sharp, relatable lyrics and driving rhythms, also evident on Lovehatetragedy. |
| Mudvayne | L.D. 50 | 2000 | Known for its complexity, this album introduced "math-metal" elements to the genre, featuring highly technical bass playing and shifting time signatures. It proved the genre could be both aggressive and intellectually demanding, themes revisited on The End of All Things to Come. |
| Disturbed | The Sickness | 2000 | Defined by David Draiman's distinctive, staccato vocal delivery and animalistic sound effects, this album focused on mechanical, rhythmic riffs and tight production. It became one of the most successful examples of the genre's second wave, followed by Believe. |
| Kittie | Spit | 1999 | This debut brought a much-needed female perspective to the genre, delivering raw, gritty riffs and a mix of melodic singing and death-metal screams. It proved the genre's "mall-metal" sound could be just as fierce and confrontational as its peers, also seen on Oracle. |
| Soulfly | Soulfly | 1998 | Following Max Cavalera's departure from Sepultura, this project integrated tribal percussion and world music elements into a heavy, grooving nu metal foundation. It emphasized a spiritual, rhythmic "vibe" that was deeply influential, also explored on Primitive. |
| Spineshank | The Height of Callousness | 2000 | This album is a prime example of the industrial-tinged nu metal sound, featuring aggressive electronic layering and sleek, modern production. The songs are built on driving, mechanical rhythms and high-octane energy, similar to their work on Self-Destructive Pattern. |
| Drowning Pool | Sinner | 2001 | Characterized by thick, southern-influenced guitar tones and powerful, gravelly vocals, this album delivered straight-to-the-point nu metal anthems. It relied on heavy, mid-tempo grooves and catchy, aggressive hooks, most famous on the track "Bodies." |
| Hed PE | Broke | 2000 | One of the most authentic fusions of G-funk and heavy rock, this album featured aggressive rapping, DJ work, and heavy, punk-influenced riffs. They embodied the "G-Punk" lifestyle, a sound they established on their self-titled debut. |
| Adema | Adema | 2001 | Featuring clean, melodic production and a focus on personal, introspective lyrics, this album represented the more radio-friendly side of the genre. It utilized atmospheric synths and steady, mid-tempo grooves, also found on Unstable. |
| Taproot | Gift | 2000 | This record showcased a more progressive approach to the genre, with intricate guitar melodies and a balance of screaming and harmonious vocals. It favored a moody, layered atmosphere over simple aggression, later leaning into melody on Welcome. |
| Nonpoint | Statement | 2000 | This album emphasized a high-energy, percussive vocal flow and groovy, rhythmic guitar work inspired by Latin music influences. Their sound is defined by its kinetic energy and bounce, also prominent on Development. |
| Alien Ant Farm | ANThology | 2001 | While known for their humor and covers, this album features sophisticated, jazz-influenced guitar work and highly melodic, rhythmic hooks. They brought a quirky, technically proficient edge to the genre, further explored on truANT. |
| Powerman 5000 | Tonight the Stars Revolt! | 1999 | This concept album combined science-fiction themes with industrial rhythms and "action-rock" riffs. It utilized campy aesthetics and electronic pulses to create a cinematic, high-energy nu metal sound, also seen on Transform. |
| Orgy | Candyass | 1998 | Defined by its "death-pop" aesthetic, this album heavily utilized synthesizers and electronic processing to create a futuristic, glam-influenced take on nu metal. It leaned more into New Wave influences than its peers, a style continued on Vapor Transmission. |
| Machine Head | The Burning Red | 1999 | A controversial but essential pivot for the band, this album adopted nu metal's rhythmic delivery and stripped-back guitar solos in favor of groove and atmosphere. It showed the genre's influence on established thrash bands, also evident on Supercharger. |
| Ill Niño | Revolution Revolución | 2001 | This debut successfully fused heavy nu metal riffs with prominent Latin percussion, acoustic guitars, and bilingual lyrics. It added a distinct cultural layer and rhythmic complexity to the genre's standard template, also heard on Confession. |
| Primer 55 | Introduction to Mayhem | 2000 | Heavily leaning into the rap-metal side of the genre, this album features high-speed rhyming and bouncy, funk-metal riffs. It captured the chaotic, party-heavy energy of the late 90s scene, also present on (The) New Release. |
| Cold | 13 Ways to Bleed on Stage | 2000 | Focusing on a dark, melancholic atmosphere, this album utilized moody electronics and emotional, strained vocals to create a sense of despair. It is a key example of the genre's more somber and "depressive" side, later refined on Year of the Spider. |
| Godsmack | Godsmack | 1998 | This album featured a thick, Alice in Chains-inspired vocal style over driving, percussive nu metal riffs. It relied on tribal drum patterns and a gritty, blue-collar aesthetic that became a staple of early 2000s hard rock. |
| Mushroomhead | Superheaviest | 1999 | A cult favorite that leaned heavily into the "jazz-core" side of nu metal, featuring intricate drumming and a mix of melodic and harsh vocals. It focused on a dark, suburban atmosphere and complex rhythmic interplay. |
| Snot | Get Some | 1997 | Mixing hardcore punk, funk, and metal, this album was a raw and energetic take on the emerging nu metal sound. Its aggressive, chaotic spirit was highly influential on the California scene before the band's tragic end. |
| American Head Charge | The War of Art | 2001 | A chaotic blend of industrial noise and aggressive nu metal, this album is known for its dense layering and intense, disturbing atmosphere. It pushed the genre into darker, more experimental territory, similar to The Feeding. |
| Dope | Felons and Revolutionaries | 1999 | This record combined the sleaze of industrial rock with the rhythmic grooves of nu metal and a rebellious punk attitude. It focused on aggressive, nihilistic themes and simple, heavy riffs, also featured on Life. |
| Filter | Title of Record | 1999 | While bordering on industrial, this album's use of distorted grooves and rhythmic vocal delivery fits squarely into the nu metal era's sonic shift. It balanced raw, screaming aggression with melodic, atmospheric moments, much like their debut Short Bus. |
| Crazy Town | The Gift of Game | 1999 | Representing the hip-hop-heavy end of the spectrum, this album blended laid-back grooves with heavy guitar accents and dual-vocalist interplay. It captured the Los Angeles "party" vibe of the genre, featuring the massive hit "Butterfly." |
| Flaw | Through the Eyes | 2001 | This album stands out for its melodic sensibility and deeply personal, emotional lyrics backed by heavy, down-tuned riffs. It emphasized the "beauty and the beast" dynamic of clean singing and aggressive outbursts, also seen on Endangered Species. |
| Trust Company | The Lonely Position of Neutral | 2002 | A late-era classic, this record focused on soaring melodies and high-gain, wall-of-sound guitar textures. It moved away from rapping entirely, favoring a more alt-metal harmonic approach that still retained the genre's rhythmic weight. |
| 40 Below Summer | Invitation to the Dance | 2001 | Known for their high-energy transitions between jazz-like melodies and explosive, screaming choruses, this band represented the underground intensity of the genre. They favored complex structures and raw emotion, also heard on The Mourning After. |
| Nothingface | Violence | 2000 | This album is often praised for its exceptional songwriting, blending jagged, discordant riffs with hauntingly melodic choruses. It offered a more sophisticated and aggressive alternative to the mainstream sound, similar to Skeletons. |
| The Union Underground | An Education in Rebellion | 2000 | Combining industrial-tinged guitars with a classic rock swagger and nu metal's rhythmic focus, this album had a high-gloss, aggressive sound. It utilized samples and loops to enhance its heavy, mid-tempo grooves. |
| One Minute Silence | Buy Now... Saved Later | 2000 | Hailing from the UK, this band brought a strong political message and a more funk-metal-influenced rhythmic section to the genre. Their sound was characterized by rapid-fire vocals and heavy, "bouncing" bass lines. |
| Factory 81 | Mankind | 1999 | A cult favorite that leaned heavily into the "jazz-core" side of nu metal, featuring intricate drumming and a mix of melodic and harsh vocals. It focused on a dark, suburban atmosphere and complex rhythmic interplay. |
| Dry Kill Logic | The Darker Side of Nonsense | 2001 | This album delivered a more aggressive, hardcore-influenced version of nu metal, featuring relentless double-bass drumming and percussive riffs. It leaned toward the "core" side of the genre, a style they continued on The Dead and Dreaming. |
| Chimaira | Pass Out of Existence | 2001 | Before becoming a pure groove metal band, their debut was a masterpiece of electronic-infused nu metal. It featured heavy programming, samples, and the genre's signature chugging riffs, a far cry from the thrash sound of The Impossibility of Reason. |
| Reveille | Laced | 1999 | This record is a prime example of the rap-heavy nu metal style, featuring aggressive, skilled flow and high-energy funk riffs. It captured the raw, unpolished spirit of the late 90s underground, also present on Bleed the Sky. |
| Methods of Mayhem | Methods of Mayhem | 1999 | Tommy Lee's project was an ambitious, chaotic blend of nu metal, hip-hop, and industrial electronic music. It featured numerous guest appearances and emphasized the genre's "anything goes" production style. |
| Project 86 | Drawing Black Lines | 2000 | Combining post-hardcore intensity with nu metal's rhythmic weight, this album featured dark, poetic lyrics and a visceral, driving sound. It stood out for its sincerity and crushing bass-driven grooves. |
| Earshot | Letting Go | 2002 | Often compared to Tool, this band brought a more atmospheric and grunge-influenced sound to the nu metal framework. It focused on mid-tempo, moody arrangements and soaring, melodic vocals, also heard on Two. |
