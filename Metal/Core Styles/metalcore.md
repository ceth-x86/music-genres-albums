# Metalcore

Metalcore originated in the early 1990s from the collision of hardcore punk's intensity and attitude with the riffing power and technicality of heavy metal, particularly thrash and melodic death metal. The genre is defined by its use of heavy palm-muted guitar riffs, rhythmic breakdowns designed for aggressive live participation, and a vocal approach that alternates between harsh screaming and melodic clean singing. Early metalcore drew heavily from New York and Cleveland hardcore scenes, while the second wave in the early 2000s incorporated Gothenburg-style twin-guitar harmonies and polished production. Instrumentation typically features downtuned guitars, prominent double-bass drumming, and a dynamic song structure that shifts between furious blast sections and crushing mid-tempo breakdowns. The genre became one of heavy music's most commercially successful forms in the mid-2000s, spawning numerous regional scenes across the United States, the United Kingdom, and Australia. Metalcore's cultural influence extends into fashion, online community building, and the broader "scene" subculture that defined a generation of heavy music fans.

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Earth Crisis | Destroy the Machines | 1995 | This album is a cornerstone of "metallic hardcore," blending chugging thrash riffs with hardcore's ethical intensity and breakdown-heavy structures. It defined the vegan straight-edge sound and paved the way for the genre's political and sonic aggression; Gomorrah's Season Ends is also essential. |
| Converge | Jane Doe | 2001 | A masterpiece of chaotic metalcore, it features dissonant, technical guitar work and raw, emotional vocals that set a new standard for intensity. Its influence on the "mathcore" leaning of the genre is unparalleled, with You Fail Me being another landmark release. |
| Killswitch Engage | Alive or Just Breathing | 2002 | This record perfected the "Melodic Metalcore" blueprint by seamlessly fusing Swedish Gothenburg-style death metal riffs with soaring clean choruses and hardcore grit. It remains the gold standard for the genre's commercial peak; The End of Heartache is equally definitive. |
| Hatebreed | Satisfaction Is the Death of Desire | 1997 | Bridging the gap between NYHC and metal, this album focuses on massive, groove-oriented breakdowns and shout-along anthems. It is the ultimate representation of the "tough guy" hardcore-leaning side of metalcore, alongside their later work Perseverance. |
| The Dillinger Escape Plan | Calculating Infinity | 1999 | This album introduced a level of technicality and rhythmic complexity that birthed the mathcore sub-strain of metalcore. Its erratic time signatures and jazz-fusion-on-acid energy are also found on their follow-up Miss Machine. |
| Shai Hulud | Hearts Once Nourished with Hope and Compassion | 1997 | A pioneer of the more progressive and "emotive" side of metalcore, using complex, layered guitar melodies rather than just relying on chugs. They influenced a generation of melodic hardcore and metalcore bands, with That Within Blood Ill-Tempered also being vital. |
| All That Remains | The Fall of Ideals | 2006 | Featuring hyper-melodic guitar leads and technical drumming, this album represents the mid-2000s peak of the Massachusetts metalcore scene. It balances aggression and accessibility perfectly, much like their previous effort This Darkened Heart. |
| As I Lay Dying | Frail Words Collapse | 2003 | This album solidified the "At the Gates" style riffing within the metalcore framework, characterized by dual-guitar harmonies and punishing breakdowns. It established them as leaders of the genre, a status cemented by Shadows Are Security. |
| Poison the Well | The Opposite of December... A Season of Separation | 1999 | This record is credited with popularizing the "melodic-to-crushing" dynamic, blending vulnerable, melodic passages with visceral metalcore explosions. It is a foundational text for the "scenecore" that followed, with You Come Before You showing their evolution. |
| Cave In | Until Your Heart Stops | 1998 | A landmark of technical metalcore, it combines blistering speed, metallic precision, and spacey, experimental flourishes. It captured the transition from hardcore to something far more intricate, a path they continued on Jupiter. |
| Unearth | The Oncoming Storm | 2004 | Known for their heavy use of 7-string guitars, Unearth focused on the "metal" side of the genre with thrashy riffs and intricate "iron maiden" style leads. This album is a masterclass in the genre's riff-driven power, followed closely by III: In the Eyes of Fire. |
| Botch | We Are the Romans | 1999 | This album is a high-water mark for complex, angular metalcore, utilizing odd time signatures and a cold, mechanical atmosphere. Its influence on modern technical metal is massive, with American Nervoso serving as its raw predecessor. |
| Integrity | Those Who Fear Tomorrow | 1991 | One of the earliest examples of the genre, blending Clevo-hardcore with dark, Slayer-esque metallic aesthetics and apocalyptic themes. It laid the groundwork for the darker "holy terror" style of metalcore, alongside Humanity Is the Devil. |
| Shadows Fall | The War Within | 2004 | Bringing a heavy thrash metal influence to the genre, this album features extensive soloing and high-velocity riffing characteristic of the NWOAHM. It showed that metalcore could be virtuosic, a trait also present on The Art of Balance. |
| Avenged Sevenfold | Waking the Fallen | 2003 | Before shifting to hard rock, this band created a defining melodic metalcore record that mixed gothic atmosphere with dueling lead guitars and punk energy. It remains a pivotal crossover moment for the genre, following their debut Sounding the Seventh Trumpet. |
| Architects | Hollow Crown | 2009 | This album represents the British wave of metalcore, utilizing technical riffs, ambient textures, and a balance of screams and melodic hooks. It remains a high point for the genre's technical evolution, with Lost Forever // Lost Together being a later masterpiece. |
| August Burns Red | Messengers | 2007 | Distinguished by its intricate drum patterns, odd time signatures, and highly technical guitar leads, this album pushed the boundaries of metalcore's complexity. It defined the "Christian metalcore" peak, alongside their follow-up Constellations. |
| Every Time I Die | Hot Damn! | 2003 | Blending metalcore with Southern rock "swagger" and witty, poetic lyrics, this album brought a unique, high-energy groove to the scene. They maintained this chaotic excellence throughout their career, notably on Low Teens. |
| Parkway Drive | Horizons | 2007 | Representing the Australian scene, this album features massive, stadium-sized breakdowns and soaring melodic leads that define the modern metalcore sound. It is a textbook example of high-production, heavy-hitting metalcore, as is Killing with a Smile. |
| The Devil Wears Prada | With Roots Above and Branches Below | 2009 | This album captured the "scenecore" era but with a focus on dark, atmospheric synth textures and a heavier, more mature sound. It helped evolve the "keyboard-heavy" metalcore style, leading into the experimental Dead Throne. |
| Bring Me The Horizon | Suicide Season | 2008 | Moving away from deathcore, this album pioneered the modern "British metalcore" sound, incorporating electronic elements and more rhythmic, catchy structures. It was a polarizing but essential shift, followed by the even more ambitious There Is a Hell... |
| Trivium | Ascendancy | 2005 | A perfect marriage of classic thrash metal (Metallica style) and contemporary metalcore, featuring dual-guitar solos and anthemic choruses. It is one of the most successful albums of the 2000s metalcore boom, alongside Shogun. |
| Norma Jean | Bless the Martyr and Kiss the Child | 2002 | A cornerstone of the "chaotic" metalcore scene, this album features massive, dissonant riffs and an unhinged vocal performance. It is a primal, noisy experience influenced by Botch, with Redeemer being another key work. |
| Zao | Where Blood and Fire Bring Rest | 1998 | This album introduced a darker, more harrowing atmosphere to the genre, moving away from standard hardcore into something more "blackened" and visceral. It is a landmark of mid-to-late 90s metalcore, as is Liberate Te Ex Inferis. |
| Darkest Hour | Undoing Ruin | 2005 | Heavily indebted to the Swedish melodic death metal sound, this album features high-speed riffing and virtuosic leads within a metalcore framework. It is a fast, technical, and highly melodic record, preceded by the raw Hidden Hands of a Sadist Nation. |
| God Forbid | Gone Forever | 2004 | Part of the NWOAHM wave, they combined thrashy New Jersey aggression with a sense of melody and technicality. This album features sophisticated songwriting and powerful grooves, much like their concept album IV: Constitution of Treason. |
| Bleeding Through | This Is Love, This Is Murderous | 2003 | This band integrated symphonic black metal keyboards and "blackened" aesthetics into a metalcore foundation. This album defined their "gothic metalcore" sound, a style they continued to refine on The Truth. |
| Between the Buried and Me | The Silent Circus | 2003 | Before they went full progressive metal, this album was a landmark of technical metalcore that blended grind, death metal, and melodic passages. It showed the genre's potential for extreme genre-mashing, leading into the legendary Colors. |
| Misery Signals | Of Malice and the Magnum Heart | 2004 | A peak for "melodic/atmospheric" metalcore, featuring complex layering, emotive guitar work, and heavy, polyrhythmic breakdowns. It is a highly influential "thinking man's" metalcore record, with Controller also being a fan favorite. |
| Eighteen Visions | Until the Ink Runs Out | 2000 | Before shifting to a more hard rock sound, they pioneered a heavy, breakdown-obsessed style with flamboyant "fashioncore" aesthetics. This album is incredibly heavy and rhythmically focused, followed by the more melodic Vanity. |
| Vision of Disorder | Vision of Disorder | 1996 | A vital bridge between the Long Island hardcore scene and aggressive metal, featuring eclectic vocal styles and jagged, rhythmic riffs. It brought a unique "alt-metal" flavor to metalcore, also heard on Imprint. |
| Coalesce | 0:12 Revolution in Just Listening | 1999 | Defined by its "sludgy," math-influenced metalcore, featuring discordant guitars and complex, crushing rhythms. It is a dense and challenging listen that influenced the technical side of the genre, much like Give Them Rope. |
| Deadguy | Fixation on a Co-Worker | 1995 | A seminal album for the "mathy" and "noisy" side of metalcore, blending metallic crunch with a cynical, dissonant punk attitude. It remains one of the most influential records of the 90s underground, preceding bands like Dillinger and Botch. |
| Morning Again | As Tradition Dies Slowly | 1998 | A key example of the Florida "melodic metalcore" sound, emphasizing clean, dual-guitar harmonies and socio-political themes. Their work was instrumental in moving the genre toward a more melodic direction, alongside their EPs collected on Hand of Hope. |
| Agnostic Front | Cause for Alarm | 1986 | While primarily a hardcore band, this specific album is one of the earliest "crossover" records that added heavy metal riffing to the hardcore template. It is a vital ancestor of the metalcore genre, alongside Victim in Pain. |
| Overcast | Expectational Dilution | 1994 | Featuring future members of Killswitch Engage and Shadows Fall, this band was one of the first to truly blend death metal vocals and riffs with hardcore structures. This record laid the foundation for the Massachusetts scene, followed by Fight Ambition to Survive. |
| Starkweather | Crossbearer | 1992 | An experimental and progressive pioneer of the genre, blending complex, dissonant metal with atmospheric and non-linear songwriting. They are often cited as a "band's band" who influenced the tech-metalcore movement, with Into the Ravine also being essential. |
| Snapcase | Progression Through Unlearning | 1997 | Known for their "snappy," high-pitched snare sound and rhythmic, staccato riffing, they brought a more technical and disciplined approach to metalcore. It is a unique, high-energy take on the genre, followed by the more melodic Designs for Automotion. |
| Candiria | Process of Self-Transformation | 1999 | Fusing metalcore with jazz, hip-hop, and ambient textures, this album pushed the genre's boundaries into "urban" experimentalism. It is a highly technical and rhythmically diverse record, as is their follow-up 300 Percent Density. |
| Protest the Hero | Kezia | 2005 | This album brought a high level of "shred" and progressive metal theatricality to the metalcore framework. Its constant technical guitar leads and complex storytelling set it apart, a style refined on Fortress. |
| Hopesfall | The Satellite Years | 2002 | One of the first bands to successfully blend spacey, "post-rock" atmospheres with metalcore aggression. This album is highly melodic and expansive, influencing the "ambient metalcore" trend, followed by A Types. |
| The Ghost Inside | Returners | 2010 | A definitive example of "melodic hardcore-infused metalcore," focusing on uplifting messages, massive riffs, and heavy, rhythmic breakdowns. It captures the modern "spirit" of the genre, much like Get What You Give. |
| Northlane | Singularity | 2013 | This album helped define the "progressive/djent" leaning of modern metalcore, utilizing low-tuned, rhythmic guitars and atmospheric electronic textures. It is a landmark of the modern Australian scene, alongside their debut Discoveries. |
| Motionless in White | Creatures | 2010 | Combining metalcore with gothic aesthetics, industrial synths, and horror-themed lyrics, this album carved out a unique "horror-metalcore" niche. It represents the genre's theatrical evolution, followed by the heavier Infamous. |
| While She Sleeps | This Is the Six | 2012 | This album brought a gritty, energetic "punk rock" urgency back to melodic metalcore, featuring gang vocals and anthemic, riff-heavy structures. It is a standout of the modern UK scene, alongside Brainwashed. |
| Of Mice & Men | The Flood | 2011 | A core example of the early 2010s "rise records" sound, blending heavy nu-metal grooves with soaring pop-inflected choruses. It was massive in the "alternative" metalcore scene, following their self-titled debut. |
| Shattered Realm | Broken Need No Resurrection | 2002 | A prime example of "beatdown metalcore," where the focus is almost entirely on slow, punishing breakdowns and crushing metallic weight. It represents the extreme hardcore end of the spectrum, much like From the Dead End Blocks where Life Means Nothing. |
| Cro-Mags | Best Wishes | 1989 | Following their hardcore debut, this album fully embraced thrash metal, serving as a blueprint for the "crossover" that would eventually be called metalcore. Its influence on the groove and riffing of the genre is immense, with The Age of Quarrel being their other pillar. |
| Terror | One with the Underdogs | 2004 | While leaning heavily toward hardcore, this band's metallic production and riffing style make them a staple of the "hardcore-core" metalcore sound. They define consistency in the genre, with Lowest of the Low being their essential debut. |
| Heaven Shall Burn | Antigone | 2004 | A powerhouse of European "melodic death metalcore," featuring high-speed aggression, massive production, and political lyrics. This album is a titan of the international scene, with Deaf to Our Prayers also being a landmark of heaviness. |
