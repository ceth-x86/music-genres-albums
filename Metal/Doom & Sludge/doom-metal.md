# Doom Metal

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Black Sabbath | Master of Reality | 1971 | The blueprint for all doom, featuring Tony Iommi's downtuned, heavy-as-lead guitar riffs and a crushing rhythmic density. It established the genre's obsession with darkness, slow tempos, and "The Tritone." Other essential titles include Black Sabbath and Vol. 4. |
| Candlemass | Epicus Doomicus Metallicus | 1986 | This album defined "Epic Doom" by pairing slow, crushing riffs with grand, operatic vocals and a sense of tragic scale. It moved the genre away from bluesy roots toward a more theatrical and mournful sound. Nightfall is also a cornerstone of their discography. |
| Saint Vitus | Born Too Late | 1986 | Representing the raw, punk-influenced side of traditional doom, this record features Scott "Wino" Weinrich's gritty vocals and fuzzy, feedback-laden guitar work. It captures the feeling of alienation and misery better than almost any other record. Die Healing is another essential work. |
| Trouble | Psalm 9 | 1984 | A landmark of American traditional doom that combined massive, Sabbath-esque riffs with a faster, NWOBHM-influenced twin-guitar attack. Their "White Metal" lyrical approach offered a unique spiritual contrast to the genre's typical occultism. The Skull is also a highly influential release. |
| Pentagram | Relentless | 1985 | Bobby Liebling's decades-long pursuit of "heavy" culminated in this collection of sinister, groovy tracks that bridge 70s hard rock and 80s doom. The guitar tone is remarkably thick and menacing for its era. Day of Reckoning remains a fan favorite. |
| The Obsessed | Lunar Womb | 1991 | A masterclass in "Doom Rock," blending biker-culture grit with heavy, mid-tempo grooves and street-level lyrics. Wino's guitar work here is both technical and incredibly soulful. Their self-titled debut is also a mandatory listen. |
| Witchfinder General | Death Penalty | 1982 | One of the earliest examples of the Second Wave of Doom, strictly following the Sabbath template while adding a distinctly British, occult-obsessed atmosphere. The production is raw, emphasizing the clattering drums and fuzzy riffs. Friends of Hell is their other primary contribution. |
| Reverend Bizarre | In the Rectory of the Bizarre Reverend | 2002 | This modern classic features extremely long, slow compositions that pay homage to the genre's roots while pushing the limits of endurance and repetition. It helped revitalize traditional doom for a new millennium. II: Crush the Insects is also highly regarded. |
| Solitude Aeturnus | Beyond the Crimson Horizon | 1992 | A pinnacle of Epic Doom, featuring Robert Lowe's soaring, high-register vocals and intricate, melodic guitar leads. They added a layer of technical sophistication to the crushing weight of the genre. Into the Depths of Sorrow is another landmark. |
| Count Raven | High on Infinity | 1993 | This Swedish band captured the "Sabbathian" sound perfectly, using analog production and nasal, Ozzy-like vocals to create a nostalgic yet heavy atmosphere. Their lyrics often focused on social and political decay. Destruction of the Void is also essential. |
| Pagan Altar | Volume 1 | 1982 | Blending New Wave of British Heavy Metal with folk elements and slow, occult doom, this album offers a unique, mystical atmosphere. The vocals are hauntingly thin and eerie, matching the ancient-feeling riffs. Lords of Hypocrisy is another masterpiece. |
| Internal Void | Standing on the Edge of Eternity | 1993 | A staple of the Maryland doom scene, this album emphasizes a bluesy, heavy swing and thick, distorted bass lines. It represents the "pure" traditional doom sound without any crossover into sludge or stoner metal. Matricide is also a key record. |
| The Gates of Slumber | Conqueror | 2008 | Drawing heavily from Robert E. Howard-style fantasy, this album features barbaric, mid-tempo riffs and a rugged, unpolished production. It is a definitive example of "Epic Traditional Doom" from the 21st century. Hymns of Blood and Thunder is equally vital. |
| Pallbearer | Sorrow and Extinction | 2012 | This album brought melodic sensibility and prog-influenced structures back to doom, using multi-layered vocal harmonies and sprawling, emotive song lengths. It is widely credited with the genre's mainstream critical resurgence. Foundations of Burden is another highlight. |
| Iron Man | The Passage | 1994 | Led by the late Al Morris III, this band specialized in incredibly heavy, blues-based doom that channeled the spirit of early Black Sabbath with modern weight. The guitar solos are particularly expressive and soulful. Black Spirits is another essential title. |
| Spirit Caravan | Jug Fulla Sun | 1999 | Another Wino project, this band leaned into the "Stoner-Doom" border but remained firmly rooted in the spiritual, groovy tradition of doom metal. The music is warm, psychedelic, and driven by massive power chords. Elusive Truth is their other major work. |
| Warning | Watching from a Distance | 2006 | This album redefined "Emotive Doom," stripping away the fantasy elements to focus on raw, soul-crushing human sadness through slow, repetitive riffs and soaring vocals. It is arguably the most heartbreaking album in the genre. The Strength to Dream is their earlier, rawer effort. |
| While Heaven Wept | Vast Oceans Lachrymose | 2009 | Fusing Epic Doom with Power Metal and Progressive Rock, this album is grand, symphonic, and intensely melodic. It demonstrates the genre's capacity for beauty and technical complexity. Of Empires Forlorn is also highly recommended. |
| Confessor | Condemned | 1991 | A unique outlier in the genre, Confessor utilized technical, odd-time signatures and "mathy" drumming while maintaining the slow, crushing weight of doom. The high-pitched, wailing vocals add to the disorienting, claustrophobic feel. Unraveled is their later comeback effort. |
| Penance | The Indigo Shore | 1999 | Emerging from the ashes of Dream Death, Penance delivered a more focused, traditional doom sound with a heavy emphasis on psychedelic atmosphere and thick guitar tones. Parallel Corners is another essential record from their catalog. |
| Mirror of Deception | Foregone | 2004 | A key player in the German doom scene, this band focuses on "Unorthodox Doom" that prioritizes atmosphere and unusual chord progressions over simple Sabbath worship. Shards is also an important release for the band. |
| Solstice | New Dark Age | 1998 | This UK band perfected "Epic English Doom," mixing heavy metal thunder with a sense of ancient history and folklore. The production is vast, and the riffs are incredibly powerful and memorable. Lamentations is their debut classic. |
| Paul Chain | In the Darkness | 1986 | An experimental take on doom from the former Death SS guitarist, featuring "phonetic" vocals and a dark, ritualistic atmosphere. It is one of the most avant-garde and eerie releases in the traditional doom sphere. Life and Death is another cult favorite. |
| Scald | Will of Gods Is a Great Design | 1996 | A cult classic of Russian Epic Doom, featuring the powerful, folk-influenced vocals of the late Agyl and Viking-themed lyrics. It combines the weight of Candlemass with a unique Slavic melancholy. |
| Crypt Sermon | The Ruins of Fading Light | 2019 | A modern triumph of Epic Doom that balances 80s theatricality with modern production and "crushing" weight. The songwriting is dynamic, featuring soaring choruses and intricate guitar work. Out of the Garden is their excellent debut. |
| Orodruin | Epicurean Mass | 2003 | Hailing from Rochester, NY, this band stays strictly within the traditional doom bounds, focusing on thick, mid-tempo riffs and a gloomy, 70s-style production. Ruins of Eternity is their impressive follow-up released years later. |
| Wheel | Wheel | 2010 | This German band produces extremely heavy, stripped-back doom that focuses on massive, repetitive riffs and a powerhouse vocal performance. It is "pure" doom in its simplest and most effective form. I Shall Die Here is another notable title. |
| Argus | Boldly Stride the Sky | 2011 | Argus blends Traditional Doom with the twin-guitar harmonies of Classic Metal (like Iron Maiden), creating a high-energy yet distinctly heavy sound. Beyond the Martyrs is another essential listen. |
| Lord Vicar | Fear No Pain | 2008 | Formed by members of Reverend Bizarre and Count Raven, this band delivers grim, no-nonsense traditional doom with a focus on massive volume and pessimistic themes. Signs of Osiris is another standout in their discography. |
| Memory Garden | Tides | 1996 | A Swedish band that blends the epic weight of Candlemass with a slightly more progressive and power-metal edge. The production is crisp and the vocals are powerful and clear. Verdict of Posterity is also a key release. |
| Forsaken | Dominaeon | 2005 | Hailing from Malta, this band is a pillar of the Mediterranean doom scene, specializing in majestic, slow-motion heavy metal with a strong liturgical feel. Animatograph is another highly praised album. |
| Revelation | Salvation's Answer | 1991 | A "Thinking Man's" doom band, they incorporated progressive rock structures and introspective lyrics into a very heavy, slow framework. Never Comes Silence is their other essential contribution to the genre. |
| Isole | Bliss of Solitude | 2008 | This Swedish group masters the art of melancholic Epic Doom, using dual vocal harmonies and sweeping, atmospheric guitar melodies to create a sense of vast emptiness. Silent Ruins is also an excellent example of their style. |
| Thunder Horse | Chosen One | 2021 | A modern example of "Southern Doom," blending the weight of the Texas sun with massive, Sabbath-inspired riffs and a thick, fuzzy production. After the Fall is their equally heavy follow-up. |
| Doomsword | Resound the Horn | 2002 | Italian masters of "Epic Barbarian Doom," their sound is heavily inspired by 80s metal and heroic fantasy literature, delivered with slow-burning intensity. Let Battle Commence is another essential record. |
| Procession | To Reap Heavens Apart | 2013 | Originally from Chile, this band plays traditional doom with a fierce, old-school conviction and a "larger-than-life" vocal performance. Spells from the Fire is their equally powerful debut. |
| Magma | Magma | 1975 | Not to be confused with the French Zeuhl band, this French group (later known as Magma/Les Variations) produced incredibly heavy, proto-doom riffs and a dark, psychedelic atmosphere. |
| Dawn of Winter | The Peaceful Village | 2008 | A German band that strictly adheres to the "Slow and Heavy" mantra, focusing on crushing simplicity and traditional structures. In the Valley of Tears is their other primary work. |
| Hour of 13 | The Ritualist | 2010 | This project blends traditional doom with the occult atmosphere of 70s horror films, featuring melodic yet eerie vocals and classic, driving riffs. Hour of 13 (self-titled) is also a classic of the revival. |
| Spiritus Mortis | The Great Seal | 2022 | Finland's first doom band, they eventually recruited Albert Witchfinder (Reverend Bizarre) to create this slab of heavy, traditional metal with a slightly theatrical edge. The God Behind the God is another key release. |
| Black Pyramid | Black Pyramid | 2009 | Fusing stoner-rock grooves with the epic scale of traditional doom, this band creates massive "walls of sound" and features lyrical themes of ancient battles. II is their other most definitive work. |
| Below | Across the River Sands | 2014 | This Swedish band follows the "Epic Doom" tradition of Candlemass perfectly, with high-quality production and soaring, emotional vocal performances. Upon a Pale Horse is their second strong outing. |
| Mortalicum | The End of Line | 2014 | A Swedish band that emphasizes the groovy, blues-rock side of doom, creating music that is heavy yet surprisingly catchy and rhythmic. Eyes of the Demon is another essential title. |
| The Sword | Age of Winters | 2006 | While they later shifted toward hard rock, their debut is a masterclass in heavy, fantasy-themed doom with a "fuzzy" retro production that helped bring the genre to a younger audience. Gods of the Earth is also essential. |
| Khemmis | Hunted | 2016 | A perfect blend of "doom and gloom" with soaring dual-guitar harmonies reminiscent of Thin Lizzy or Iron Maiden. It represents the modern evolution of melodic doom. Absolution is their equally strong debut. |
| Mountain Witch | Burning Village | 2017 | This band captures the "70s Proto-Doom" aesthetic perfectly, using vintage gear and a raw, analog sound to evoke the early days of heavy rock. Extinct Cults is their other notable release. |
| Wretched | The Road to Hell | 1988 | A rare example of early American doom that leaned into a darker, more aggressive sound without becoming thrash or death metal. Vile is another underground classic of theirs. |
| Sorcerer | In the Shadow of the Inverted Cross | 2015 | Though they formed in the 80s, their modern comeback albums are masterpieces of Epic Doom, featuring massive production and incredibly polished songwriting. The Night Visitor is another highlight. |
| Magic Circle | Magic Circle | 2013 | This band brings a "hardcore" energy to traditional doom, resulting in music that is slow and heavy but possesses a raw, urgent intensity. Journey Blind is also highly recommended. |
| Stygian Crown | Stygian Crown | 2020 | A newer "Epic Doom" band that incorporates "Death-Doom" weight (specifically a heavy, bolt-thrower-esque rhythm section) but with clean, operatic female vocals. |
