# Stoner Metal

Stoner metal crystallized in the early 1990s in the California desert, drawing on the downtuned heaviness of Black Sabbath, the spacey improvisation of 1970s psychedelic rock, and the raw energy of punk. The sound is characterized by thick, fuzz-drenched guitar tones, repetitive hypnotic riffs, a heavy rhythmic groove, and a warm analog production aesthetic that evokes sun-baked landscapes and hazy consciousness. Typical instrumentation is the classic power-trio format of guitar, bass, and drums, with liberal use of wah-wah pedals, phaser effects, and vintage amplifiers cranked to their limits. Vocals tend toward a laid-back, bluesy delivery, often buried in the mix beneath walls of distortion. Lyrically and thematically, the genre gravitates toward psychedelia, desert imagery, retro science fiction, and counterculture attitudes. Stoner metal has spawned a global scene with particularly strong footholds in Sweden, Germany, and Australia, thriving in underground circuits and festival culture.

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Kyuss | Welcome to Sky Valley | 1994 | This album is the quintessential blueprint for the genre, featuring low-tuned, "sun-baked" guitar riffs, a heavy groove, and a sprawling, desert-influenced atmosphere. It perfectly balances psychedelic jams with massive, fuzzy hooks, as heard also on their other essential releases Blues for the Red Sun and ...And the Circus Leaves Town. |
| Sleep | Holy Mountain | 1992 | Defining the heavy, Sabbath-worshipping side of the genre, this record utilizes massive, slow-motion riffs and a thick, "wall of sound" production style. Its repetitive, hypnotic structures and weed-themed lyricism set the standard for the "stoner" aesthetic, further explored in their hour-long epic Dopesmoker. |
| Monster Magnet | Spine of God | 1991 | This album bridges the gap between garage rock energy and heavy psychedelic space rock, characterized by swirling effects and "trippy" lyrics. It emphasizes the genre's debt to 70s acid rock and Hawkwind, a sound they continued on Superjudge and Dopes to Infinity. |
| Fu Manchu | In Search Of... | 1996 | Representing the "fuzz rock" side of the spectrum, this album focuses on catchy, mid-tempo riffs and lyrics centered on 70s muscle car culture and skateboarding. The production is dry and punchy, capturing the raw energy found on their other staples like The Action Is Go and Daredevil. |
| Orange Goblin | Time Travelling Blues | 1998 | A cornerstone of the British scene, this album blends heavy stoner grooves with a classic rock and doom metal backbone. It features beer-soaked vocals and bluesy, swinging rhythms, themes they also mastered on Frequencies from Planet Ten and The Big Black. |
| Electric Wizard | Come My Fanatics... | 1997 | While often leaning into doom, this specific record defines the genre through its extremely distorted, "crushing" fuzz and occult-driven psychedelic atmosphere. It sounds ancient and monolithic, setting the stage for their legendary Dopethrone. |
| Bongzilla | Gateway | 2002 | This album is a masterclass in "sludgy" stoner metal, utilizing raspy vocals and incredibly slow, blues-inflected riffs that feel physically heavy. Its singular focus on cannabis culture and grit is also prominent on Stash and Amerijuanican. |
| Corrosion of Conformity | Deliverance | 1994 | Shifting from their hardcore roots, this album introduced a southern-fried stoner sound characterized by soulful vocals and heavy, Sabbath-style riffing. It brings a "swampy" groove to the genre, which they refined further on Wiseblood. |
| Clutch | Clutch | 1995 | This self-titled effort is a landmark for its integration of funk-inspired rhythms and quirky, surrealist lyrics into a heavy rock framework. The interplay between the driving bass and percussive guitar work is unique to their sound, also heard on Transnational Speedway League. |
| Nebula | To the Center | 1999 | Formed by former Kyuss and Fu Manchu members, this album leans heavily into the psychedelic, "space-fuzz" side of the genre. It features wild wah-wah solos and a loose, improvisational feel similar to their Sun Creature EP and Charged. |
| Goatsnake | Flower of Disease | 2000 | This album defines the genre through its massive, melodic vocal hooks paired with some of the heaviest, most resonant guitar tones in the scene. It feels soulful yet immovable, a dynamic also present on their debut 1. |
| The Obsessed | The Church Within | 1994 | Led by Scott "Wino" Weinrich, this album showcases the "street-level" stoner metal sound with biker-rock attitude and intricate, bluesy soloing. It is more grounded and hard-rock oriented than its peers, much like their album Lunar Womb. |
| Stoned Jesus | Seven Thunders Roar | 2012 | A modern classic that emphasizes the progressive and melodic potential of the genre with long, building compositions like "I'm the Mountain". It captures a perfect balance of heavy riffs and folk-tinged acoustic passages, as also seen on First Communion. |
| Weedeater | God Luck and Good Speed | 2007 | This record provides a raw, stripped-back take on the genre, featuring a massive, distorted bass sound and gravelly, aggressive vocals. It emphasizes the "dirty" and unpolished side of the stoner-sludge spectrum, similar to Jason... The Dragon. |
| Lowrider | Ode to Io | 2000 | Often cited as the best Kyuss-style album not made by Kyuss, this Swedish masterpiece features soaring desert-rock vocals and warm, fuzzy grooves. It defines the "Desert Rock" aesthetic with high precision, a sound they revisited years later on Refractions. |
| Acid King | Busse Woods | 1999 | This album is defined by its hypnotic, mid-tempo pacing and the ethereal, melodic vocals of Lori S., which float over a sea of thick distortion. It creates a tranced-out, heavy atmosphere that is quintessential to the genre, also found on III. |
| Dozer | Through the Eyes of Heathens | 2005 | This album showcases a high-energy, "space-fuzz" direction with crisp production and powerful, melodic delivery. It bridges the gap between stoner metal and modern heavy rock, a style they perfected on Madre de Dios. |
| Truckfighters | Gravity X | 2005 | Known for their high-octane energy, this debut features "fuzz" pedals pushed to the limit and driving, infectious rhythms. It is the definitive example of the high-energy Swedish stoner scene, also exemplified on Phi. |
| Colour Haze | Tempel | 2006 | Representing the European "Jam" style, this album utilizes warm, analog tones and long, jazzy improvisational sections within heavy structures. It is more technical and lush than American stoner metal, as heard on their Self-Titled record. |
| Spirit Caravan | Jug Fulla Sun | 1999 | Another "Wino" project, this album is defined by its spiritual, uplifting tone and classic power-trio chemistry. It focuses on pure, soulful songwriting and timeless heavy riffs, similar to their follow-up Elusive Truth. |
| 7Zuma7 | Deep Inside... | 1999 | This album is a prime example of the "stoner-n-roll" sub-style, mixing heavy fuzz with a high-energy garage rock and blues swagger. It's groovy, fast-paced, and devoid of the slower doom influences found in other branch bands. |
| Ufomammut | Snailking | 2004 | This record pushes the genre into psychedelic and industrial territories, using synthesizers and repetitive, crushing riffs to create a "space-doom" atmosphere. It is more experimental and dense than standard stoner metal, a path they continued on Eve. |
| Samsara Blues Experiment | Long Distance Trip | 2010 | This album defines the heavy psychedelic side of the genre, incorporating sitars and long, spiraling guitar solos. It feels like a heavy 70s vintage throwback with modern weight, much like their album Revelation & Bliss. |
| Elder | Dead Roots Stirring | 2011 | While they eventually moved toward prog, this album is a definitive stoner metal record with massive, melodic riffs and evolving song structures. It captures the genre's shift toward more complex, "epic" storytelling, also present on their Self-Titled debut. |
| Windhand | Soma | 2013 | This album uses a massive, low-end drone and haunting, reverb-drenched female vocals to create a foggy, psychedelic atmosphere. It leans into the "forest-dwelling" aesthetic of the genre, which they refined on Grief's Infernal Flower. |
| Calamity Rocket | Calamity Rocket | 2008 | An overlooked gem that captures the raw, desert-rock essence with driving tempos and dusty, sun-bleached production. It emphasizes the "highway-rock" feeling of the genre with straightforward, effective riffing. |
| Greenleaf | Agents of Ahriman | 2007 | Originally a side project, this album defines the genre's "supergroup" era with bluesy vocals and infectious, swinging guitar work. It's more polished and "rocking" than doom-leaning bands, a sound also found on Secret Blossoms. |
| Red Fang | Murder the Mountains | 2011 | This album brings a "working-man's" grit to the genre, featuring dual vocals and catchy, high-energy heavy metal riffs. It is punchy and accessible without losing the fuzzy edge, similar to their Self-Titled debut. |
| The Sword | Age of Winters | 2006 | This album leans into the "fantasy/literary" side of the genre, with crisp, interlocking riffs and lyrics about mythology and epic battles. It has a more traditional heavy metal structure but keeps the low-tuned stoner groove, also found on Gods of the Earth. |
| Torche | Meanderthal | 2008 | Defining "stoner pop," this album uses extremely low-tuned guitars to play bright, major-key melodies and catchy hooks. It is unique for its "bomb-string" tuning and upbeat energy, a style they pioneered on their Self-Titled debut. |
