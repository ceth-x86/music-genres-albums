| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| The 13th Floor Elevators | The Psychedelic Sounds of the 13th Floor Elevators | 1966 | Often cited as the first true psychedelic rock album, it features Tommy Hall's "electric jug" and Roky Erickson's frantic vocals, creating a reverb-drenched, hallucinogenic atmosphere. Other essential works include Easter Everywhere. |
| The Jimi Hendrix Experience | Are You Experienced | 1967 | This album redefined the electric guitar through the use of fuzz, feedback, and backward recording, merging blues roots with cosmic exploration. Hendrix also produced the psych-masterpieces Axis: Bold as Love and Electric Ladyland. |
| Pink Floyd | The Piper at the Gates of Dawn | 1967 | Led by Syd Barrett, this record captures the whimsical yet unsettling British psychedelic sound, utilizing childlike imagery, long instrumental jams, and avant-garde studio effects. Barrett-era Floyd also produced the essential A Saucerful of Secrets. |
| The Doors | The Doors | 1967 | Defined by Ray Manzarek's swirling organ melodies and Jim Morrison's shamanic poetry, this album's dark, moody atmosphere set the standard for the genre's "acid rock" side. The band also released the definitive Strange Days. |
| Jefferson Airplane | Surrealistic Pillow | 1967 | A cornerstone of the San Francisco Sound, it blends folk-rock harmonies with trippy instrumentation and lyrics directly referencing drug culture, most notably on "White Rabbit." They also released the experimental After Bathing at Baxter's. |
| Love | Forever Changes | 1967 | Arthur Lee's masterpiece uses lush orchestral arrangements and acoustic guitars to create a sophisticated, slightly paranoid psychedelic landscape. The band's harder-edged Da Capo is also a high point of the era. |
| The Beatles | Magical Mystery Tour | 1967 | While Sgt. Pepper is legendary, this collection leans harder into pure psychedelia with surreal soundscapes, phasing, and complex studio arrangements on tracks like "Strawberry Fields Forever." Revolver remains their other primary psych-rock contribution. |
| The Grateful Dead | Anthem of the Sun | 1968 | This experimental collage of live and studio recordings captures the band's free-form improvisational style and "acid test" origins. Their self-titled debut and Live/Dead are also foundational to the genre. |
| Cream | Disraeli Gears | 1967 | The ultimate "power trio" record, it features Eric Clapton's "woman tone" and wah-wah pedals, blending heavy blues with surreal, colorful lyrics. Their follow-up, Wheels of Fire, further explored extended psychedelic jamming. |
| The Yardbirds | Roger the Engineer | 1966 | Moving away from R&B, this album utilized heavy distortion and Gregorian chants to pioneer the heavier side of psych-rock. Guitarist Jeff Beck also explored these sounds on the influential Truth. |
| The Byrds | The Fifth Dimension | 1966 | Transitioning from folk-rock, this album introduced "raga rock" via Coltrane-influenced guitar drones on the track "Eight Miles High." The band continued this exploration on Younger Than Yesterday. |
| Iron Butterfly | In-A-Gadda-Da-Vida | 1968 | Known for its 17-minute title track, this album brought a heavy, organ-driven darkness to the genre, bridging the gap between psychedelia and early heavy metal. Their debut, Heavy, is also highly regarded. |
| Quicksilver Messenger Service | Happy Trails | 1969 | A definitive example of the San Francisco ballroom sound, featuring the dual guitar interplay of John Cipollina and Gary Duncan over long, improvisational blues jams. Their self-titled debut is equally essential. |
| The Zombies | Odessey and Oracle | 1968 | A peak of psychedelic pop, featuring mellotrons and baroque vocal harmonies that create a dreamy, timeless atmosphere. Key tracks like "Time of the Season" define the era's sophisticated side. |
| The Rolling Stones | Their Satanic Majesties Request | 1967 | The Stones' most overtly psychedelic work, utilizing tablas, African rhythms, and Mellotrons to create a chaotic, immersive sonic world. It stands as a unique, experimental pivot before their return to roots-rock. |
| Strawberry Alarm Clock | Incense and Peppermints | 1967 | Combining bubblegum pop melodies with fuzzy guitars and farfisa organs, this album encapsulates the "flower power" aesthetic of the late 60s. The follow-up, Wake Up... It's Tomorrow, is also a cult classic. |
| Blue Cheer | Vincebus Eruptum | 1968 | Often called the first heavy metal album, its extreme volume and fuzz-heavy covers represent the raw, "heavy" end of the psychedelic spectrum. They followed this with the equally loud Outsideinside. |
| Country Joe & The Fish | Electric Music for the Mind and Body | 1967 | One of the most political and overtly "druggy" albums of the San Francisco scene, featuring organ-heavy textures and satirical lyrics. I-Feel-Like-I'm-Fixin'-to-Die is another staple of their catalog. |
| The Soft Machine | The Soft Machine | 1968 | Merging whimsical English psychedelia with early jazz-fusion and Dadaist lyrics, this album is a pillar of the Canterbury scene's early psych roots. Their follow-up, Volume Two, is even more experimental. |
| Spirit | The Family That Plays Together | 1968 | Blending jazz, rock, and classical influences, this album features Randy California's sophisticated guitar work and an eclectic, trippy songwriting approach. Their debut and Twelve Dreams of Dr. Sardonicus are also masterpieces. |
| Gong | Flying Teapot | 1973 | A foundational "Space Rock" album that uses synthesizers, glissando guitar, and mythology to create a unique psychedelic universe. It is part of the essential Radio Gnome Invisible trilogy. |
| Tomorrow | Tomorrow | 1968 | A classic of the UK underground, featuring Steve Howe on guitar; it blends melodic pop sensibilities with heavy phasing and eccentric storytelling. Their single "My White Bicycle" is a genre anthem. |
| The Pretty Things | S.F. Sorrow | 1968 | One of the first rock operas, this album uses sitars, tape loops, and dense layering to tell a dark, psychedelic narrative. It is often compared to Tommy but remains more experimental in its soundscapes. |
| Traffic | Mr. Fantasy | 1967 | Led by Steve Winwood, this album combines soulful vocals with flutes, sitars, and organ-drenched jams, capturing the "cottage industry" vibe of UK psych. The Low Spark of High Heeled Boys is their later prog-leaning classic. |
| Vanilla Fudge | Vanilla Fudge | 1967 | Known for slowed-down, heavy organ-driven covers of pop songs, this album pioneered the "Heavy Psych" sound that influenced early Doom. Their follow-up, The Beat Goes On, is a bizarre experimental collage. |
| Os Mutantes | Os Mutantes | 1968 | A centerpiece of Brazil's Tropicalia movement, this album uses homemade distortion pedals and found sounds to create a wildly inventive, subversive psychedelic pop. Mutantes and A Divina Comedia are also essential. |
| The Electric Prunes | I Had Too Much to Dream (Last Night) | 1967 | Characterized by its use of tremolo, fuzz, and oscillating sound effects, this album is a highlight of the garage-psych crossover. They also released the conceptual Mass in F Minor. |
| Silver Apples | Silver Apples | 1968 | An outlier that used homemade oscillators and primitive synthesizers decades before their time, creating a pulsating, rhythmic psychedelia. Their second album, Contact, is also a must-listen for electronic psych fans. |
| Moby Grape | Moby Grape | 1967 | Featuring three lead guitarists and five vocalists, this album perfected the "San Francisco" blend of rock, folk, and blues with a high-energy psychedelic edge. They also released the sprawling Wow. |
| The United States of America | The United States of America | 1968 | A pioneering work that eschewed guitars in favor of ring modulators and primitive synthesizers, creating a cold, avant-garde psychedelic experience. This was their only album, but it remains a cult masterpiece. |
| Gandalf | Gandalf | 1969 | A dreamy, organ-heavy album that captures the softer, more mystical side of the genre with ethereal vocals and lush production. It remains one of the most sought-after "lost" psych treasures. |
| July | July | 1968 | A British psych classic that utilizes sitars, tablas, and backwards tapes to create a quintessential "Summer of Love" sound. Songs like "Dandelion Seeds" are definitive examples of the era's experimentation. |
| H.P. Lovecraft | H.P. Lovecraft II | 1968 | Named after the horror author, this band used haunting vocal harmonies and eerie organ textures to create a "Gothic" psychedelic sound. Their first album is equally atmospheric and essential. |
| The Move | Move | 1968 | This album showcases the heavy, melodic British psych sound that would eventually evolve into ELO and Wizzard. Their later work, Shazam, features even longer, heavier psychedelic explorations. |
| The Deviants | Ptooff! | 1967 | A raw, underground classic that leaned into the "anarchist" side of the scene, featuring distorted guitars and spoken word passages. Led by Mick Farren, they also released Disposable. |
| Arzachel | Arzachel | 1969 | A heavy, organ-driven blues-psych album featuring members who would later form Egg and Khan; it represents the darker, more instrumental side of the UK underground. The track "Metempsychosis" is a standout jam. |
| The Creation | We Are Paintermen | 1967 | Famous for guitarist Eddie Phillips using a violin bow on his guitar, this album sits at the intersection of "Freakbeat" and pure psychedelia. Their compilation Our Music Is Red with Purple Flashes is also vital. |
| Ultimate Spinach | Ultimate Spinach | 1968 | Part of the "Bosstown Sound," this album features complex arrangements, anti-war themes, and heavy use of the Theremin and fuzz guitar. They also released the ambitious Behold & See. |
| Fifty Foot Hose | Cauldron | 1968 | Similar to Silver Apples, this band integrated electronic noise and oscillators into a rock framework, creating a jarring, futuristic psychedelic sound. It is a key link between psych and early industrial music. |
| The West Coast Pop Art Experimental Band | Part One | 1967 | Blending delicate folk-pop with abrasive noise and political lyrics, this album is a strange, multifaceted example of the L.A. scene. Vol. 2 (Breaking Through) is their most overtly psychedelic work. |
| Moving Sidewalks | Flash | 1968 | Featuring a young Billy Gibbons (later of ZZ Top), this album is a Texas psych staple, characterized by heavy reverb, feedback, and bluesy riffs. Their single "99th Floor" is a garage-psych anthem. |
| Spacemen 3 | The Perfect Prescription | 1987 | Representing the "Neo-Psychedelia" revival, this album uses minimalist drones, gospel influences, and heavy tremolo to simulate a chemical "trip." Playing with Fire is their other definitive masterpiece. |
| The Brian Jonestown Massacre | Their Satanic Majesties' Second Request | 1996 | A 90s homage to the 60s sound, incorporating sitars, tablas, and 12-string guitars to create a sprawling, authentic psychedelic experience. Methodrone is their essential shoegaze-leaning work. |
| Kula Shaker | K | 1996 | A Britpop-era revival of "Raga Rock," blending heavy 60s-style organ riffs with Indian mysticism and Sanskrit lyrics. Their follow-up Peasants, Pigs & Astronauts expanded on these themes. |
| Tame Impala | Innerspeaker | 2010 | Kevin Parker revived the genre for the modern era with phasing guitars, distorted drums, and "dreamy" vocal melodies that sound both vintage and fresh. Lonerism is his more synth-heavy psych-pop follow-up. |
| King Gizzard & The Lizard Wizard | I'm in Your Mind Fuzz | 2014 | This album showcases the band's signature "motorik" psychedelic beat and heavy flute usage, creating a relentless, high-energy trip. They also explored psych-jazz on Quarters! and Sketches of Brunswick East. |
| The Black Angels | Passover | 2006 | Inspired by the Velvet Underground and the 13th Floor Elevators, this album features dark, droning guitars and war-themed lyrics, defining the modern "Austin Psych" scene. Directions to See a Ghost is another dark essential. |
| Temples | Sun Structures | 2014 | A meticulously produced revivalist album that captures the ornate, melodic British psych sound of 1967 with shimmering guitars and 12-string textures. Volcano added more synth elements to their sound. |
| The Dukes of Stratosphear | Psonic Psunspot | 1987 | An XTC side-project that perfectly parodies and pays tribute to the 60s psych era, utilizing authentic vintage equipment and songwriting styles. 25 O'Clock is their equally brilliant EP. |
| Unknown Mortal Orchestra | Unknown Mortal Orchestra | 2011 | Featuring lo-fi production and intricate, soulful guitar work, this album reimagines psychedelia through a quirky, modern bedroom-pop lens. II is also a key record in their discography. |
