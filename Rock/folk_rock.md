# Folk Rock

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| The Byrds | Mr. Tambourine Man | 1965 | This album is the foundational blueprint of the genre, blending Bob Dylan's poetic lyricism with the jangling 12-string Rickenbacker sound and Beatles-esque vocal harmonies. It effectively bridged the gap between the Greenwich Village folk scene and the British Invasion. Other essential titles include Turn! Turn! Turn! and Fifth Dimension. |
| Bob Dylan | Bringing It All Back Home | 1965 | Marking the moment Dylan "went electric," the A-side features a full rock band backing his surrealist folk poetry, fundamentally shifting the trajectory of popular music. It maintains a raw, bluesy energy while preserving the lyrical depth of traditional folk. Other notable albums include Highway 61 Revisited and Blonde on Blonde. |
| Simon & Garfunkel | Sounds of Silence | 1966 | Following the overdubbing of electric instruments onto their acoustic track, this album perfected the polished, cerebral side of folk rock with intricate vocal layering and poetic urban narratives. It emphasizes melody and crystalline production over grit. Also consider Parsley, Sage, Rosemary and Thyme and Bookends. |
| Fairport Convention | Liege & Lief | 1969 | This masterpiece invented "British Folk Rock" by applying rock instrumentation and rhythms to traditional English ballads and jigs rather than American blues. Sandy Denny's ethereal vocals and Richard Thompson's inventive guitar work create a unique, pastoral intensity. Other vital releases are What We Did on Our Holidays and Unhalfbricking. |
| The Mamas & the Papas | If You Can Believe Your Eyes and Ears | 1966 | Representing the sun-drenched California sound, this album features sophisticated four-part vocal harmonies backed by a tight rock rhythm section. It leans toward the "pop" end of the folk rock spectrum while retaining acoustic textures. Deliver is another significant entry. |
| Buffalo Springfield | Buffalo Springfield | 1966 | This debut showcased the synergy of Stephen Stills and Neil Young, blending protest lyrics with country-inflected rock arrangements and biting dual-lead guitars. It captures the transition from folk's earnestness to rock's electric power. Their follow-up, Again, is equally definitive. |
| The Lovin' Spoonful | Do You Believe in Magic | 1965 | John Sebastian's "good-time music" integrated jug-band sensibilities and autoharp into a rock format, creating a breezy, upbeat variation of the genre. It reflects the optimism of the mid-60s folk revival entering the mainstream. Daydream is another essential listen. |
| The Turtles | It Ain't Me Babe | 1965 | Led by Howard Kaylan and Mark Volman, this album leaned heavily into the Dylan-cover trend but with a distinctively lush, harmonic pop-rock sensibility. It helped solidify the commercial viability of the folk rock sound on the West Coast. You Baby also fits this era perfectly. |
| Donovan | Sunshine Superman | 1966 | Transitioning from an acoustic "Dylan clone" to a pioneer of psychedelic folk rock, Donovan introduced sitars and jazzy arrangements to his melodic folk base. The album creates a whimsical, medieval-meets-modern atmosphere. Mellow Yellow is another key transition record. |
| Jefferson Airplane | Jefferson Airplane Takes Off | 1966 | Before they fully embraced heavy psychedelia, their debut was a quintessential folk rock record featuring Signe Toly Anderson's traditional folk vocals and Marty Balin's earnest songwriting. It features clean electric guitar tones and standard rock structures. Surrealistic Pillow marks their shift toward psych-rock. |
| The Band | Music from Big Pink | 1968 | By stripping away the studio sheen of the era, The Band created a rustic, rootsy sound that returned folk rock to its earthy, communal origins using organ, mandolin, and gritty harmonies. It defined the "Americana" subset of the genre. Their self-titled "Brown Album" is also a masterpiece. |
| Steeleye Span | Hark! The Village Wait | 1970 | Taking the British folk rock template further, this album utilizes electric bass and drums to reinvigorate ancient traditional songs with a driving, often dark energy. Maddy Prior's powerful folk voice provides a stark contrast to the rock backbeat. Please to See the King is another staple. |
| Crosby, Stills & Nash | Crosby, Stills & Nash | 1969 | This "supergroup" debut perfected the acoustic-electric hybrid through impeccable three-part harmonies and intricate fingerpicking styles layered over a rock foundation. It represents the maturation of the 60s folk-rock movement into the 70s singer-songwriter era. Déjà Vu (with Neil Young) is also essential. |
| Lindisfarne | Nicely Out of Tune | 1970 | Blending Geordie folk sensibilities with a pub-rock enthusiasm, this album uses mandolin and harmonica to create a raucous, accessible version of the genre. It focuses on storytelling and catchy, sing-along melodies. Fog on the Tyne is their biggest commercial success. |
| Pentangle | Basket of Light | 1969 | A sophisticated blend of folk, jazz, and early music, featuring the dual guitar interplay of Bert Jansch and John Renbourn and Danny Thompson's double bass. While intricate, the rock-steady rhythm section keeps it firmly within the folk rock lineage. Their self-titled debut and Sweet Child are also seminal. |
| Neil Young | Harvest | 1972 | This album defines the "mellow" folk rock of the early 70s, utilizing pedal steel, acoustic guitar, and Young's signature fragile vocals against a soft rock backdrop. It balances personal introspection with broad melodic appeal. After the Gold Rush and Comes a Time are other high-water marks. |
| Joni Mitchell | Court and Spark | 1974 | While her earlier work was purely folk, this record introduced a jazz-rock fusion backing band that elevated her sophisticated folk compositions into a new realm of "sophisti-folk rock." The production is slick yet emotionally resonant. For the Roses and The Hissing of Summer Lawns are also significant. |
| The Beau Brummels | Introducing the Beau Brummels | 1965 | One of the first American bands to respond to the British Invasion by mixing folk-style melancholia with a driving beat and 12-string guitars. They helped establish the San Francisco scene's folk-rock roots. Triangle is their later, more experimental masterwork. |
| Gene Clark | No Other | 1974 | After leaving The Byrds, Clark created this sprawling, high-budget epic that blends cosmic folk with gospel and rock orchestrations. It is a dense, philosophical take on the genre that remains a cult classic. White Light is his more stripped-back folk rock effort. |
| Richard & Linda Thompson | I Want to See the Bright Lights Tonight | 1974 | Post-Fairport Convention, Richard Thompson paired his stinging electric guitar with Linda's mournful vocals to create a stark, emotionally heavy folk rock. The arrangements are sparse but rhythmically driving. Shoot Out the Lights is their other essential collaboration. |
| The Youngbloods | The Youngbloods | 1967 | Led by Jesse Colin Young, this band specialized in a gentle, "good vibes" folk rock that incorporated elements of blues and jug-band music. Their sound is characterized by melodic basslines and shimmering guitar. Elephant Mountain is another highly regarded title. |
| Cat Stevens | Tea for the Tillerman | 1970 | Defined by heavy acoustic strumming and soulful, gravelly vocals, this album bridges the gap between pure folk and the radio-friendly folk rock of the 70s. The arrangements are simple but feature a tight, driving rock percussion. Teaser and the Firecat is equally definitive. |
| Trees | On the Shore | 1970 | A standout in the British acid-folk-rock scene, this album features long, brooding instrumental passages and haunting female vocals. It leans into a darker, more atmospheric interpretation of traditional melodies. Their debut, The Garden of Jane Delawney, is also notable. |
| The Incredible String Band | The Hangman's Beautiful Daughter | 1968 | Though highly experimental, this album is a pillar of "acid folk rock," using a vast array of world instruments over a psychedelic folk-rock framework. It captures the whimsical, boundary-pushing spirit of the late 60s. The 5000 Spirits or the Layers of the Onion is another key work. |
| Grateful Dead | American Beauty | 1970 | Shifting away from their experimental jams, this album focus on tight songwriting, acoustic guitars, and high-lonesome vocal harmonies. It is a quintessential example of "Country-Folk Rock" with a distinctively laid-back West Coast feel. Workingman's Dead is the perfect companion piece. |
| America | America | 1971 | Known for their shimmering 12-string guitars and three-part vocal stacks, this debut brought a polished, radio-ready sound to the folk rock template. The music is characterized by breezy tempos and lush, desert-themed imagery. Homecoming is their other major work. |
| John Denver | Poems, Prayers & Promises | 1971 | This album exemplifies the mainstream, populist side of folk rock, utilizing simple acoustic structures bolstered by light rock percussion and strings. It focuses on nature and Americana themes with clear, soaring vocals. Rocky Mountain High is another essential title. |
| Al Stewart | Year of the Cat | 1976 | Stewart blended historical narrative lyrics with a sophisticated, soft-rock production style that utilized folk-rock acoustic guitar as a core element. The sound is polished, featuring intricate arrangements and smooth melodic shifts. Past, Present and Future is his more folk-focused epic. |
| James Taylor | Sweet Baby James | 1970 | This record defined the sensitive singer-songwriter era of folk rock, featuring Taylor's intricate fingerstyle guitar and warm, conversational vocals. While intimate, the backing band provides a steady, subtle rock groove. Mud Slide Slim and the Blue Horizon is another definitive release. |
| Gordon Lightfoot | Sit Down Young Stranger | 1970 | (Later retitled If You Could Read My Mind) This album showcases Lightfoot's baritone and masterful 12-string guitar work, supported by subtle bass and percussion. It is a masterclass in the storytelling tradition of folk rock. Sundown is his other major electric-folk success. |
| Tim Buckley | Goodbye and Hello | 1967 | A grandiose fusion of folk poetry, baroque arrangements, and rock dynamics, Buckley's soaring multi-octave voice carries the emotional weight of the record. It represents the ambitious, artistic heights of the 1960s folk rock boom. Happy Sad moves into jazz-folk territory. |
| Judy Collins | Wildflowers | 1967 | Transitioning from traditional folk to a more orchestral folk-rock sound, this album features covers of Joni Mitchell and Leonard Cohen backed by lush, pop-rock arrangements. It helped elevate the genre's "artistic" status. Who Knows Where the Time Goes is another key electric transition. |
| The Holy Modal Rounders | The Moray Eels Eat The Holy Modal Rounders | 1968 | A chaotic, "freak-folk-rock" record that deconstructs traditional folk songs with rock energy and psychedelic weirdness. They were key figures in the underground folk-rock scene. Indian War Whoop is another notable, albeit stranger, release. |
| Fred Neil | Fred Neil | 1966 | Featuring his deep, resonant voice and rhythmic 12-string guitar style, this album (containing "Everybody's Talkin'") influenced everyone from Tim Buckley to the Jefferson Airplane. It has a jazzy, effortless folk-rock swing. Bleecker & MacDougal is his other essential work. |
| Poco | Pickin' up the Pieces | 1969 | Formed from the ashes of Buffalo Springfield, Poco pioneered the country-folk-rock sound with prominent pedal steel and high-energy rock rhythms. Their focus on upbeat tempos and harmony vocals influenced the entire 70s Southern California scene. Deliverin' is also vital. |
| Fairport Convention (feat. Sandy Denny) | The North Star Grassman and the Ravens | 1971 | Actually Sandy Denny's solo debut, this record maintains the Fairport "British Folk Rock" DNA with intricate electric guitar/piano interplay and her peerless, folk-rooted vocals. It is essentially a sophisticated extension of the genre. Sandy is her other must-listen. |
| Renaissance | Renaissance | 1969 | Featuring ex-Yardbirds members, this debut blended folk melodies with classical piano and rock percussion, creating a "baroque folk rock" sound. It predates their shift into full symphonic prog. Illusion is the other key album from this lineup. |
| The Pentangle | Cruel Sister | 1970 | A departure from their jazzier work, this album focuses on long, electric-folk reinterpretations of traditional ballads. It features prominent electric guitar work by Jansch and Renbourn while maintaining their intricate acoustic roots. |
| Tudor Lodge | Tudor Lodge | 1971 | A gem of the UK folk-rock scene, this album features lush vocal harmonies, flute, and a gentle rock backbeat. It exemplifies the pastoral, slightly melancholic vibe of the early 70s British movement. |
| Amazing Blondel | Evensong | 1970 | Utilizing period instruments like lutes and recorders within a folk-rock context, this "pseudo-Elizabethan" album offers a unique, historical take on the genre. It is rhythmic and melodic, despite the archaic instrumentation. Fantasia Lindum is another key work. |
| Matthews' Southern Comfort | Later That Same Year | 1970 | Led by ex-Fairport singer Ian Matthews, this album blends British folk sensibilities with American country-rock textures. It features clean, jangly guitars and smooth vocal harmonies. Second Spring is another representative title. |
| Fotheringay | Fotheringay | 1970 | A short-lived supergroup led by Sandy Denny, this album is a heavy, professional folk-rock tour de force. It features driving electric guitar solos alongside delicate acoustic ballads. |
| The Strawbs | From the Witchwood | 1971 | This album captures the transition from acoustic bluegrass-folk to a more "heavy" folk rock sound, featuring Rick Wakeman's organ and Dave Cousins' unique vibrato. It is atmospheric and often mystical. Grave New World is their other folk-rock peak. |
| Magna Carta | Seasons | 1970 | A conceptual folk-rock album featuring acoustic layers, spoken word, and a soft-rock rhythm section. It is highly melodic and representative of the more sensitive British folk-rock sound. Songs from Wasties Orchard is another notable release. |
| Mellow Candle | Swaddling Songs | 1972 | An Irish folk-rock masterpiece that combines complex vocal harmonies with a strong, rock-oriented rhythm section and haunting themes. It is widely considered one of the finest examples of the genre's artistic peak. |
| Bread | On the Waters | 1970 | While often categorized as soft rock, David Gates' songwriting is deeply rooted in the folk tradition, featuring acoustic-led arrangements and earnest delivery. This album perfected the commercial folk-rock ballad. Manna is another essential title. |
| Loggins and Messina | Sittin' In | 1971 | Blending Jim Messina's country-rock guitar with Kenny Loggins' folk-pop sensibilities, this debut is a high-energy example of 70s folk rock. It features prominent flute, fiddle, and rock percussion. Loggins and Messina is their other major work. |
| Nick Drake | Bryter Layter | 1971 | While Five Leaves Left was pure folk, this follow-up added a jazz-rock rhythm section (members of Fairport Convention) to Drake's intricate guitar work. The result is a lush, sophisticated folk-rock sound. Pink Moon returns to a stark, solo acoustic format. |
| Jackson Browne | Jackson Browne | 1972 | This debut defined the introspective, "Canyon" sound of folk rock, utilizing piano and acoustic guitar backed by a subtle, tasteful rock band. His lyrics are deeply personal and literate. For Everyman and Late for the Sky are equally essential. |
| Arlo Guthrie | Alice's Restaurant | 1967 | Following his father's footsteps, Arlo Guthrie combined talking-blues folk with a 60s rock sensibility and humor. The title track is a definitive counter-culture folk-rock narrative. Running Down the Road is another significant title. |
| The Flying Burrito Brothers | The Gilded Palace of Sin | 1969 | While leaning into country, this album's soul-inflected folk-rock harmonies and fuzz-guitar-meets-pedal-steel sound were revolutionary. It represents the "Cosmic American Music" branch of folk rock. Burrito Deluxe is their other major contribution. |
| Dillard & Clark | The Fantastic Expedition of Dillard & Clark | 1968 | A collaboration between Gene Clark and bluegrass pioneer Doug Dillard, this album is a pristine example of acoustic instruments (banjo, fiddle) driven by a rock pulse and rock-structured songwriting. Through the Morning, Through the Night is their other release. |
| The Ozark Mountain Daredevils | The Ozark Mountain Daredevils | 1973 | Combining Ozark folk traditions with a polished rock sound, this album features harmonica, slide guitar, and catchy pop-rock hooks. It is a more rugged, "backwoods" take on the folk-rock genre. It'll Shine When It Shines is another favorite. |
| Melanie | Gather Me | 1971 | Melanie Safka's powerful, idiosyncratic voice is the center of this record, which blends folk earnestness with gospel-rock fervor and pop hooks. It captures the theatrical side of the genre. Candles in the Rain is her other definitive folk-rock statement. |
| Linda Ronstadt | Heart Like a Wheel | 1974 | This album showcases Ronstadt as a premier interpreter of folk and country songs, backed by a top-tier rock band that highlighted the genre's melodic strengths. Her powerful vocals brought folk-rock to stadium levels. Hasten Down the Wind is another key record. |
| Leon Russell | Leon Russell | 1970 | Russell blended Oklahoma "dust bowl" folk with swampy rock and roll and gospel piano. The result is a gritty, rhythmic version of folk rock that feels both ancient and modern. And the Shelter People is also essential. |
| Harry Chapin | Heads & Tales | 1972 | Known for his long, narrative story-songs, Chapin used a folk-rock foundation of acoustic guitar and cello, supported by rock drums and bass. His music is cinematic and intensely lyrical. Sniper and Other Love Songs is another major work. |
| The Dillards | Wheatstraw Suite | 1968 | This album was a turning point where a traditional bluegrass band adopted rock drums, orchestras, and pop structures. It is a foundational text for the "progressive" side of folk rock. Copperfields continued this innovative direction. |
| New Riders of the Purple Sage | New Riders of the Purple Sage | 1971 | Often associated with the Grateful Dead, this band focused on a cleaner, more direct country-folk-rock sound featuring Jerry Garcia's pedal steel. The music is characterized by outlaw themes and breezy tempos. Powerglide is another key release. |
| Pure Prairie League | Bustin' Out | 1972 | This album features lush vocal harmonies and a blend of acoustic and electric guitars, creating a smooth "Midwest" folk-rock sound. It bridges the gap between the folk revival and the "AOR" rock of the late 70s. Their self-titled debut is also representative. |
| Seals and Crofts | Summer Breeze | 1972 | Utilizing mandolin and acoustic guitar as lead instruments within a soft-rock framework, this duo created a highly melodic and spiritual brand of folk rock. Their harmonies are intricate and airy. Diamond Girl is another major commercial success. |
| Jim Croce | You Don't Mess Around with Jim | 1972 | Croce's music features two-guitar interplay (with Maury Muehleisen) and a solid rock rhythm section, supporting his vignettes of blue-collar life. It is rhythmically driving yet essentially acoustic at its core. Life and Times is another essential record. |
| Dave Mason | Alone Together | 1970 | The ex-Traffic member's solo debut is a masterpiece of layered acoustic and electric guitars, featuring a soulful, folk-inflected rock sound. The production is rich, utilizing world-class session musicians. Let It Flow is a later, more pop-oriented folk-rock effort. |
| Traffic | John Barleycorn Must Die | 1970 | Returning to their roots, this album blends traditional English folk (the title track) with jazz-rock and soulful R&B. Steve Winwood's vocals and multi-instrumental talents anchor the sound in a rustic, organic place. Music in a Doll's House (by Family) is a similar stylistic relative. |
| Jethro Tull | Songs from the Wood | 1977 | A later peak for the genre, this album fully embraced "Folk Rock" with a heavy emphasis on mandolin, lute, and flute, all driven by a powerful rock backbeat and prog-rock complexity. It celebrates British folklore and pastoral life. Heavy Horses is the logical follow-up. |
| Roy Harper | Stormcock | 1971 | Featuring four long, sprawling tracks, this album pushes folk rock into progressive territory with intricate acoustic guitar arrangements and biting social commentary, backed by subtle rock orchestration. Flat Baroque and Berserk is another key release. |
| Michael Chapman | Fully Qualified Survivor | 1970 | A gritty, "northern" English take on folk rock, featuring Chapman's idiosyncratic guitar style and Mick Ronson's early electric guitar contributions. It is raw, bluesy, and rhythmically complex. Rainmaker is another essential early work. |
| John Martyn | Solid Air | 1973 | While leaning toward folk-jazz, the rhythmic pulse and use of echoplexed acoustic guitar create a unique "ambient folk rock" sound. The atmosphere is hazy, melancholic, and deeply soulful. Bless the Weather is another must-hear. |
| Sutherland Brothers & Quiver | Lifeboat | 1972 | This collaboration between a folk duo and a rock band resulted in a perfect "power-folk-rock" sound, featuring ringing guitars and soaring harmonies. They represent the polished, melodic side of the early 70s UK scene. Reach for the Sky is also notable. |
| The Roches | The Roches | 1979 | Produced by Robert Fripp, this album features three-part sibling harmonies and quirky, literate songwriting backed by a minimalist rock pulse. It is a unique, late-period folk-rock classic. Keep On Doing is another fan favorite. |
| The McGarrigle Sisters | Kate & Anna McGarrigle | 1976 | Blending French-Canadian folk traditions with a sophisticated singer-songwriter rock sensibility, this album is characterized by poignant harmonies and varied instrumentation (accordion, banjo, piano). Dancer with Bruised Knees is equally strong. |
| The Albion Band | Rise Up Like the Sun | 1978 | A "supergroup" of British folk-rockers, this album features a heavy, professional rock sound applied to traditional dance tunes and original songs. It is powerful, rhythmic, and intensely English. Battle of the Field (by Albion Country Band) is the more acoustic predecessor. |
| Gryphon | Midnight Mushrumps | 1974 | Starting as an all-acoustic medieval folk group, this album added electric bass and drums to their bassoons and recorders, creating a "progressive folk rock" sound that is both whimsical and heavy. Red Queen to Gryphon Three moves further into rock. |
| Janis Ian | Between the Lines | 1975 | While predominantly a singer-songwriter record, the jazz-inflected folk-rock arrangements and literate, social commentary place it firmly in the genre's tradition. The production is subtle but effective. Stars is another highly regarded title. |
| Shawn Phillips | Contribution | 1970 | Phillips blended folk, pop, and rock with an incredibly wide vocal range and intricate guitar work. His sound is atmospheric and often experimental, utilizing both acoustic and electric textures. Second Contribution is his most famous work. |
| Stone Poneys | Evergreen, Vol. 2 | 1967 | Featuring a young Linda Ronstadt, this album is a classic of the mid-60s LA folk-rock scene, blending sensitive acoustic ballads with a steady rock backbeat and Baroque flourishes. Stone Poneys (debut) is also essential. |
| The Seekers | Seen in Green | 1967 | This Australian group brought a polished, "clean-cut" folk-rock sound to the global charts, featuring Judith Durham's crystalline vocals and soaring harmonies. They represent the more conservative, pop-oriented side of the genre. Come the Day is another key release. |
| We Five | You Were on My Mind | 1965 | One of the earliest successful folk-rock bands after The Byrds, their sound was characterized by bright, optimistic harmonies and a clean electric rhythm section. The title track remains a genre anthem. |
| The Kingston Trio | Children of the Morning | 1966 | Late in their career, the architects of the folk revival attempted to modernize with this electric folk-rock album. It provides a fascinating look at the genre's transition from purely acoustic roots. |
| The Association | And Then... Along Comes the Association | 1966 | While often called "Sunshine Pop," their roots were in folk, and this debut features sophisticated multi-part harmonies and intricate arrangements over a folk-rock base. Insight Out is another classic. |
| Spanky and Our Gang | Spanky and Our Gang | 1967 | This band mixed folk-revival earnestness with jazz-pop and rock energy, creating a diverse and highly melodic folk-rock sound. They were known for their complex vocal arrangements. Like to Get to Know You is another major work. |
| The Grass Roots | Where Were You When I Needed You | 1966 | Originally a studio project for P.F. Sloan, this debut is pure 12-string folk rock in the Byrds' vein. They later moved into more soul-influenced pop-rock. Let's Live for Today is another key early record. |
| P.F. Sloan | Songs of Our Times | 1965 | The writer of "Eve of Destruction," Sloan's solo work is archetypal 60s folk rock: protest lyrics, Dylan-esque vocals, and a driving electric band. He was a central figure behind the scenes of the genre. Twelve More Times is also notable. |
| Barry McGuire | Eve of Destruction | 1965 | This album's title track is the definitive folk-rock protest song, featuring a gravelly, urgent vocal over a pounding rock rhythm. The rest of the album follows the gritty, electric-folk blueprint of the era. |
| The Critters | Younger Girl | 1966 | Blending the Lovin' Spoonful's "good-time" folk with high-harmony vocals and light rock arrangements, this album captures the lighter, melodic side of the folk-rock boom. |
| The Pozo-Seco Singers | Time | 1966 | Featuring Don Williams, this group specialized in a gentle, harmony-rich folk rock that leaned toward the burgeoning "country-rock" sound. Their arrangements are simple and effective. I Can Make It With You is another representative title. |
| Hearts and Flowers | Now Is the Time for Hearts and Flowers | 1967 | A cult favorite of the LA scene, they blended folk, country, and bluegrass with psychedelic touches and a steady rock beat. They were a bridge to the more famous country-rock bands that followed. Of Horses, Kids and Forgotten Women is their other release. |
| The Gosdin Brothers | Sounds of Goodbye | 1968 | Having played with Chris Hillman and Gene Clark, the Gosdins created a lush, harmony-drenched folk rock that is a quintessential example of the "Bakersfield meets Laurel Canyon" sound. |
| Ian & Sylvia | Great Speckled Bird | 1970 | The legendary folk duo "went electric" with this self-titled band project, creating a heavy, country-inflected folk-rock sound that influenced the entire Nashville-rock scene. Nashville is another key transition album. |
| Dion | Dion | 1968 | After his teen-idol years, Dion reinvented himself as a folk-rocker with "Abraham, Martin and John." This album features intimate acoustic guitar backed by a subtle, soulful rock ensemble. Born to Be with You is his later, Spector-produced masterpiece. |
| Tim Hardin | Tim Hardin 1 | 1966 | Blending folk, jazz, and blues with a fragile, soulful voice, Hardin's debut features a jazz-rock rhythm section that perfectly complements his poetic songwriting. He was a major influence on the folk-rock community. Tim Hardin 2 is equally essential. |
| Richie Havens | Mixed Bag | 1966 | Known for his intense, percussive acoustic guitar style and soulful vocals, Havens' debut features rock instrumentation that elevates his rhythmic folk into something more powerful and driving. Something Else Again is another key work. |
| The Blues Project | Projections | 1966 | While rootsy, this album blends folk, blues, and jazz with a loud rock energy. They were a key part of the Greenwich Village scene's shift into electrified music. Live at The Cafe Au Go Go is their other major statement. |
| The Pentangle (feat. Jacqui McShee) | Solomon's Seal | 1972 | The final album from the original lineup, it perfectly balances their intricate acoustic interplay with a refined rock sensibility. The vocal harmonies and rhythmic double-bass are hallmarks of the genre. |
| Five Hand Reel | For A' That | 1977 | A powerful Scottish folk-rock band that combined traditional Gaelic influences with a heavy, driving rock rhythm section. They were part of the "second wave" of British folk rock. Their self-titled debut is also definitive. |
| Jack the Lad | It's Jack the Lad | 1974 | Formed by former members of Lindisfarne, this band played a more traditional-leaning but still high-energy brand of folk rock. They emphasize humor, storytelling, and accordion-led rock rhythms. The Old Straight Track is another key album. |
| Horslips | The Táin | 1973 | The pioneers of "Celtic Rock," Horslips blended Irish traditional tunes with hard rock and progressive elements. This concept album is a landmark in the genre's development in Ireland. Happy to Meet... Sorry to Part is their debut. |
| Oysterband | Step Outside | 1986 | Representing the modern evolution of the genre, this album features a punchy, almost punk-inflected folk-rock sound with prominent fiddle and melodeon. They revitalized the UK scene in the 80s. Ride is another high-energy favorite. |
| The Waterboys | Fisherman's Blues | 1988 | A late-era folk-rock masterpiece that stripped away 80s production for a "Big Music" sound based on fiddles, mandolins, and acoustic guitars. It captures the spiritual, communal heart of the genre. Room to Roam continues this folk exploration. |
| Billy Bragg | Workers Playtime | 1988 | While often associated with solo electric-folk, this album features a full band, creating a "socialist folk-rock" sound that blends Motown rhythms with traditional folk balladry and protest lyrics. Talking with the Taxman About Poetry is another essential. |
