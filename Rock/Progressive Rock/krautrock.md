# Krautrock

Krautrock is the umbrella term for the diverse wave of experimental rock music that emerged from West Germany in the late 1960s and early 1970s, as a generation of musicians rejected Anglo-American rock conventions in favor of radical sonic experimentation rooted in electronic music, minimalism, free jazz, and the European avant-garde. The genre encompasses an extraordinarily wide range of sounds, from the hypnotic "motorik" beat of Neu! and the metronomic precision of Can to the cosmic synthesizer explorations of Tangerine Dream and Klaus Schulze, and the communal psychedelic chaos of Amon Duul II. Typical instrumentation varies dramatically but often includes synthesizers (particularly Moog and ARP models), electric guitar, organ, bass, drums, flute, and extensive use of tape manipulation and studio processing. What unifies krautrock is its commitment to forward-looking experimentation, its rejection of blues-based song structures in favor of repetition, drone, and trance-like rhythmic patterns, and its embrace of the recording studio as a creative instrument. The movement was shaped by the cultural context of postwar Germany, where young musicians sought to forge a new artistic identity untethered from both the country's recent past and the dominant Anglo-American pop tradition. Krautrock's influence has been immeasurably vast, providing foundational ideas for punk, post-punk, ambient, techno, and virtually every strain of electronic and experimental music that followed.

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Can | Tago Mago | 1971 | This masterpiece defines the genre through Jaki Liebezeit's "metronomic" drumming and immersive, avant-garde soundscapes. It perfectly balances structured funk-inflected rhythms with pure experimentalism; other essential works include Ege Bamyasi and Future Days. |
| Neu! | Neu! | 1972 | The ultimate blueprint for the "Motorik" beat, characterized by Klaus Dinger's repetitive 4/4 rhythm and Michael Rother's melodic, layered guitar lines. This minimalist approach influenced countless post-punk and electronic artists; Neu! 2 and Neu! '75 are also vital. |
| Faust | Faust IV | 1973 | Blending industrial noise, pastoral folk, and hypnotic drones, this album captures the collective's radical approach to studio-as-instrument. It features the definitive track "Krautrock," which oscillates between fuzzy chaos and rhythmic bliss; see also The Faust Tapes. |
| Kraftwerk | Ralf und Florian | 1973 | Before their full transition to pure electronic pop, this transitional album showcased the flute-heavy, organic-electronic hybrid sound typical of early Düsseldorf scene explorations. It bridges the gap between cosmic jamming and rhythmic precision; Autobahn and their self-titled debut are equally influential. |
| Amon Düül II | Yeti | 1970 | A sprawling double album that epitomizes the dark, heavy, and chaotic psychedelic side of the genre with long-form improvisations and communal energy. It represents the "Munich sound" at its most experimental; Phallus Dei and Wolf City are also essential listening. |
| Popol Vuh | Hosianna Mantra | 1972 | Shifting from early Moog experiments to a sacred, acoustic "Cosmic Music," this album utilizes piano, oboe, and tambura to create a spiritual atmosphere. It represents the meditative, ambient branch of the movement; Affenstunde and Einsjäger und Siebenjäger are other key works. |
| Harmonia | Musik von Harmonia | 1974 | A supergroup comprising members of Neu! and Cluster, creating a "perfect" blend of rhythmic propulsion and ambient electronic textures. The sound is incredibly disciplined yet atmospheric, pre-dating modern ambient-pop; Indie Free is another notable title. |
| Cluster | Zuckerzeit | 1974 | Produced by Michael Rother, this album moved the duo from industrial drones toward rhythmic, proto-electronic pop sketches. It defines the genre's ability to innovate within short, melodic structures using early synthesizers; 71 and Sowiesoso are also definitive. |
| Tangerine Dream | Phaedra | 1974 | While later known for New Age, this era utilized the Moog sequencer to create dark, pulsating, and evolving cosmic soundscapes. It is a cornerstone of the "Berlin School" within the broader movement; Zeit and Rubycon are equally significant. |
| Ash Ra Tempel | Ash Ra Tempel | 1971 | Featuring Manuel Göttsching's searing guitar work, this album consists of two side-long tracks that explore the extremes of "space rock" and ambient tranquility. It is the definitive heavy-psych-meets-cosmic-drone record; Schwingungen and Join Inn are also essential. |
| Guru Guru | Hinten | 1971 | Driven by the powerhouse drumming of Mani Neumeier, this album showcases the "freak-out" side of the genre with heavy, distorted blues-rock deconstructions. It is chaotic, humorous, and rhythmically relentless; UFO and Kanguru are other must-hear titles. |
| Agitation Free | 2nd | 1973 | A sophisticated blend of jazz-inflected improvisation, global folk influences, and electronic textures that avoids traditional rock structures. It defines the "cosmic" travelogue aspect of the West Berlin scene; Malesch is their other essential masterwork. |
| La Düsseldorf | La Düsseldorf | 1976 | Formed by Klaus Dinger after Neu!, this project injected a more anthemic, proto-punk energy into the Motorik rhythm. The tracks are expansive, optimistic, and highly melodic; Viva is another highly influential release. |
| Klaus Schulze | Timewind | 1975 | A pioneer of the Berlin School, Schulze used massive synthesizers to create sweeping, orchestral-scale electronic meditations. This album is a masterclass in evolving textures and atmospheric depth; Irrlicht and Moondawn are also foundational. |
| Conrad Schnitzler | Rot | 1973 | A radical departure from rock, this album uses purely electronic sound sources to create harsh, abstract, and highly original soundscapes. Schnitzler was a founding member of both Tangerine Dream and Kluster; Blau is the companion piece to this work. |
| Gila | Bury My Heart at Wounded Knee | 1973 | This album combines haunting folk-rock elements with psychedelic organ and spacey guitar textures to create a unique narrative atmosphere. It showcases the genre's ability to tackle heavy thematic content through experimental arrangements; their debut Gila is also vital. |
| Jane | Together | 1972 | Representing the "heavy" progressive end of the spectrum, Jane utilized thick organ textures and soulful, bluesy vocals over steady rhythms. It is a prime example of the more accessible, melodic side of the Krautrock era; Here We Are is another notable title. |
| Eloy | Floating | 1974 | A transition from early heavy-psych to full-blown symphonic space rock, featuring swirling keyboards and driving percussion. It defines the more "epic" and structured side of the German underground; Inside and Dawn are also high-water marks. |
| Embryo | Opal | 1970 | A fusion of jazz, rock, and ethnic world music that emphasizes communal improvisation and non-Western scales. This album highlights the genre's "global" curiosity and technical proficiency; Rocksession and Embryo's Reise are essential follows. |
| Xhol Caravan | Electrip | 1969 | One of the earliest examples of the genre, blending soul-jazz, flute-led psychedelia, and avant-garde noise. It captures the transition from 60s psych to the unique German "experimental" sound; Motherfuckers Live is another key document. |
| Mythos | Mythos | 1972 | This album features a distinctive blend of flute, acoustic guitars, and early electronic "Mellotron" effects, creating a dreamy, pastoral atmosphere. It is an archetypal example of the "Interstellar" folk-rock sub-strain; Dreamlab is also highly regarded. |
| Wallenstein | Mother Universe | 1972 | Led by keyboardist Jürgen Dollase, this album features complex, high-energy symphonic rock with a distinctively German, aggressive rhythmic drive. It represents the intersection of the "Cosmic Couriers" scene and progressive rock; Blitzkrieg is their other major work. |
| Cosmic Jokers | Cosmic Jokers | 1974 | Part of the infamous LSD-fueled jam sessions organized by Rolf-Ulrich Kaiser, this album is a pure, unedited exploration of space-drone and synthesizer textures. It is the ultimate "studio-jam" document of the era; Galactic Supermarket is the other notable release. |
| Manuel Göttsching | Inventions for Electric Guitar | 1975 | A solo tour-de-force where Göttsching used multi-tracked guitars to create hypnotic, proto-techno rhythmic patterns and loops. It redefined the guitar as a rhythmic, electronic-style instrument; E2-E4 (though later) is his other world-defining work. |
| Michael Rother | Flammende Herzen | 1977 | After his time in Neu! and Harmonia, Rother perfected a lush, melodic, and highly rhythmic style of ambient-rock. The album is characterized by its clean guitar tones and steady, driving percussion; Sterntaler and Katzenmusik are also essential. |
| Dämmerung | Darker | 1971 | A lesser-known but essential piece of dark, heavy psychedelia featuring brooding organ and distorted guitars. It represents the underground, slightly occult-tinged side of the movement; often sought after by collectors for its raw energy. |
| Gomorrha | I'm on My Way | 1972 | This album showcases a transition from heavy blues-rock to sophisticated, electronic-tinged progressive music with experimental vocal effects. It captures the restless innovation typical of the early 70s German scene; Trauma is their other key release. |
| Brainticket | Cottonwoodhill | 1971 | Though led by a Belgian, the band was based in the German-Swiss scene and produced this legendary "head" record featuring heavy Hammond organ and hallucinogenic spoken word. It is arguably the most "trippy" record associated with the movement; Psychonaut is also vital. |
| Krokodil | An Invisible World Revealed | 1971 | Often called the "Swiss Amon Düül II," this band blended sitar, harmonica, and heavy progressive riffs into a unique ethno-kraut hybrid. It defines the genre's obsession with blending the ancient and the futuristic; Getting Up for the Morning is also recommended. |
| Et Cetera | Et Cetera | 1971 | Led by Wolfgang Dauner, this is a prime example of "Jazz-Kraut," featuring frantic percussion, ring modulators, and avant-garde brass. It pushes the boundaries between free jazz and the motorik energy of rock; Knirsch is another essential title. |
| Witthüser & Westrupp | Trips und Träume | 1971 | The definitive "Folk-Kraut" duo, using acoustic instruments and German lyrics to explore psychedelic and mystical themes. It represents the pastoral, hippie-communal side of the movement; Der Bauer und der Reiche is another masterpiece. |
| Annexus Quam | Osmose | 1970 | An experimental jazz-rock album that emphasizes collective improvisation and unusual textures over traditional song structures. Released on the Ohr label, it is a hallmark of the genre's avant-garde fringe; Beziehungen is their second essential work. |
| Night Sun | Mournin' | 1972 | A rare example of heavy-metal-infused Krautrock, featuring blistering guitar work and a massive, distorted organ sound. It shows the influence of Deep Purple but filtered through the aggressive, experimental German lens. |
| Twenty-First Century | Twenty-First Century | 1972 | A project featuring members of various underground German bands, focusing on high-energy, brass-heavy progressive rock. It showcases the genre's ability to integrate diverse instrumentation into a cohesive, driving sound. |
| Message | From Books and Dreams | 1973 | A dark, atmospheric blend of heavy rock and progressive elements, featuring haunting vocals and intricate guitar-keyboard interplay. It captures the melancholic, "heavy" mood that permeated much of the Hessian scene; The Dawn Anew Is Coming is also notable. |
| Cornucopia | Full Horn | 1973 | A wildly experimental album that shifts between frantic rock, avant-garde noise, and orchestral passages within single tracks. It is a quintessential "one-off" gem that encapsulates the genre's total lack of boundaries. |
| Ibliss | Supernova | 1972 | Produced by Conny Plank, this album is a fusion of jazz-rock and heavy percussion with a distinctly "spacey" atmosphere. It highlights the importance of the producer in shaping the genre's signature sound. |
| Rufus Zuphall | Phallobst | 1971 | Known for their extensive use of flute and "clavinet," this band played a rhythmic, blues-based style of progressive rock. It is a key example of the genre's ability to take Anglo-American influences and make them distinctly "Teutonic." |
| Pell Mell | Marburg | 1972 | This album is famous for integrating classical music themes (like Liszt) into a heavy, organ-driven rock context. It represents the "Symphonic" branch of the German underground; From the Open Window is also highly regarded. |
| Fricke & Popol Vuh | Affenstunde | 1970 | Florian Fricke's debut, utilizing the massive Moog IIIp synthesizer to create prehistoric, organic-sounding electronic textures. It is one of the first "pure" electronic albums in the genre; In den Gärten Pharaos is its essential companion. |
| Sperrmüll | Sperrmüll | 1973 | A heavy, organ-driven rock album with bluesy undertones and sudden shifts into experimental territory. It serves as a perfect example of the "hard-rocking" side of the Brain Records roster. |
| Grobschnitt | Grobschnitt | 1972 | Combining humor, theatricality, and long-form symphonic rock, their debut features the epic "Symphony." They were the genre's most famous live act; Solar Music: Live and Rockpommel's Land are also legendary. |
| Gomorrha | Trauma | 1970 | An earlier, more psychedelic effort than their later work, featuring heavily processed vocals and experimental sound collages. It is a raw look at the genre's formative years. |
| Yatha Sidhra | A Meditation Mass | 1974 | A beautiful, four-part instrumental suite blending flutes, vibraphone, and gentle percussion into a spiritual whole. It is a landmark of the "Cosmic Folk" subgenre. |
| Eruption | Eruption | 1971 | A radical experimental project led by Conrad Schnitzler, featuring free-form noise and electronic improvisations. It is one of the most uncompromising documents of the Berlin Zodiak Free Arts Lab scene. |
| Zweistein | Trip - Flip Out - Meditation | 1970 | A bizarre triple-album of sound effects, processed voices, and musique concrète, representing the absolute furthest fringe of the genre. It is an artifact of pure, unbridled studio experimentation. |
| Limbus 4 | Mandalas | 1970 | Using a wide array of acoustic instruments (flutes, cellos, tablas) in a completely non-traditional way, this album is a masterclass in "Free-Folk." It captures the communal, anti-establishment spirit of the era. |
| Deuter | D | 1971 | Chaitanya Hari Deuter's debut is a multi-instrumental blend of flutes, sitar, and early synthesizers. It is widely considered the precursor to "New Age," but with the experimental grit of early Krautrock; Aum is also essential. |
| Silberbart | 43 Men in the Strategic Air Command | 1971 | A heavy, distorted power trio that pushed blues-rock into near-atonal, noisy territory. It is the genre's answer to heavy psych, characterized by frantic energy and raw production. |
| Sand | Golem | 1974 | A haunting, atmospheric album that blends acoustic folk with early electronic drones and "spectral" vocals. It is a unique, dark masterpiece that has gained a massive cult following in recent decades. |
