# Glam Rock

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| T. Rex | Electric Warrior | 1971 | Marc Bolan's masterpiece effectively invented the glam sound by blending boogie rhythms with space-age lyrics and heavy string arrangements. It moved rock away from hippie earnestness toward a pose of stylized, campy cool. Other essential titles include The Slider and Tanx. |
| David Bowie | The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust and the Spiders from Mars | 1972 | This concept album defined the "alien superstar" archetype, utilizing Mick Ronson's aggressive guitar riffs and Bowie's theatrical vocal delivery to create a blueprint for the genre's visual and sonic identity. Aladdin Sane and Diamond Dogs are also cornerstones of this era. |
| Mott the Hoople | All the Young Dudes | 1972 | Produced by Bowie, the title track became the anthem of the glam movement, merging Dylan-esque street poetry with anthemic, sing-along choruses. The album captures the transition from hard rock to the more flamboyant, self-aware glam style. Mott is another vital release from this period. |
| Slade | Slayed? | 1972 | Slade brought a working-class stomp to glam rock, characterized by Noddy Holder's raspy vocals and deliberately misspelled song titles that emphasized loud, rowdy fun. Their sound relied on heavy rhythmic "boot-stomping" beats and simple, catchy hooks. Old New Borrowed and Blue is also highly recommended. |
| Roxy Music | Roxy Music | 1972 | This debut introduced an art-school sophistication to glam, mixing 1950s rock and roll with Brian Eno's futuristic electronic treatments and Bryan Ferry's lounge-singer vibrato. It established the "Art Rock" side of the glam spectrum. For Your Pleasure and Stranded are equally influential. |
| New York Dolls | New York Dolls | 1973 | Merging the grit of the Rolling Stones with drag-queen aesthetics, this album provided the bridge between glam and the upcoming punk movement. The sound is raw, messy, and high-energy, defined by Johnny Thunders' distorted guitar work. Too Much Too Soon is their other essential glam-punk statement. |
| Sweet | Desolation Boulevard | 1974 | Transitioning from bubblegum pop to hard-edged glam, Sweet utilized high-pitched harmonies and crunchy guitar riffs to create radio-friendly but heavy anthems like "Fox on the Run." Their sound represents the genre's peak commercial polish. Sweet Fanny Adams is their other definitive heavy glam record. |
| Lou Reed | Transformer | 1972 | Another Bowie-produced landmark, this record took Reed's gritty New York street stories and dressed them in lush, glittery arrangements and cabaret-style irony. It remains the most commercially successful and "glam" moment of Reed's career. Berlin offers a darker, more theatrical take on similar themes. |
| Suzi Quatro | Suzi Quatro | 1973 | As one of the first female frontwomen in glam, Quatro's leather-clad image and driving bass-heavy rock proved that glam wasn't just for the boys. Her sound is stripped-back, aggressive, and deeply rooted in 50s rock and roll revivalism. Quatro is another essential high-energy follow-up. |
| Gary Glitter | Glitter | 1972 | Known for the "Glitter Stomp," the sound is defined by massive, double-tracked tribal drum beats and simple, shouted choruses designed for arena participation. While his personal legacy is tarnished, the music's influence on the rhythmic foundation of glam is undeniable. Touch Me follows a similar sonic template. |
| Alice Cooper | Billion Dollar Babies | 1973 | This album perfected "Shock Rock," a theatrical sub-sect of glam that used macabre imagery and vaudevillian stage shows paired with muscular, polished hard rock. It features lush production and hit-focused songwriting that defined 70s rock radio. School's Out and Welcome to My Nightmare are other pillars of his work. |
| Iggy & The Stooges | Raw Power | 1973 | While often cited as proto-punk, the Bowie-mixed production and Iggy Pop's hyper-sexualized, metallic persona align perfectly with the more aggressive end of the glam spectrum. It is a violent, glitter-smeared explosion of guitar-driven energy. Fun House is their earlier, jazzier exploration of high-intensity rock. |
| Wizzard | Wizzard Brew | 1973 | Formed by Roy Wood, Wizzard took glam into "Wall of Sound" territory, utilizing chaotic arrangements with horns, multiple drummers, and 1950s rock pastiche. It is one of the most sonically dense and eccentric records of the era. Introducing Eddy and the Falcons is another essential retro-glam tribute. |
| Mud | Mud Rock | 1974 | Mud were masters of the glam-era rock and roll revival, delivering catchy, danceable tracks with a distinct 50s greaser aesthetic filtered through 70s production. Their sound is characterized by tight vocal harmonies and upbeat, guitar-led grooves. Mud Rock Vol. 2 continues this high-energy revivalist trend. |
| Silverhead | Silverhead | 1972 | Fronted by Michael Des Barres, Silverhead specialized in a sleazy, high-fashion version of hard rock that leaned heavily into the peacocking nature of glam. Their sound is characterized by bluesy riffs and flamboyant, bratty vocals. 16 and Savaged is their other notable contribution to the sleaze-glam canon. |
| Jobriath | Jobriath | 1973 | Marketed as the "American Bowie," Jobriath's debut is a lavish, orchestral glam-opera that combined cabaret theatricality with progressive rock ambitions. Though commercially unsuccessful at the time, its campy grandiosity is quintessential glam. Creatures of the Street is his equally ambitious second album. |
| Cockney Rebel | The Human Menagerie | 1973 | Steve Harley's group brought a theatrical, almost European folk-horror vibe to glam, replacing the standard guitar-heavy sound with prominent violin and keyboard arrangements. The atmosphere is decadent, cynical, and deeply stylized. The Psychomodo is another masterpiece of their idiosyncratic art-glam style. |
| Hello | Keeps Us Off The Streets | 1976 | A later entry in the glam movement, Hello specialized in "Teenage Rampage" style anthems with heavy clapping beats and distorted, simplistic guitar hooks. They represent the genre's shift toward a more accessible, power-pop influenced sound. Their version of "New York Groove" remains a definitive glam moment. |
| Sparks | Kimono My House | 1974 | Combining operatic vocals, rapid-fire lyrics, and jagged guitar riffs, Sparks brought an eccentric, intellectual wit to the glam aesthetic. Their sound is incredibly tight, rhythmic, and characterized by Russell Mael's soaring falsetto. Propaganda and Indiscreet are other essential works of their glam period. |
| Be-Bop Deluxe | Axe Victim | 1974 | Bill Nelson's outfit blended the futuristic themes of glam with highly technical, melodic guitar playing that flirted with progressive rock. The album captures the movement's fascination with science fiction and dystopian cityscapes. Futurama pushed their sound even further into art-rock territory. |
| The Hollywood Brats | The Hollywood Brats | 1973 | Often called the British New York Dolls, this band played a fast, crude, and piano-driven version of glam that was far too aggressive for the charts of the time. It is a quintessential "lost" glam gem characterized by sneering vocals and chaotic energy. The album was later released as Grown Up Wrong. |
| Geordie | Hope You Like It | 1973 | Featuring a young Brian Johnson (later of AC/DC), Geordie delivered a heavy, blues-influenced glam rock that prioritized power and grit over art-school artifice. Their sound is defined by stomping rhythms and Johnson's powerful, gravelly delivery. Don't Be Fooled by the Name is their other core glam-rock effort. |
| Alvin Stardust | The Untouchable | 1974 | With his black leather gloves and ominous stage presence, Stardust took the 50s revival aspect of glam to a moody, almost gothic extreme. The music features echo-drenched vocals and minimal, repetitive guitar motifs that created a unique "space-rockabilly" atmosphere. Rock On contains his most iconic hits. |
| Arrows | First Hit | 1976 | Known for the original version of "I Love Rock 'n' Roll," the Arrows combined the glitter-stomp rhythm with sharp, hook-laden power pop. Their sound is bright, upbeat, and epitomizes the late-stage commercial glam sound. They are also notable for having their own glam-focused TV variety show. |
| Skyhooks | Living in the 70's | 1974 | This Australian group brought glam's theatricality to the Southern Hemisphere with satirical lyrics and colorful, costume-heavy performances. Their sound is a mix of hard rock and pop-sensibility with a distinctly cynical edge. Ego Is Not a Dirty Word is their other massive domestic success. |
| Milk 'N' Cookies | Milk 'N' Cookies | 1975 | An American group that leaned into the "fop" side of glam, delivering sugary, high-energy power pop with a campy, boyish charm. They are a crucial link between the glam aesthetic and the burgeoning power-pop scene of the late 70s. Their self-titled debut is their only full-length statement. |
| Doctors of Madness | Late Night Movies, All Night Brainstorms | 1976 | A darker, more avant-garde take on glam that incorporated electric violin and dystopian lyrical themes. They represented the transition where glam's theatricality met the bleakness of the early punk and post-punk movements. Figments of Emancipation is their other highly influential cult classic. |
| The Sensational Alex Harvey Band | Next... | 1973 | Harvey was a veteran of the music scene who used glam as a vehicle for theatrical hard rock, vaudeville, and storytelling. His "street-tough" persona mixed with elaborate stage costumes created a unique brand of "Gutter Glam." Framed and The Impossible Dream are other essential listening. |
| Sailor | Trouble | 1975 | Sailor offered a unique "Nickelodeon" glam sound, using mechanical keyboards and maritime themes to create a highly stylized, theatrical pop experience. It is glam at its most whimsical and art-directed. Their self-titled debut also features their signature nautical-glam aesthetic. |
| Heavy Metal Kids | Heavy Metal Kids | 1974 | Led by Gary Holton, this band combined Cockney swagger with hard-driving rock and roll, fitting into the same "street glam" niche as Mott the Hoople. The sound is raucous, piano-augmented, and full of personality. Anvil Chorus is their other major contribution to the genre. |
| Brett Smiley | Breathless | 1974 | Produced by Andrew Loog Oldham, Smiley was the ultimate "manufactured" glam idol, possessing a look that was almost hyper-feminine even by glam standards. The music is lush, baroque pop-glam with high-drama vocals. The album was shelved for decades but remains a definitive artifact of glam excess. |
| Steve Harley & Cockney Rebel | The Best Years of Our Lives | 1975 | After restructuring his band, Harley moved toward a more polished, anthemic glam-pop sound that retained his signature poetic eccentricity. The album features "Make Me Smile (Come Up and See Me)," one of the genre's most enduring hits. Timeless Flight followed in a similar ambitious vein. |
| John Kongos | Kongos | 1971 | This album utilized African tribal rhythms and heavy slide guitar to create a unique, stomping proto-glam sound that heavily influenced the "Glitter Beat." "Tokoloshe Man" and "He's Gonna Step On You Again" are foundational rhythmic tracks for the genre. It remains a singular, rhythmically driven masterpiece. |
| The Jook | Jook Rule! | 1978 | Though their recordings were mostly singles from 1972-1974, this retrospective captures their essential "aggro-glam" sound. They were a bridge between the stomping rhythms of Slade and the sharp, short-haired aesthetic of the early Mod revival and punk. Their singles are cult classics of the genre. |
| Zolar X | Timeless | 1982 | While released later, Zolar X were a Los Angeles glam fixture in the mid-70s, performing entirely in "alien" personas with a space-age, synthesizer-heavy rock sound. They represent the extreme end of glam's sci-fi obsession. Their archival recordings capture their 1970s peak perfectly. |
| Iron Virgin | Archers Looe | 1974 | A Scottish band that specialized in a very heavy, almost proto-metal version of glam rock. Their sound featured thick guitar riffs and high-pitched, screaming vocals, typical of the "heavy" glam style that would eventually influence 80s hair metal. Their limited recorded output is highly prized by genre collectors. |
| Hector | Lady Killer | 1973 | Hector played a rough-and-ready style of "junkshop glam" that emphasized loud guitars and rowdy, chant-along choruses. They were the epitome of the short-lived, high-energy glam bands that populated the UK club circuit. Their singles like "Wired Up" are definitive examples of the "glam-stomp." |
| Queen | Queen II | 1974 | Before they became global superstars, Queen were a premier glam-rock act, combining heavy metal riffs with multi-tracked operatic vocals and high-fantasy themes. This album is their most "glam" and theatrical, featuring the "White Side" and "Black Side" concept. Sheer Heart Attack is their other essential glam-era record. |
| Girl | Sheer Greed | 1980 | A late-era glam band that leaned into the "androgynous beauty" aesthetic, featuring Phil Lewis (later of L.A. Guns). Their sound was a mix of 70s glam flash and the harder edge of the New Wave of British Heavy Metal. Wasted Youth continued their pursuit of stylized sleaze-rock. |
| Hammer | Hammer | 1973 | Produced by Mickie Most, this band featured a heavy, rhythmic sound designed to capitalize on the success of Slade and T. Rex. It's a textbook example of the "commercial glam" sound of the early 70s, characterized by loud drums and fuzzy guitars. They are a staple of "junkshop glam" compilations. |
| Elton John | Goodbye Yellow Brick Road | 1973 | While a massive pop star, Elton's mid-70s period was peak glam in terms of stage costumes, eyewear, and theatrical production. This double album contains several glam-rock anthems like "Saturday Night's Alright for Fighting" and "Bennie and the Jets." Caribou also features his signature flamboyant glam-pop style. |
| Jet | Jet | 1975 | Not to be confused with the 2000s band, this group featured former members of Sparks and Roxy Music. Their sound was high-glam art-pop with quirky lyrics and sharp, crunchy guitar work. Their only album is a lost classic of the sophisticated UK glam scene. |
| Starz | Starz | 1976 | An American band that combined the power-pop hooks of Raspberries with the arena-rock staging of Kiss and Aerosmith. They represent the mid-70s American attempt to refine glam into a sleek, radio-ready hard rock product. Violation is another strong example of their polished glam-rock sound. |
| Kiss | Destroyer | 1976 | Kiss took the theatricality of Alice Cooper and the simple, anthemic song structures of Slade to their logical extreme with face paint and pyrotechnics. This album, produced by Bob Ezrin, is their most polished and "glamorous" studio statement. Alive! and Dressed to Kill are also essential for their glam-rock foundation. |
| Dana Gillespie | Ain't Gonna Play No Second Fiddle | 1974 | A former associate of David Bowie, Gillespie's glam era was marked by bluesy vocals and theatrical, often risqué, arrangements. She brought a mature, cabaret-influenced femininity to the movement. Weren't Born a Man is her other notable record from this high-glam period. |
| The Runaways | The Runaways | 1976 | Produced by Kim Fowley, this all-female group merged the "jailbait" aesthetic with tough, New York Dolls-inspired rock and roll. Their sound was raw, aggressive, and highly stylized, making them the premier female glam-rock icons. Queens of Noise is their other essential studio effort. |
| Chicory Tip | Son of My Father | 1972 | Chicory Tip were pioneers in using the Moog synthesizer in a glam-pop context, creating a bubbling, electronic sound that was still rooted in the glam-stomp tradition. They represent the genre's willingness to experiment with new technology for pop hooks. Their title track remains a landmark of early electronic-glam. |
| Cozy Powell | The World of Cozy Powell | 1974 | As one of the premier drummers of the era, Powell's solo work epitomized the "drum-heavy" nature of glam. His tracks were often instrumental power-houses built around massive, echoing beats that defined the genre's rhythmic core. "Dance with the Devil" is the quintessential glam-drum anthem. |
| Showaddywaddy | Showaddywaddy | 1974 | This band took the 1950s rock and roll revival aspect of glam to its extreme with teddy-boy outfits and vocal-group harmonies. Their sound is upbeat, nostalgic, and incredibly colorful, representing the lighter, family-friendly side of the glam phenomenon. Step Two is another high-charting example of their revivalist style. |
| Paper Lace | Paper Lace | 1974 | Known for narrative-driven pop songs like "The Night Chicago Died," Paper Lace used the lush production and rhythmic drive of the glam era to create storytelling pop. Their sound features the compressed drums and bright guitars typical of mid-70s UK production. And Other Bits of Material is their other primary release. |
