# Northern Soul

Northern Soul is a music and dance movement that emerged in the late 1960s in the industrial towns of Northern England, centered on rare, uptempo American soul and R&B records from the mid-1960s that had been commercial failures in the United States. The sound is characterized by a driving, four-on-the-floor beat, pounding snare drums, lush orchestral arrangements, and powerful, emotionally charged vocals rooted in the gospel and Motown traditions. Key venues like the Wigan Casino, the Twisted Wheel in Manchester, and the Blackpool Mecca became legendary all-night dance clubs where DJs competed to unearth the most obscure 45s from labels such as Motown, Okeh, Brunswick, and countless small Detroit, Chicago, and New York imprints. The genre prizes rarity and danceability above all else — many of its most iconic tracks were album deep cuts or B-sides by artists who never achieved mainstream fame. Instrumentation typically features driving bass, punchy brass sections, tambourines, and sweeping string arrangements that build to ecstatic climaxes. Northern Soul's enduring cultural legacy lies in its passionate collector community and its role in preserving a vast catalog of overlooked Black American music that might otherwise have been forgotten.

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Edwin Starr | Soul Master | 1968 | Featuring the stomping "25 Miles," this album epitomizes the high-energy, four-on-the-floor beat and gritty vocals essential to the Wigan Casino dancefloor. His other essential works include War & Peace and Involved. |
| The Tams | A Little More Soul | 1968 | This album captures the "Group Soul" sound with breezy harmonies and a mid-tempo "shuffling" rhythm that became a staple of the Northern Soul scene. Time for the Tams is another notable collection of their upbeat beach-soul style. |
| Dobie Gray | Drift Away | 1973 | While the title track is a ballad, the album contains the uptempo, Motown-influenced "Out on the Floor," arguably the most iconic Northern Soul anthem ever recorded. Gray's Sings for "In" Growers also features early floor-fillers. |
| Gloria Jones | Share My Love | 1973 | Though famous for the original "Tainted Love," this album showcases her powerful, church-trained vocals over the driving, orchestrated arrangements favored by UK soul fans. Her 1966 sessions released as Come Go With Me are also highly influential. |
| The Isley Brothers | This Old Heart of Mine | 1966 | A masterpiece of the "Motown Sound" that Northern Soul was built upon, featuring relentless snare hits and lush orchestral swells. Soul on the Rocks is another essential high-energy LP from their Tamla era. |
| Darrell Banks | Darrell Banks Is Here! | 1967 | This album is a masterclass in sophisticated Detroit soul, blending raw emotional power with the polished production of the Revilot label. His second album, Here To Stay, is also a cult favorite among rare soul collectors. |
| The Fascinations | Out of My Systems | 1967 | Produced by Curtis Mayfield, this album defines the "Chicago Sound" within Northern Soul, characterized by sweet girl-group harmonies and a bouncing, syncopated rhythm. Their various singles collected on Girls Are Out to Get You are equally vital. |
| The Velvelettes | The Velvelettes (Anthology) | 1966 | Representing the faster, more aggressive side of the Motown girl groups, their tracks like "Needle in a Haystack" provided the blueprint for Northern Soul's tempo. Their Motown Anthology contains every essential track used in the UK scene. |
| Chuck Wood | Seven Days Too Long (Single/Collection) | 1967 | While mostly known for the title track, this era of Wood's work features the frantic, brass-heavy production that fueled the "all-nighter" culture. Collectors often seek out his rare Roulette sessions for more of this high-octane soul. |
| Major Lance | The Monkey Time | 1963 | As a titan of the Okeh label, Lance's smooth vocals paired with the "Carl Davis/Curtis Mayfield" rhythm sections created the mid-tempo "shufflers" beloved in Northern clubs. Major's Greatest Hits and Um, Um, Um, Um, Um, Um are also definitive. |
| Jackie Wilson | Higher and Higher | 1967 | The title track is the quintessential Northern Soul "stomper," featuring a massive bassline and Wilson's unmatched operatic soul range. Whispers and Manufacturers of Soul are other key albums from his peak Brunswick years. |
| The Chiffons | Sweet Talkin' Guy | 1966 | This album features the title track's driving beat and distinctive New York "uptown" production, which became a favorite for its danceability and catchy hooks. He's So Fine is another hallmark of their classic girl-group sound. |
| The Impressions | Keep on Pushing | 1964 | Showcasing the sophisticated arrangements of Johnny Pate, this album's blending of gospel harmony and driving rhythms influenced the "cross-over" sound of the 70s. The Never Ending Impressions is also a core text of Chicago soul. |
| Frankie Valli | Timeless | 1968 | This solo effort includes "The Night," a track that gained massive popularity at the Casino due to its haunting melody and propulsive beat. His work with The Four Seasons on Inside You also features Northern Soul staples. |
| R. Dean Taylor | I've Passed This Way Before | 1970 | A rare white artist on Motown, Taylor's tracks like "There's a Ghost in My House" became iconic for their eerie atmosphere and relentless backbeat. Indiana Wants Me is his other primary contribution to the Motown/Northern canon. |
| The Elgins | Darling Baby | 1966 | This album is a perfect example of the "Motown B-side" phenomenon, where forgotten album tracks became UK hits due to their high tempo. The Motown Anthology for The Elgins covers all their essential Northern Soul dancers. |
| Billy Butler | Right Track (Single/Collection) | 1966 | Butler's "The Right Track" is a definitive Okeh stomper, featuring a joyous, building brass arrangement and a driving rhythm that defines the genre's energy. His work with The Chanters, like Soul Artistry, is also highly regarded. |
| Gene Chandler | The Duke of Earl | 1962 | Known for his suave delivery, Chandler's tracks on the Vee-Jay and Brunswick labels provided the scene with sophisticated, rhythmic dancers. The Girl Don't Care and There Was a Time are also essential Northern Soul documents. |
| The Artistics | I'm Gonna Miss You | 1967 | This album captures the Brunswick "Chicago Sound," utilizing soaring male harmonies and a distinctive, snapping snare beat that filled dancefloors. The Artistics Get My Mind Made Up is another high-quality soul collection. |
| Barbara McNair | Here I Am | 1966 | McNair's Motown recordings are prized for their "pop-soul" crossover appeal, featuring polished orchestration and a bright, uptempo feel. The Real Barbara McNair also contains tracks that became favorites in the Northern Soul rare-collecting circles. |
| The San Remo Golden Strings | Hungry for Love | 1967 | A rare instrumental entry, this album highlights the lush, orchestral "Wall of Sound" that characterized many Northern Soul backing tracks. Their Ric-Tic label recordings are fundamental to the Detroit soul sound. |
| Jimmy Radcliffe | Long After Tonight is All Over (Single) | 1964 | Though he never released a full Northern album, this specific track is the "closing hymn" of the Wigan Casino, defining the genre's emotional, epic atmosphere. His various 60s singles are collected on numerous Greatest Hits compilations. |
| The Flirtations | Sounds Like The Flirtations | 1969 | Their hit "Nothing But a Heartache" is a cornerstone of the genre, blending dramatic, soaring vocals with a thunderous, Spector-esque production. This album remains a high-water mark for the UK-produced "Deram" soul sound. |
| JJ Barnes | The Groovesville Masters | 1967 | Barnes was a hero of the Detroit scene; this collection features the gritty, mid-tempo soul and driving rhythms that defined the "Motor City" sound. Born Again is another notable title from his later career. |
| Tami Lynn | Love Is Here and Now You're Gone | 1972 | Including the iconic "I'm Gonna Run Away From You," this album features the punchy horns and New Orleans soul influence that resonated with UK fans. Her recordings for the Mojo and Cotillion labels are essential. |
| The Casualeers | Dance, Dance, Dance (Single) | 1968 | This group's output epitomizes the "over-the-top" energy and driving percussion of late-60s soul that became Northern Soul's trademark. Their rare singles are staples on various Northern Soul floor-filler compilations. |
| Marlena Shaw | Out of Different Bags | 1967 | This album features "Let's Wade in the Water," a track that defines the jazz-inflected, groovy side of Northern Soul with its massive bassline. The Spice of Life is another legendary album featuring her powerful, versatile voice. |
| Al Wilson | Searching for the Dolphins | 1968 | Featuring "The Snake," a narrative-driven stomper that became a massive club hit, this album showcases Wilson's smooth baritone over driving pop-soul beats. Show and Tell is his other major commercial success. |
| The Toys | A Lover's Concerto | 1966 | Known for merging classical melodies with girl-group soul, their tracks feature the rhythmic precision and bright arrangements that defined the "Golden Era". Their self-titled LP is a must-have for fans of the "DynoVoice" sound. |
| Edwin Starr & Blinky | Just We Two | 1969 | A rare duet album that highlights the high-energy chemistry and booming production of Motown's late-60s output, tailored for the dancefloor. Blinky's solo work on the Sunny and Warm sessions is also highly sought after. |
