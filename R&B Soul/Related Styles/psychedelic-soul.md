# Psychedelic Soul

Psychedelic soul is a genre that emerged in the late 1960s when soul and R&B artists began incorporating the experimental techniques, extended song structures, and mind-expanding aesthetics of psychedelic rock into their music. The sound is characterized by heavy use of wah-wah guitar pedals, fuzz bass, swirling organ and synthesizer textures, echo and reverb effects, and lush orchestral arrangements that create a dreamlike, atmospheric quality around traditional soul vocal performances. Pioneered by artists like Sly Stone, The Temptations under Norman Whitfield's production, and Curtis Mayfield, psychedelic soul expanded the boundaries of Black popular music beyond the three-minute single format into sprawling, cinematic compositions that addressed both personal and political themes. The genre distinguishes itself from psychedelic rock through its retention of soul's rhythmic groove, gospel-influenced vocal traditions, and funk-derived bass patterns, and from standard soul through its experimental production techniques, extended instrumental passages, and countercultural aesthetic. Psychedelic soul was a crucial bridge genre that directly gave rise to funk, blaxploitation soundtracks, and the progressive production innovations of 1970s soul and R&B.
