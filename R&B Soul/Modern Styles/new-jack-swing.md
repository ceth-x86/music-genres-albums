# New Jack Swing

New Jack Swing is a fusion genre that emerged in the mid-to-late 1980s, blending the rhythmic intensity of hip-hop with the melodic traditions of R&B and soul. Pioneered primarily by producer Teddy Riley, along with key contributors like Jimmy Jam & Terry Lewis and L.A. Reid & Babyface, the sound is defined by its use of heavy, swinging drum machine patterns—particularly the triplet-based 16th-note groove played on Roland TR-808 and TR-909 machines—layered with punchy synthesizer stabs, funky basslines, and gospel-influenced vocal harmonies. The genre introduced hip-hop's aggressive energy and production techniques into R&B, creating a danceable, urban sound that dominated pop charts from roughly 1987 to 1993. Vocally, New Jack Swing ranges from smooth group harmonies to assertive, rap-adjacent delivery, often within the same track. The style was deeply connected to fashion, choreography, and visual culture, making it as much a movement as a musical genre. It served as a crucial bridge between classic soul/funk and the hip-hop soul and contemporary R&B styles that followed in the mid-1990s.

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Janet Jackson | Control | 1986 | While Rhythm Nation 1814 (1989) is equally iconic, this Jimmy Jam and Terry Lewis production laid the blueprint with its aggressive, synthesized percussion and funky, industrial-edged syncopation. It bridged the gap between Minneapolis funk and the emerging New Jack sound. |
| Keith Sweat | Make It Last Forever | 1987 | Produced by Teddy Riley, this album's title track and "I Want Her" introduced the signature "swing" beat—a triplet-based 16th-note rhythm—that defined the era's dance floors. Sweat's whining, soulful vocals became a genre staple. |
| Bobby Brown | Don't Be Cruel | 1988 | This is arguably the genre's commercial peak, blending hip-hop production techniques with R&B melodies. Tracks like "My Prerogative" and "Every Little Step" perfected the use of the Roland TR-808 drum machine in a pop context. |
| Guy | Guy | 1988 | As Teddy Riley's own group, this self-titled debut is the "purest" distillation of New Jack Swing, featuring heavy "stabs," dense synth-bass, and gospel-influenced vocal arrangements. Other essential Guy works include The Future. |
| Al B. Sure! | In Effect Mode | 1988 | This album introduced a smoother, more "pretty boy" aesthetic to the genre, characterized by lush synthesizers and breathy vocals. "Nite and Day" remains a masterclass in New Jack production's softer side. |
| New Edition | Heart Break | 1988 | After moving away from bubblegum pop, the group teamed with Jam & Lewis to create a sophisticated sound featuring heavy swing beats and tight five-part harmonies. The album served as a bridge for members' solo careers like Bell Biv DeVoe. |
| Karyn White | Karyn White | 1988 | Produced primarily by L.A. Reid and Babyface, this album showcased the female perspective of the genre with powerful, assertive vocals over crisp, mechanical drum patterns. "The Way You Love Me" is a quintessential New Jack anthem. |
| Bell Biv DeVoe | Poison | 1990 | This album famously combined "mentality" of hip-hop with "smoothness" of R&B. The title track's jagged beat and aggressive delivery represent the genre's shift toward a harder, more street-oriented sound. |
| Tony! Toni! Toné! | The Revival | 1990 | Incorporating more live instrumentation than their peers, this album added a neo-soul precursor feel while maintaining the strict New Jack rhythmic structure. "Feels Good" is their most definitive contribution to the movement. |
| Johnny Gill | Johnny Gill | 1990 | Produced by both Jam & Lewis and L.A. Reid & Babyface, this album utilized Gill's powerhouse baritone to add gravitas to the high-energy swing beats. Tracks like "Rub You the Right Way" are peak New Jack floor-fillers. |
| En Vogue | Born to Sing | 1990 | This debut fused classic girl-group harmonies with the booming, quantized percussion of the early 90s. "Hold On" features the characteristic a cappella intro followed by a hard-hitting, funky breakbeat. |
| Today | Today | 1988 | Another Teddy Riley project, this group offered a youthful, energetic take on the genre. Their hits like "Him or Me" utilized the dense, layered synth patches and snappy snare sounds that Riley pioneered. |
| The Winans | Return | 1990 | This album is a landmark for "Gospel New Jack Swing," proving the genre's rhythms could work in a spiritual context. The collaboration with Teddy Riley on "It's Time" is a definitive cross-over moment. |
| Heavy D & The Boyz | Big Tyme | 1989 | Heavy D was the "overweight lover" who perfectly balanced rapping with New Jack Swing melodies. This album features the infectious "Somebody For Me," which highlights the genre's heavy reliance on catchy synth hooks. |
| Color Me Badd | C.M.B. | 1991 | This multi-racial group brought a pop-centric harmony style to the genre. "I Wanna Sex You Up" utilizes a slower, grittier swing beat that helped transition the genre into the early 90s. |
| Jodeci | Forever My Lady | 1991 | While leaning toward "Hip-Hop Soul," their debut is rooted in the New Jack Swing aesthetic with heavy, swinging drum loops and church-reared vocals. It signaled the genre's evolution into a more sensual, rugged territory. |
| Wreckx-N-Effect | Hard or Smooth | 1992 | Best known for "Rump Shaker," this album represents the late-stage New Jack sound where hip-hop elements began to dominate the R&B influences. The production is characterized by heavy saxophone samples and booming basslines. |
| Father MC | Father's Day | 1990 | This album is a prime example of the "Hip-Hop New Jack" hybrid, featuring a young Mary J. Blige on backing vocals. The tracks are built on classic funk samples rearranged into the New Jack "swing" grid. |
| After 7 | After 7 | 1989 | Formed by Babyface's brothers, this group delivered a polished, sophisticated version of the sound. Tracks like "Can't Stop" feature the signature L.A. & Babyface "galloping" percussion style. |
| Troop | Attitude | 1989 | This quintet focused on high-energy choreography and vocal precision. "Spread My Wings" and "Mamacita" utilize the classic "stuttering" snare rolls and bright synth stabs synonymous with the genre. |
| Blackstreet | Blackstreet | 1994 | As Teddy Riley's second major group, their debut refined the sound for the mid-90s. "Before I Let You Go" shows the transition from New Jack into a smoother, more mid-tempo R&B style. |
| TLC | Ooooooohhh... On the TLC Tip | 1992 | The trio's debut is a vibrant explosion of New Jack Swing energy, characterized by colorful fashion and "New Jack" beats with a heavy hip-hop edge. "Ain't 2 Proud 2 Beg" is the definitive track here. |
| Jody Watley | Larger Than Life | 1989 | Following her disco/pop roots, Watley fully embraced New Jack on this record. "Real Love" features the iconic André Cymone production style—a lean, mean, and highly syncopated dance beat. |
| Ralph Tresvant | Ralph Tresvant | 1990 | The New Edition lead singer's solo debut, specifically the hit "Sensitivity," perfectly captured the "mellow" New Jack Swing sound. It focused on smooth vocal runs over a rigid, synthesized drum machine backbone. |
| Michael Jackson | Dangerous | 1991 | Jackson hired Teddy Riley specifically to update his sound. Tracks like "Jam" and "Remember the Time" are the most high-budget examples of New Jack Swing production ever recorded. |
| Boyz II Men | Cooleyhighharmony | 1991 | Before becoming ballad superstars, their debut was heavily influenced by the New Jack movement. "Motownphilly" is an essential genre anthem, blending old-school vocal traditions with a fast-paced swing beat. |
| The Boys | Messages from the Boys | 1988 | This sibling group offered a "teen" version of the sound. "Dial My Heart" features the bright, quantized electronic drums and cheerful synth-pop crossover elements common in early New Jack releases. |
| Basic Black | Basic Black | 1990 | This often-overlooked group produced one of the most mechanically "perfect" New Jack albums. "She's Mine" features the heavy use of orchestral hit samples and the classic "swing" hi-hat patterns. |
| Hi-Five | Hi-Five | 1990 | Another teen-led entry, their debut featured the massive hit "I Like the Way (The Kissing Game)." The production is characterized by a light, bouncy swing rhythm and sugary R&B melodies. |
| Jade | Jade to the Max | 1992 | This female trio's debut helped close out the New Jack era. "Don't Walk Away" features the definitive late-era beat: a prominent bassline, heavy swing-time percussion, and catchy, layered vocal hooks. |
