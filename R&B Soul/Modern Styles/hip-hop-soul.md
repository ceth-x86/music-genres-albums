# Hip-Hop Soul

Hip-hop soul is a subgenre of R&B that emerged in the early 1990s, defined by the fusion of soulful, gospel-influenced vocal performances with the hard-hitting drum programming, sampling techniques, and urban aesthetic of contemporary hip-hop production. Pioneered by artists on labels like Bad Boy, Uptown, and So So Def, the genre replaced the polished synthesizer-driven sound of new jack swing with grittier, boom-bap-influenced beats, dusty soul samples, and a street-conscious lyrical sensibility. The sound is characterized by prominent breakbeat-style drum patterns, sampled soul and funk loops, prominent bass, and vocal performances that balance technical R&B skill with the attitude and rhythmic cadence of hip-hop culture. Hip-hop soul distinguishes itself from new jack swing through its rawer, less synthesized production and from neo-soul through its commercial orientation and emphasis on hip-hop beats over live instrumentation. The genre dominated mid-1990s urban radio and served as a crucial bridge between the worlds of rap and R&B, with many releases featuring rapper guest verses that reinforced the genre's dual identity.
