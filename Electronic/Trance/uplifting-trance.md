# Uplifting Trance

Uplifting Trance is the emotionally charged, euphoria-driven branch of trance music, engineered to produce the feeling of transcendence and catharsis through soaring synthesizer melodies, orchestral breakdowns, and massive climactic payoffs. Emerging in the mid-1990s from the Dutch, German, and Belgian scenes, with key architects including Ferry Corsten, Paul van Dyk, and the Rank 1 project, the genre established a signature structural formula: a driving, rolling bassline introduction builds through layered percussion into an extended melodic breakdown, which then resolves into a full-power "hands-in-the-air" climax where the lead synth returns over the beat. The iconic instrumentation centers on the Roland JP-8000's "Supersaw" waveform -- a massively detuned, layered sawtooth sound that produces the genre's characteristic wide, shimmering leads -- paired with TR-909-derived kicks and crisp, gated snare drums at tempos of 136 to 142 BPM. What distinguishes Uplifting Trance from its progressive and tech-trance siblings is its unabashed emotionalism and structural predictability -- the genre operates on the principle that anticipation and release, executed with precision, can produce genuine physical and emotional euphoria. The style became the dominant sound of massive trance events like A State of Trance and Luminosity, and has maintained a devoted global fanbase across decades. Modern variants incorporate vocal "anthem" structures and cinematic orchestration while purist producers continue to champion the classic Supersaw-and-breakdown formula.

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Armin van Buuren | 76 | 2003 | This album captures the transition from classic trance to the polished, emotive "Armada" sound, featuring soaring lead synths and high-BPM drive. It prioritizes melodic euphoria and orchestral breakdowns over tech-elements; other essential works include Imagine and Shivers. |
| Ferry Corsten | Out of the Blue | 2001 | Under his System F alias, Corsten defined the "Dutch sound" with massive, detuned sawtooth leads and poignant, simple melodies that create a sense of flying. This record is the blueprint for the genre's anthemic structure; he also produced the legendary Right of Way. |
| Tiësto | Just Be | 2004 | Representing the peak of his trance era, this album features long, cinematic builds and "hands-in-the-air" vocal hooks that define the stadium uplifting experience. It remains a masterclass in emotional pacing; his debut In My Memory is equally foundational. |
| Above & Beyond | Tri-State | 2006 | This album brought a deeper, more song-oriented approach to uplifting trance, utilizing lush string arrangements and melancholic yet hopeful chord progressions. It focuses on the "heavenly" atmosphere of the genre; they are also known for the Group Therapy era. |
| Paul van Dyk | Out There and Back | 2000 | A cornerstone of the melodic German style, this album utilizes fast, rolling basslines and shimmering, crystalline synth plucks to create a feeling of constant forward motion. It is purely focused on the energy of the dancefloor; Reflections is another key title. |
| Aly & Fila | Rising Sun | 2010 | As the masters of the "Egyptian Trance" sound, this duo utilizes incredibly high BPMs and exotic, minor-scale melodies that resolve into massive, bright climaxes. This album is a pure representation of the 140 BPM modern uplifting style; see also Quiet Storm. |
| Solarstone | Rain Stars Eternal | 2008 | A pioneer of "Pure Trance," Solarstone emphasizes clarity of sound and organic-feeling textures over aggressive processing, focusing on the beauty of the melody. This album defines the lush, atmospheric side of the genre; Pure is another essential follow-up. |
| The Thrillseekers | Escape | 2016 | Steve Helstrip's work is the epitome of the "classic" uplifting feel, characterized by warm pads and nostalgic, heart-tugging lead lines. This album serves as a modern tribute to the original 90s spirit; he is also famous for the single "Synaesthesia." |
| Rank 1 | Symsonic | 2002 | Known for their "power-trance" approach, this album features heavy use of the Roland JP-8000 Supersaw to create wall-of-sound climaxes. It is a technical benchmark for the genre's instrumentation; they are also essential for the Airwave sessions. |
| Push | From Beyond | 2000 | M.I.K.E. Dierickx crafted the "Belgian sound" here, using dark, brooding intros that explode into bright, shimmering minor-key melodies. This album contains some of the most iconic synth riffs in the genre; he also released the notable Strange World collection. |
| OceanLab | Sirens of the Sea | 2008 | This project perfected the "Vocal Uplifting" sub-style, combining ethereal female vocals with high-energy, cloud-like synth arrangements. It defines the genre's ability to be both delicate and powerful; it is the primary full-length collaboration for Justine Suissa. |
| John O'Callaghan | Never Fade Away | 2009 | Representing the harder, more driving Irish uplifting style, this album features aggressive percussion paired with surprisingly delicate, soaring melodies. It is the gold standard for high-energy trance; Subculture is his other definitive body of work. |
| Airscape | Now & Then | 2010 | Johan Gielen's project focuses on the "Big Room" side of uplifting trance, featuring massive, gated reverb snares and bright, optimistic lead hooks. It captures the festival-scale energy of the early 2000s; he also contributed heavily to the Svenson & Gielen catalog. |
| Giuseppe Ottaviani | Magenta | 2013 | Known for his "Live On Air" approach, Ottaviani's music is characterized by crisp production and incredibly catchy, fast-paced Italian melodies. This album represents the "Nu-NRG" influence within uplifting trance; ALMA is another standout title. |
| Nu NRG | Freefall | 2004 | This duo brought an incredible sense of speed and rhythmic complexity to the genre, using bouncy basslines and sharp, acidic leads. It is one of the most energetic albums in the genre's history; they also released the influential Most Wanted compilation. |
| Daniel Kandi | Always Alive | 2011 | Kandi is the modern master of the "Anjunabeats" style of uplifting, using elegant piano breakdowns and very bright, clean synth layers. This collection defines the "sunny" side of the genre; he is also known for the track "Breathe." |
| Sean Tyas | Degenerate | 2016 | Tyas is famous for a "sharper" edge, utilizing heavy compression and technical synth work to make the uplifting melodies feel more impactful. This album showcases the intersection of power and emotion; he is also known for the Tytanium series. |
| The Noble Six | The Oracle | 2019 | This album represents the "Cinematic" direction of modern uplifting trance, featuring long, orchestral intros and massive, sweeping 140 BPM drops. It emphasizes the journey-like quality of the genre; his Meteor series is also highly regarded. |
| Gouryella | From the Heavens | 2016 | Though a collaboration (originally Tiësto and Corsten), this album features the modern revival led by Ferry Corsten, focusing on "celestial" themes and choral-backed melodies. It is the purest "Heavenly Trance" record available; see also the early Gouryella singles. |
| Masters & Nickson | 5th Dimension | 2003 | This project defines the "Deep-Uplifting" crossover, where the BPM is high but the mood is introspective and melancholic. It uses layered, swirling textures to create a sense of vast space; they are best known for the seminal track "Out There." |
| Smith & Pledger | Anjunabeats Worldwide 01 | 2006 | While a mix/compilation, it serves as the definitive statement of their production style: lush, wide-stereo synths and emotional chord changes. They were instrumental in the mid-2000s uplifting boom; see also their work under the Aspekt alias. |
| Simon Patterson | Open Up | 2014 | Patterson blends "Psy-Trance" rhythms with massive "Uplifting" melodies, creating a hybrid that is much more aggressive than standard fare. This album captures the "tech-lifting" energy perfectly; he is also known for the VII collective works. |
| Roger Shah | Songbook | 2008 | As Sunlounger, Shah created the "Balearic Uplifting" sound, incorporating Spanish guitars and chilled-out atmospheres into a high-BPM trance framework. It represents the "sunset" side of the genre; Another Day on the Terrace is also essential. |
| Arctic Moon | Stardust | 2012 | This artist focuses on the "Epic" style, featuring huge, echoing lead synths and heavy, driving kicks that dominate the mix. The album is designed for maximum impact in large arenas; he is also known for his remix of "True Bliss." |
| Factor B | Theatre of the Mind | 2021 | A modern purist, Factor B uses 90s-inspired synth patches and very long, emotional breakdowns to recapture the original "trance" feeling. This album is a testament to the genre's longevity; his Sea of Sea project is also notable. |
| Super8 & Tab | Empire | 2010 | This duo perfected the "Finnish" sound: clinical, high-tech production mixed with deeply soulful and soaring vocal melodies. It represents the polished, professional side of modern uplifting; their debut Helsinki Scorchin' is a classic. |
| Will Atkinson | Last King of Scotland | 2020 | Atkinson is known for pushing the boundaries of uplifting trance with experimental sounds and high-velocity percussion. This album shows the genre's ability to be avant-garde while remaining euphoric; he is also a key figure in the Victims label. |
| Suncatcher | The Best of Enriched | 2014 | This album is the gold standard for "Light" uplifting trance, featuring bouncy, clean rhythms and very melodic, happy lead synths. It avoids all darkness in favor of pure positivity; he is often associated with the Always Alive label sound. |
| Miroslav Vrlik | Hyperfocus | 2019 | Vrlik represents the "Solid State" of uplifting trance, with incredibly consistent, high-quality 138 BPM production that follows the genre's rules perfectly. It is a great example of modern genre-purity; Evolution is another of his key works. |
| Craig Connelly | Believe | 2017 | This album focuses on "Power-Vocals" and massive, driving energy, making it a favorite for modern trance festivals. It balances pop-sensibilities with traditional trance structures; his follow-up A Head Full of Dreams is also mandatory listening. |
