# Drum and Bass

Drum and Bass is a fast-tempo electronic genre born in the UK in the early-to-mid 1990s, evolving from the breakbeat hardcore and jungle scenes into a distinct form defined by rapid, syncopated breakbeats (typically 160-180 BPM) and deep, rolling sub-bass frequencies. The genre's rhythmic foundation lies in the manipulation of sampled breaks -- most iconically the Amen break -- which are chopped, time-stretched, and layered into complex percussive patterns that range from liquid and soulful to dark and aggressive. Sub-styles span an enormous range: the jazz-inflected elegance of Liquid Funk, the clinical precision of Techstep, the distorted synthesis of Neurofunk, and the anthemic energy of Jump-Up. Key instruments and tools include software samplers, hardware drum machines, Reese-style basslines derived from analog synthesizers, and extensive digital signal processing. The genre's epicenters have historically been London, Bristol, and the Midlands, with labels like Metalheadz, Hospital Records, and Ram Records shaping successive eras. Drum and Bass remains one of electronic music's most technically demanding and rhythmically sophisticated genres, with an unbroken lineage from the pirate radio culture of the 1990s to the global festival circuit of today.

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Goldie | Timeless | 1995 | A landmark release that merged complex, chopped breakbeats with soulful, symphonic textures and ambient soundscapes. Other essential works include Saturnz Return and the Inner City Life EP. |
| LTJ Bukem | Logical Progression | 1996 | This compilation defined the "Atmospheric" or "Intelligent" Drum and Bass sound, prioritizing lush synths and jazzy vibes over aggression. Bukem's Journey Inwards also explores these deep, melodic territories. |
| Roni Size / Reprazent | New Forms | 1997 | It bridged the gap between electronic production and live jazz instrumentation, featuring double bass lines and organic percussion. Size is also known for In the Møde and his work with the Full Cycle crew. |
| Adam F | Colours | 1997 | A masterclass in sophisticated production, blending hip-hop influences and jazz-fusion elements with crisp jungle rhythms. His tracks "Circles" and "Metropolis" remain foundational blueprints for the genre's melodic side. |
| Photek | Modus Operandi | 1997 | Renowned for its clinical, samurai-precise drum programming and minimalist, cinematic tension. Other essential Photek projects include Form & Function and the Natural Born Killer EP. |
| Dillinja | Cybotron | 2001 | Representing the "Valve" sound, this album focuses on earth-shattering sub-bass and raw, rolling breakbeats designed for massive sound systems. Dillinja's My Sound collection is equally vital for understanding D&B bass architecture. |
| Bad Company UK | Shot Down on Safari | 2000 | This album solidified the "Neurofunk" aesthetic with its dark, tech-driven synths and aggressive, mechanical rhythms. Their debut Inside the Machine is also considered a pillar of the late-90s tech-step movement. |
| Ed Rush & Optical | Wormhole | 1998 | The definitive "Techstep" album, replacing the soul of early jungle with eerie, sci-fi atmospheres and distorted, modulated basslines. The duo also produced the influential The Creeps and Travel the Galaxy. |
| High Contrast | True Colours | 2002 | A cornerstone of the "Liquid Funk" subgenre, characterized by uplifting melodies, disco samples, and high-energy soulful vocals. His follow-up High Society further refined this accessible, club-friendly sound. |
| Pendulum | Hold Your Colour | 2005 | This album brought a rock-infused energy and polished production that propelled D&B into the mainstream consciousness. While later albums moved toward electronic rock, this remains their pure D&B magnum opus. |
| Konflict | The Nightmare Continue EP | 1999 | Though a shorter release, it defined the hard-edged, sterile, and frantic "Neuro" sound that influenced a generation of producers. The tracks "Messiah" and "Rashomon" are archetypal examples of technical aggression. |
| Source Direct | Exorcise the Demons | 1999 | Known for a dark, brooding, and highly intricate style of breakbeat manipulation that feels both haunting and cinematic. Their Controlled Developments era tracks are also essential for fans of "Darkside" D&B. |
| Calibre | Musique Concrete | 2002 | Often cited as the king of Liquid D&B, Calibre's sound is defined by its understated elegance, deep bass, and emotive piano loops. Second Sun and Shelflife are other mandatory listens in his massive catalog. |
| Noisia | Split the Atom | 2010 | Showcases the pinnacle of sound design, featuring distorted "Reese" basslines and incredibly punchy, complex percussion. The Dutch trio also pushed boundaries with Outer Edges and various high-profile remixes. |
| Marcus Intalex | 21 | 2011 | A deep, soul-drenched journey that balanced techno-inspired repetition with the warmth of classic Drum and Bass. His work as M.I.S.T. and tracks like "How You Make Me Feel" are foundational to the soulful movement. |
| London Elektricity | Billion Dollar Gravy | 2003 | A pioneer of the "Live D&B" sound, utilizing funky bass guitars, brass sections, and whimsical, jazz-inflected songwriting. The debut Pull the Plug also highlights this organic approach to the genre. |
| Dom & Roland | Industry | 1998 | A relentless exploration of the "Architecture" of Drum and Bass, focusing on industrial textures and heavy, rolling breakbeats. Chronology and The No-Files also showcase his mastery of the dark, techy sound. |
| Total Science | Advance | 2000 | This duo specialized in "Old School" revivalism, bringing back the heavy breakbeat edits of the jungle era with modern production standards. Their Mars Needs Total Science is another classic of the rolling style. |
| DJ Marky | In Rotation | 2002 | Introduced the "Brazilian Sound" to the global D&B scene, mixing bossa nova and upbeat Latin influences with fast-paced rhythms. His collaborative work on "LK" remains one of the genre's most recognizable anthems. |
| Spor | Conquerors & Conquerors | 2010 | A high-octane representation of modern Neurofunk, characterized by razor-sharp percussion and innovative synth modulation. Jon Gooch (Spor) also released the influential Caligo after a long hiatus. |
| Commix | Call to Mind | 2007 | Blended the aesthetics of Detroit Techno and minimal house with Drum and Bass, creating a sleek, stripped-back, and highly sophisticated sound. Their Re:Call to Mind album offers further variations on this vibe. |
| Break | Resistance | 2010 | Known for having the "crispiest" drums in the industry, Break defines the pure, no-nonsense rolling sound of modern D&B. His albums Symmetry and Another Way are equally revered for their impeccable mixdowns. |
| Alix Perez | 1984 | 2009 | A seminal work in the "Minimal" D&B movement, focusing on negative space, deep sub-frequencies, and melancholic atmospheres. He later explored darker, more experimental half-time sounds on Chroma. |
| Kemistry & Storm | DJ-Kicks | 1999 | While a mix album, it is the definitive document of the "Metalheadz" sound, featuring the dark, experimental, and breakbeat-heavy tracks that defined the mid-90s underground. |
| Black Sun Empire | Driving Insane | 2004 | The masters of the dark, sci-fi "Neuro" sound, utilizing cinematic intros and aggressive, dancefloor-focused drops. They continued this legacy with albums like Cruel & Unusual and Lights and Wires. |
| dBridge | The Gemini Principle | 2008 | As a member of Bad Company and founder of Exit Records, dBridge's solo work focuses on the "Autonomic" sound—half-time, minimalist, and deeply atmospheric. A Love I Can't Explain is another essential listen. |
| Sub Focus | Sub Focus | 2009 | Defined the polished, synth-heavy, and melodic sound of the late 2000s that bridged the gap between the underground and the charts. His Torus album further leaned into the big-room, electronic dance style. |
| TeeBee | Black Science Labs | 2000 | A pioneer of the Scandinavian "Tech" sound, focusing on intricate drum programming and cold, futuristic soundscapes. His collaborative albums with Calyx, such as Anatomy, are also genre-defining. |
| Shy FX | Diary of a Digital Soundboy | 2005 | Merges the roots of Ragga-Jungle with modern D&B production, featuring heavy reggae basslines and dancehall-inspired vocals. His early work like "Original Nuttah" is foundational to the entire genre. |
| Klute | Lie, Cheat & Steal | 2003 | A unique fusion of punk-rock energy, techno sensibilities, and melodic Drum and Bass. As the head of Commercial Suicide, Klute's albums like No One's Listening Anymore provide a distinct, eclectic perspective on the genre. |
