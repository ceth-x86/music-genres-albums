# Glitch Hop

Glitch Hop is an electronic genre that applies the microscopic, error-based sound design techniques of glitch music -- digital stutters, granular synthesis, bit-crushing, and buffer overflows -- to the rhythmic framework of hip-hop, creating a mid-tempo, bass-heavy style that is both technically intricate and physically groovy. The genre emerged in the early 2000s through producers like Prefuse 73, who pioneered the chopping of vocal samples into rhythmic confetti, and later expanded through artists like Tipper, The Glitch Mob, and Flying Lotus, who brought increasingly sophisticated sound design and heavier bass to the template. Typical productions operate at tempos of 90 to 115 BPM, featuring syncopated, boom-bap-derived drum patterns processed through digital manipulation, deep sub-bass, and intricate layers of synthesized textures that shift rapidly between timbres and spatial positions. What distinguishes Glitch Hop from conventional hip-hop production is the foregrounding of digital artifact as an aesthetic choice -- the clicks, pops, and micro-edits are not errors but the primary compositional material, creating a sense of controlled chaos that rewards close listening. The genre developed strong regional scenes in Los Angeles (the Low End Theory/Brainfeeder orbit), the Pacific Northwest, and Australia, and has close ties to the visual arts community through immersive audiovisual live performances. Glitch Hop occupies a unique position in electronic music, bridging the cerebral experimentalism of IDM with the bodily funk of hip-hop and bass music.

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Prefuse 73 | Vocal Studies + Uprock Narratives | 2001 | This album is a cornerstone of the genre, pioneering the technique of chopping hip-hop vocal samples into rhythmic, stuttering patterns. It perfectly bridges the gap between boom-bap foundations and digital deconstruction; other essential works include One Word Extinguisher. |
| The Glitch Mob | Drink the Sea | 2010 | Moving away from their early mashup style, this album defined a cinematic, stadium-ready version of glitch hop with heavy basslines and clean, precise digital edits. The group's signature "west coast" sound is also captured in their Drink the Sea (Part II) and Love Death Immortality releases. |
| Tipper | Forward Escape | 2014 | Known for unparalleled sound design, Tipper combines incredibly intricate, squelching textures with a distinct mid-tempo funk. While Broken Soul Jamboree is more ambient-leaning, this record represents the pinnacle of his "crunchy" glitch hop aesthetic. |
| Ed IT | Crying Over Prosper | 2007 | This record is a masterclass in organic-digital fusion, layering field recordings and acoustic textures over jagged, syncopated hip-hop beats. It captures the "EdIT" sound before his transition to the more aggressive The Glitch Mob style, seen also on Certified Air Raid Material. |
| Flying Lotus | Los Angeles | 2008 | While often associated with the broader "Brainfeeder" beat scene, this album's off-kilter swing and dense layers of digital grit are fundamental to glitch hop's evolution. Other pivotal works by Ellison include the more jazz-fusion oriented Cosmogramma. |
| KOAN Sound | Dynasty EP | 2014 | This duo brought a level of technical precision and funk-influenced bass that redefined the genre's production standards in the 2010s. Their ability to blend heavy "neuro" bass with melodic glitching is also heard on the Adventures of Kapwob EP. |
| Opiuo | Slurp and Giggle | 2010 | This album solidified the "funky" side of glitch hop, characterized by bouncy rhythms, heavy brass samples, and infectious, high-energy synth leads. Opiuo's subsequent work, such as Meraki, continues this high-octane, dancefloor-focused trajectory. |
| Machinedrum | Now You Know | 2001 | An early gem that showcased Travis Stewart's ability to weave intricate, microscopic edits into soulful, urban soundscapes. His exploration of rapid-fire glitching and syncopation can also be found on his later work, Vapor City. |
| Hudson Mohawke | Butter | 2009 | This neon-soaked debut combined maximalist synth work with aggressive, stuttering hip-hop drum programming. It helped usher in a more colorful, "wonky" sub-strain of glitch hop; other notable early releases include his Polyfolk Dance EP. |
| Savant | ISM | 2012 | Combining chiptune aesthetics with massive glitch-hop production, this album is a whirlwind of melodic complexity and rapid-fire rhythmic changes. Aleksander Vinter is incredibly prolific, with Alchemist and Overworld also serving as key genre touchstones. |
| Dabrye | Two/Three | 2006 | A quintessential "Detroit" take on the genre, featuring stripped-back, mechanical beats that emphasize the "glitch" through stark minimalism and dry textures. This followed his equally influential One/Three, which helped lay the groundwork for the instrumental hip-hop crossover. |
| PANTyRAiD | The Sauce | 2009 | A collaboration between Ooah and MartyParty, this album brought a sexy, R&B-influenced swagger to glitch hop rhythms. It defined the "purple" sound of the era, a style further explored on their follow-up, PillowTalk. |
| Mr. Bill | The Collaborative Endeavors | 2013 | Known for his educational approach to production, Mr. Bill's music is a showcase of hyper-technical IDM-style edits applied to a heavy glitch hop framework. His mastery of microscopic sound design is also evident on Apophenia. |
| Griz | Mad Liberation | 2012 | This album successfully fused live saxophone and blues-rock influences with heavy, glitchy electronic production. Along with Rebel Era, it helped define the "future funk" aesthetic within the glitch hop community. |
| Nosaj Thing | Drift | 2009 | Characterized by its atmospheric, ghostly textures and perfectly timed digital skips, this album represents the more emotive and minimalist side of the genre. His later work, Home, continues to refine this ethereal, beat-driven sound. |
| KiloWatts | Problematic EP | 2009 | A deep dive into the more experimental and IDM-leaning side of glitch hop, featuring complex rhythmic structures and crystalline sound design. Jamie Watts also explores similar territory in his project KiloWatts & Vanek. |
| Gramatik | Beatz & Pieces Vol. 1 | 2011 | This record exemplifies the sample-heavy, soul-infused glitch hop style that dominated the early 2010s. It blends vintage aesthetics with modern, crunchy drum programming, a theme continued in his Street Bangerz series. |
| Pretty Lights | Filling Up the City Skies | 2008 | This massive double album defined the soulful, melodic, and cinematic side of the genre, using extensive sampling to create a lush, nostalgic atmosphere. Smith's earlier work, Taking Up Your Precious Time, is equally fundamental. |
| Vaetxh | Libet Tones | 2011 | Representing the extreme "glitch" end of the spectrum, this album features incredibly dense, mathematical deconstructions of hip-hop rhythms. Rob Clouth (Vaetxh) also released the equally complex Cycotic EP under this moniker. |
| Eskmo | Eskmo | 2010 | This self-titled Ninja Tune release showcases a unique blend of found-sound percussion, soaring melodies, and intricate digital processing. Brendan Angelides' work as Welder also explores the more melodic facets of this sound. |
| Dimlite | This Is Embracing | 2006 | A quirky, avant-garde take on the genre that utilizes organic instruments and eccentric vocal snippets amidst a sea of digital hiccups. His album Prisms & Pyramids further showcases his idiosyncratic approach to "beat" music. |
| Teebs | Ardour | 2010 | While softer than many of its peers, this album's use of granular synthesis and "shuffling" glitch rhythms makes it a staple of the LA beat scene. Collections 01 offers more of his signature "warm glitch" aesthetic. |
| Glitch.Cool (Various) | Atomizer | 2020 | While a collective effort, this project represents the modern "Neuro-Glitch" movement, pushing sound design to its absolute digital limits. It serves as a definitive showcase for the genre's evolution into the 2020s. |
| Frequent | Dream State | 2017 | This album is a landmark in "Neuro-hop," characterized by liquid-like bass textures and hyper-clean, technical glitching. Frequent's sound is highly influential in the modern underground scene, also heard on his Ethereal EP. |
| Culprate | Deliverance | 2014 | Funded by fans to avoid commercial constraints, this album is a psychedelic journey of experimental glitch and organic instrumentation. Culprate's more aggressive dancefloor tracks can be found on his 5 Star series. |
| Blue Sky Black Death | Glaciers | 2013 | This album leans into the "Cloud" side of glitch hop, featuring slow, sweeping instrumental passages interrupted by sharp, precise digital artifacts. Their collaborative work with rappers often features these same glitchy foundations. |
| Spoonbill | Bungalow | 2016 | An Australian staple, Spoonbill uses "wonky" horns and organic whistles layered over complex, shuffling glitch rhythms. His albums Airborne and Zoom also showcase this whimsical, highly technical sound design. |
| Haywyre | Two Fold Pt. 1 | 2014 | Fusing jazz piano virtuosity with modern glitch hop production, this album brought a new level of musicality to the genre. His Dubstep Processor and The Voyage also highlight his evolution from glitch to more melodic electronic styles. |
| Zebbler Encanti Experience | Freakquency | 2013 | This project focuses on heavy, psychedelic glitch hop designed for visual-audio immersion. The duo's signature "inner-dimensional" sound is also captured on their End of the World EP. |
| Reso | Tangram | 2012 | While known for drum and bass, Reso's glitch hop tracks feature some of the most aggressive and precise "mechanical" edits in the genre. This album showcases his ability to apply high-tempo technicality to a mid-tempo glitch framework. |
