| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Merzbow | Pulse Demon | 1996 | A quintessential example of Japanese "harsh noise," this album utilizes extreme distortion and high-frequency oscillations to create a dense, layered wall of sound. It eschews traditional melody and rhythm for pure sonic texture, a style further explored in his massive works like Venereology and the Merzbox. |
| Masonna | Spectrum Ripper | 1998 | This album captures the chaotic, explosive energy of "harsh noise wall" and "power electronics" through hyper-distorted vocals and rapid-fire sonic bursts. Maso Yamazaki's work is characterized by its brevity and intense physical impact, seen also in Inner Mind Rocket. |
| The Rituals | The Rituals | 2005 | This project exemplifies the more rhythmic but still deeply abrasive side of experimental electronic noise, blending industrial textures with chaotic synthesis. It focuses on the manipulation of hardware to produce unpredictable, grinding sonic landscapes. |
| Whitehouse | Bird Seed | 2003 | A landmark in the "power electronics" sub-strain of noise, this album utilizes high-pitched frequencies and aggressive, transgressive vocal delivery. It defines the genre's interest in psychological discomfort and sonic assault, similar to their influential Dedicated to Peter Kürten. |
| Prurient | Pleasure Ground | 2005 | Dominick Fernow blends screeching feedback with buried, distorted vocals to create an atmosphere of claustrophobic dread. This release represents the bridge between industrial music and pure electronic noise, a theme continued in Frozen Niagara Falls. |
| Wolf Eyes | Burned Mind | 2004 | This album showcases "trip metal" and "noise rock" hybrids, using handmade electronics and horns to create a murky, suffocating atmosphere. They are masters of the slow-build sonic decay also found on Human Animal. |
| Hijokaidan | Windom | 1991 | Known as the "King of Noise," this Japanese group utilizes massive amounts of feedback and live-performance chaos to create a literal wall of sound. This record is a prime example of their "non-music" philosophy, also heard on King of Noise. |
| Government Alpha | Venomous Spark | 1999 | Yasutoshi Yoshida creates complex, multi-layered noise that emphasizes processed electronic signals and rhythmic static. The album is a masterclass in controlled chaos, a signature style also found on Alphaville. |
| Aube | Stellar | 1998 | Akifumi Nakajima specialized in creating entire albums from a single sound source; here, he uses luminous electronics to create shimmering, metallic noise. His "minimalist noise" approach is unique in the genre, also highlighted in Pages from the Book. |
| Hanatarash | 3: Hans-a-trax | 1990 | Led by Boredoms' Yamantaka Eye, this project is famous for its dangerous live shows and high-intensity, "scum noise" electronics. This album captures the frantic, cut-up aesthetic that defines the more aggressive end of the genre. |
| Incapacitants | As Noiseman | 1993 | This duo focuses on "pure noise," intentionally avoiding any musical elements or rhythmic structures in favor of sheer volume and frequency saturation. Their work, including Default Standard, is essential for understanding the physical power of electronic noise. |
| Yellow Swans | Going Places | 2010 | This album represents the "dreamy" yet abrasive side of the genre, blending rhythmic drones with soaring, distorted electronic washes. It provides a more atmospheric and textured approach to noise, also seen on Bring the Neon War Home. |
| Pharmakon | Abandon | 2013 | Margaret Chardiet uses contact microphones and synthesizers to create visceral, bodily noise that focuses on feedback loops and agonizing screams. This release revitalized the industrial noise scene, followed by the equally intense Bestial Burden. |
| Puce Mary | The Drought | 2018 | A modern masterpiece of "industrial noise," this album uses precise electronic sequencing and field recordings to build tension and sonic violence. Her work is characterized by a cold, clinical precision, also evident on Persona. |
| Vomir | Prohibit | 2009 | The definitive artist of "Harsh Noise Wall" (HNW), Vomir creates static, unchanging monoliths of sound that lack any internal movement. This album is a pure exercise in sonic endurance and total immersion. |
| The Gerogerigegege | Tokyo Anal Direct | 1990 | This project blends extreme noise with performance art and "shambolic" electronic outbursts. Their work often challenges the boundaries of what constitutes an album, a concept pushed further in Instruments Disorder. |
| Kevin Drumm | Sheer Hellish Miasma | 2002 | Transitioning from guitar to pure electronics, Drumm created a terrifying landscape of digital distortion and high-decibel drones on this record. It is a cornerstone of "laptop noise," alongside his work on Imperial Distortion. |
| C.C.C.C. | Rocket Plane | 1994 | This "Cosmic Coincidence Control Center" collective specialized in "psychedelic noise," using electronics to create swirling, space-oriented walls of sound. This album is a peak of the Japanese noise scene's more expansive side. |
| Karkowski / Xper.Xr | The Third Mind | 1994 | Zbigniew Karkowski was a pioneer of academic and digital noise; this collaboration features cold, clinical, and mathematically complex electronic textures. His solo work like Choice of Materials is also essential. |
| Bastard Noise | A Culture of Monsters | 2010 | Emerging from the "powerviolence" scene, this project utilizes "the caveman" electronics—custom-built oscillators—to create harsh, socially-conscious noise. Their sound is brutal and uncompromising, also heard on Our Earth's Blood. |
| Pedestrian Deposit | Fatigue | 2008 | This project blends highly technical, detailed noise with moments of quiet tension and acoustic elements. They represent the "compositional" side of noise, focusing on structure within the chaos. |
| Macronympha | Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania | 1995 | This group is known for creating dense, "junk noise" collages using metal scraps and heavy electronic processing. This album defines their thick, claustrophobic sound, also found on Intensive Care. |
| Richard Ramirez | Memorial | 2001 | A prolific figure in the Texas noise scene, Ramirez focuses on "static noise" and "industrial" textures. This album showcases his ability to create oppressive atmospheres through repetitive, grinding electronic loops. |
| Emptyset | Borders | 2017 | While leaning toward techno, this album uses procedural electronics and signal processing to create "structural noise." It focuses on the physics of sound and the architecture of the space it inhabits. |
| Ben Frost | By the Throat | 2009 | Frost combines massive electronic sub-bass with glitchy, predatory noise and organic samples. The album defines a cinematic, high-fidelity approach to the genre, also heard on A U R O R A. |
| Tim Hecker | Virgins | 2013 | Hecker processes acoustic instruments until they become unrecognizable electronic noise and "shimmering static." This album is an archetypal example of "ambient noise," bridging the gap between beauty and harshness. |
| Dave Phillips | Humanity is the Virus | 1995 | A member of the Schimpfluch-Gruppe, Phillips uses field recordings and electronics to create "actionist noise." His work is often thematic and confrontational, focusing on environmental and social decay. |
| Sutcliffe Jügend | When Purity Were Violate | 1995 | Closely related to the power electronics scene, this project uses minimalist electronic loops and visceral vocals to create a sense of psychological horror. Their sound is sparse but incredibly heavy. |
| Francisco López | La Selva | 1998 | Though often labeled as "field recording," López processes environmental sounds into dense "bio-acoustic noise." This album represents the "environmental" or "found" side of experimental electronic noise. |
| Pan Sonic | Kesto (234.48:4) | 2004 | This Finnish duo used custom-built analog oscillators to create cold, rhythmic, and skeletal electronic noise. This sprawling 4-CD set covers everything from "industrial techno" to "pure sine wave noise." |
