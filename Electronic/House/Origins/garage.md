# Garage

Garage is a broad umbrella term for several strains of house music that emerged from New York City and London in the late 1980s and early 1990s, rooted in the soulful, vocal-driven sound of Larry Levan's legendary Paradise Garage nightclub. The genre is characterized by swung, shuffled rhythms, prominent gospel- and R&B-influenced vocal performances, warm sub-bass, and jazzy chord progressions that distinguish it from the more mechanical pulse of Chicago house. New York garage, championed by producers like Masters at Work and Todd Edwards, emphasized lush orchestration and deep, soulful grooves, while the UK offshoot — known as UK garage or UKG — accelerated the tempo to around 130 BPM and introduced choppy, time-stretched vocal edits and syncopated two-step rhythms. Key instrumentation includes Roland drum machines, sampled orchestral stabs, organ chords, and heavily processed vocal snippets layered over rolling basslines. UK garage in particular spawned enormously influential sub-styles including 2-step, speed garage, and eventually grime and dubstep, making it one of the most consequential lineages in electronic dance music. The genre's emphasis on groove, swing, and vocal soul over stark minimalism gives it a distinctly warm, human quality within the wider house music spectrum.

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| The Kingsmen | The Kingsmen in Person | 1963 | Featuring the definitive version of "Louie Louie," this album captures the raw, unpolished energy and amateurish charm that birthed the genre. Its lo-fi production and rhythmic urgency set the template for thousands of basement bands; other essential tracks can be found on The Kingsmen Volume 3. |
| The Sonics | Here Are The Sonics!!! | 1965 | This album is the pinnacle of aggressive garage rock, characterized by Gerry Roslie's shredded vocals and overdriven, distorted saxophone and guitar. It pushed the boundaries of volume and ferocity for the era, a sound they continued on the equally vital Boom. |
| The 13th Floor Elevators | The Psychedelic Sounds of the 13th Floor Elevators | 1966 | While leaning into psychedelia, the foundation is pure garage grit defined by Roky Erickson's manic howling and the unique, rhythmic bubbling of the electric jug. It remains the bridge between raw R&B and acid rock, followed by the more polished Easter Everywhere. |
| The Monks | Black Monk Time | 1966 | Recorded by GIs in Germany, this album features a repetitive, percussive sound driven by an electric banjo and nihilistic lyrics. Its minimalist, heavy-handed rhythm was decades ahead of its time; the band's earlier Demo Tapes 1965 also captures this proto-punk essence. |
| The Seeds | The Seeds | 1966 | Driven by Sky Saxon's snarling delivery and Daryl Hooper's hypnotic, repetitive organ riffs, this album defines the "flower power" side of garage rock. The track "Pushin' Too Hard" is a genre anthem, and their follow-up A Web of Sound further explored this fuzzy, rhythmic template. |
| The Standells | Dirty Water | 1966 | The title track's iconic guitar riff and the album's overall cynical, street-wise attitude exemplified the tough "American Punk" sound of the mid-60s. They specialized in a tight, organ-heavy R&B style also heard on Try It. |
| The Music Machine | (Turn On) The Music Machine | 1966 | Known for Sean Bonniwell's distinctive baritone and the band's use of fuzz-tone bass, this album features a dark, moody, and highly disciplined take on garage rock. Their precision and gloom are also evident on the later The Bonniwell Music Machine. |
| The Remains | The Remains | 1966 | Often cited as the most technically proficient garage band, their self-titled debut blends soulful vocals with fierce, driving guitar work and sophisticated arrangements. Their live energy is better captured on Live in Boston, but this studio effort is a landmark. |
| The Shadows of Knight | Gloria | 1966 | Hailing from Chicago, they brought a harder, blues-based edge to the garage sound, centered around their hit cover of Van Morrison's "Gloria". The album is a masterclass in aggressive suburban R&B, a style they maintained on Back Door Men. |
| The Stooges | The Stooges | 1969 | This album stripped garage rock to its most primal, high-volume essentials, featuring Ron Asheton's wah-heavy riffs and Iggy Pop's confrontational vocals. It serves as the ultimate bridge to punk, a descent into chaos that intensified on Fun House and Raw Power. |
| The MC5 | Kick Out the Jams | 1969 | Recorded live, this album captures the explosive, revolutionary energy of Detroit garage-rock-turned-proto-punk. Its dual-guitar assault and high-velocity rhythm sections are peerless; their studio effort Back in the USA offers a tighter, pop-inflected version of this sound. |
| The Count Five | Psychotic Reaction | 1966 | Built around the Yardbirds-inspired "rave-up" sections, this album features distorted harmonicas and frantic tempos that define the frantic garage aesthetic. The title track remains a cornerstone of the genre's history. |
| The Troggs | From Nowhere | 1966 | Defining the British "freakbeat" side of garage, this album features the primal, suggestive thumping of "Wild Thing". Their sound is characterized by simple, heavy riffs and an unpolished vocal delivery, also heard on Trogglodynamite. |
| The Cramps | Songs the Lord Taught Us | 1980 | Revitalizing the genre through a "Psychobilly" lens, this album uses reverb-drenched guitars and B-movie aesthetics to return to the genre's dark roots. It is a masterpiece of camp and grit, followed by the similarly wild Psychedelic Jungle. |
| The Chesterfield Kings | Here Are the Chesterfield Kings | 1982 | Leaders of the 80s garage revival, this album is a meticulous recreation of the 1966 sound, complete with fuzz pedals and Farfisa organs. They championed the "back to basics" movement also found on Stop!. |
| The Fuzztones | Lysergic Emanations | 1985 | This album defined the "Grindhouse" garage sound of the 80s, emphasizing distorted Vox Phantom guitars and a gothic, psychedelic atmosphere. It is essential for the revival era, as is their live record Leave Your Mind at Home. |
| The Gories | Houserockin' | 1989 | A minimalist, bass-less trio from Detroit, they returned the genre to its most skeletal, R&B-influenced form. The production is intentionally crude, focusing on raw rhythm and soul, a sound perfected on I Know You Fine, But How Lonesome Are You. |
| The Mummies | Never Been Caught | 1992 | Representing the "Budget Rock" movement, this album features an intentionally "lo-fi" and abrasive sound played on cheap equipment. Their chaotic, organ-driven tracks are the definition of 90s garage punk, also seen on Tales from the Crypt. |
| The Cynics | Rock 'n' Roll | 1989 | Featuring Gregg Kostelich's searing guitar leads, this album is a high-water mark for the 80s revival, blending 60s sensibilities with 80s power. Blue Train Station is another vital entry in their discography. |
| The Sonics' Rendezvous Band | Strike Like Lightning | 1980 | Formed by members of The Stooges and MC5, this band carried the Detroit garage torch with high-energy, soul-infused rock and roll. Their anthem "City Slang" captures the genre's transition into the power-pop era. |
| The Oblivians | Popular Favorites | 1996 | This Memphis trio combined garage rock with gospel and blues influences, delivered with a dirty, distorted punk attitude. It's a raw, stripped-down experience also found on Soul Food. |
| The Makers | Howl | 1993 | This album is a fierce example of the Northwest garage sound, featuring frantic vocals and a primitive, stomping beat. They leaned heavily into the "mod" and R&B roots of the genre, also seen on The Makers (1995). |
| The Headcoats | Beach Bums Must Die | 1990 | Billy Childish's most prolific group, they utilized vintage gear to create a "Medway" sound that was stubbornly anti-commercial and raw. This record is a definitive example of their prolific, lo-fi output, including The Kids Are All Square. |
| The Nomads | Where the Wolf Bane Blooms | 1983 | This Swedish band helped spearhead the European garage revival, blending heavy 60s fuzz with a darker, more modern edge. Their influence on the "Scandi-rock" scene is immense, furthered by Outburst. |
| The Lyres | On Friday 13th | 1984 | Led by Jeff Conolly, this album is centered on the swirling, rhythmic sound of the Vox Continental organ. It is a quintessential 80s garage record that sounds like a lost 1966 gem, a feat they repeated on Lyres Lyres. |
| The Pandoras | It's About Time | 1984 | An all-female powerhouse that brought a fierce, aggressive energy to the Paisley Underground and garage scenes. The fuzz-drenched guitars and defiant vocals on this record are definitive; Stop Pretending is their other essential work. |
| The Dirtbombs | Ultraglide in Black | 2001 | Mick Collins (formerly of The Gories) fused garage rock with classic soul and funk on this album, featuring a unique dual-bass and dual-drum lineup. It's a heavy, groovy take on the genre, also explored on Dangerous Magical Noise. |
| The Detroit Cobras | Life, Love and Leaving | 2001 | This album consists of obscure R&B and soul covers played with a rough, garage-rock grit and Rachel Nagy's powerhouse vocals. It captures the genre's obsession with digging up lost musical history, a theme continued on Mink, Rat or Rabbit. |
| Teengenerate | Get Action! | 1994 | Hailing from Japan, this band played garage rock at breakneck speeds with maximum distortion, blurring the line with hardcore punk. It is a high-octane, chaotic masterpiece, as is their Savage! collection. |
| The White Stripes | De Stijl | 2000 | Before their global fame, this album captured the duo's core garage sound: minimalist, blues-driven, and rawly produced. It emphasizes the "red, white, and black" aesthetic and the power of a simple guitar-and-drum attack, also found on their Self-Titled debut. |
