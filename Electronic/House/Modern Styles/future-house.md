# Future House

Future House emerged around 2013-2014, pioneered by French producer Tchami, who coined the term to describe a style that fuses the deep, groovy foundations of house music with bright, metallic bass tones, crisp digital sound design, and pop-oriented song structures. The genre is built on a distinctive sonic palette: "pluck" and "stab" synthesizer leads with a sharp, bell-like or organ-like timbre, heavy side-chained basslines that create a pumping rhythmic effect, and clean, punchy percussion at house tempos around 126 to 128 BPM. Unlike the rawer aesthetics of deep house or the distorted aggression of electro house, Future House favors a high-gloss, polished production style that bridges underground grooves and mainstream accessibility. The Dutch electronic scene became a major hub for the genre, with producers like Oliver Heldens, Don Diablo, and Brooks developing sub-strains including "Future Bounce" -- characterized by even bouncier, more melodic lead synths. The genre achieved massive commercial success through festival circuits and streaming platforms in the mid-to-late 2010s, becoming a staple of the Spinnin' Records and Hexagon label catalogs. While its peak commercial moment has somewhat passed, Future House's influence persists in modern pop production and in hybrid styles that continue to blend its signature metallic textures with other dance music forms.

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Tchami | Year Zero | 2020 | As the pioneer credited with coining the term "Future House," Tchami combines soulful, gospel-inspired vocals with "metallic" basslines and deep house rhythms. This album showcases the sophisticated, church-organ synth textures and high-gloss production that define his sound; other essential works include the Promesses and After Life EPs. |
| Don Diablo | FUTURE | 2018 | This album is a masterclass in the "Hexagon" sound, featuring bright, staccato synth leads, futuristic sound design, and a mix of pop sensibilities with heavy club drops. It solidified the transition of the genre into a mainstream powerhouse; his later album FORVER and the Generations compilation are also seminal. |
| Oliver Heldens | The Albums | 2016 | While often releasing singles, this collection encapsulates the "Gecko" era sound characterized by "rubbery" basslines and a groovy, shuffle-heavy rhythm that bridged Deep House and Big Room. His work under the alias HI-LO explores darker territories, but his self-titled hits remain the blueprint for the genre's groove. |
| Lucas & Steve | Letters to Remember | 2020 | This duo represents the melodic, uplifting side of Future House, utilizing soaring piano chords and bright, brassy synth stabs. The album perfectly balances radio-friendly vocals with the energetic "bounce" that became a staple of the Dutch Future House scene. |
| Brooks | The Music (EP) | 2018 | Brooks introduced a more technical, glitch-influenced style of Future House, characterized by intricate synth modulation and rapid-fire melodic sequences. His collaboration-heavy discography, including Bite and Lynx, helped define the "Future Bounce" sub-movement. |
| Mike Williams | Make You Mine (EP) | 2020 | Known for his signature "bouncy" leads, Williams' work focuses on highly melodic, major-key synth lines and clean, punchy percussion. His tracks are archetypal examples of how the genre adapted festival-sized energy for a house tempo; other key tracks are found on his Chill and Club collections. |
| Curbi | Dash EP | 2017 | Curbi's sound is distinct for its metallic, "crunchy" bass textures and eccentric, high-energy melodic patterns that border on Bass House but retain the House structure. This release highlights his ability to blend dark atmospheres with frantic, danceable rhythms. |
| Mesto | Messing Around (EP) | 2020 | Mesto is a key figure in the "Future Bounce" evolution, utilizing "pluck" synths and a distinctive swinging rhythm. His production is characterized by its minimalism and extreme clarity, focusing on a singular, infectious hook. |
| Madison Mars | Pulse (EP) | 2018 | Mars specializes in a "spacey," atmospheric version of Future House, often featuring lush pads and shimmering synth leads. His style is slightly more "cosmic" than his peers, as heard on his various New Life and Shuffle singles. |
| Pep & Rash | Rumors (The Remixes) | 2015 | This act was vital in the genre's early explosion, utilizing "wooden" percussion sounds and deep, resonant bass plucks. Their sound is more stripped-back and groove-oriented, focusing on the interplay between the bass and the kick drum. |
| Zonderling | Cluster EP | 2017 | Zonderling brought a quirky, almost progressive edge to Future House, using unique synth timbres and unconventional melodic structures. Their sound is often described as "clinical" yet incredibly groovy, influencing a more "intelligent" approach to the genre's sound design. |
| Chocolate Puma | Lullaby (EP) | 2016 | These veterans adapted their house roots to the "Future" sound by incorporating industrial-tinged basslines and complex drum programming. Their work often leans into a "tougher" sound that maintains the genre's characteristic metallic synth leads. |
| Sagan | Bouncers (EP) | 2018 | Sagan's tracks are definitive for their sharp, "chirp-like" synth leads and high-energy transitions. This release showcases the more frantic, high-BPM-feeling energy that many producers adopted during the peak of the Future House era. |
| Keanu Silva | King of My Castle (EP) | 2019 | Silva's style is characterized by a very clean, "polished" production style and a focus on soulful vocal hooks paired with driving, mid-range basslines. He often blends elements of Future House with classic 90s House aesthetics. |
| RetroVision | Get Down (EP) | 2018 | RetroVision is a leader in "Future Bounce," known for using video-game-esque synth patches and incredibly high-energy arrangements. His music is defined by its bright, neon-colored atmosphere and relentless rhythmic drive. |
| King Arthur | Believe in the Kingdom | 2016 | This artist focuses on a more "magical" and piano-driven side of the genre, utilizing organic instruments like trumpets and pianos alongside heavy digital bass. It represents the "House" side of the genre more strongly than the "Future" side, emphasizing song structure. |
| CID | Night Service Only | 2019 | CID brings a New York house sensibility to the genre, focusing on tech-influenced rhythms and shorter, punchier bass stabs. His sound is less about festival leads and more about club-focused, percussive energy. |
| Bali Bandits | To The Top (EP) | 2016 | Known for their incredibly unique and often bizarre sound design, Bali Bandits use "springy" synths and unpredictable melodic jumps. They represent the experimental fringe of the genre that values sound-texture over traditional melody. |
| Stadiumx | Metropolis (EP) | 2018 | Primarily known for Progressive House, their foray into Future House resulted in a "cinematic" version of the genre. They utilize wide, stereo-imaged synths and dramatic builds that resolve into standard Future House drops. |
| Matroda | The RED Tape | 2019 | Matroda sits at the intersection of Bass House and Future House, but this project defines the genre through its use of "dark" Future House basslines and rapid-fire percussion. His "Matroda Sound" is characterized by its heavy low-end and rhythmic complexity. |
| Raven & Kreyn | Singles Collection | 2019 | This duo is known for a "funky" take on Future House, often using slap-bass samples and disco-influenced rhythms. Their sound is light, energetic, and highly focused on the "shuffle" of the hats and the bounce of the bass. |
| Jonas Aden | Tell Me A Lie (EP) | 2019 | Aden's production is celebrated for its clarity and "weighty" bass hits. He often utilizes vocal chops as a primary melodic instrument, a technique that became a hallmark of the genre's mid-period. |
| Justin Mylo | Motown (EP) | 2019 | Mylo's sound is heavily melodic and often features "whistling" synth leads and light, airy atmospheres. He represents the "summer-vibe" side of the genre, focusing on catchy, simple hooks and driving beats. |
| Plastik Funk | Follow Me (EP) | 2019 | This act combines "G-House" grit with Future House structures, resulting in a "tougher" sound characterized by low-slung basslines and aggressive percussion. Their work often bridges the gap between the club floor and the festival stage. |
| Leandro Da Silva | The Album | 2020 | Da Silva incorporates a "tribal" and "funky" house influence into the Future House framework. His use of world-music percussion and Latin-inspired rhythms provides a unique, organic texture to the digital synth palette of the genre. |
| MOTi | The Future (EP) | 2019 | MOTi transitioned from Big Room to a hybrid style that uses Future House's "metallic" sound design within a more aggressive, high-impact structure. His tracks are designed for maximum impact in large-scale settings. |
| Mo Falk | Feel So Good (EP) | 2020 | A rising figure in "Future Bounce," Falk uses extremely intricate, "jittery" synth programming and a wide variety of foley-based percussion sounds. His sound is playful, high-energy, and technically dense. |
| Breathe Carolina | DEADTHEALBUM | 2019 | This group transitioned from Emo-pop to a polished Future House sound, bringing a unique focus on song-based structures and emotional vocals. The album features the characteristic "stutter" synths and clean bass drops of the genre. |
| Snavs | SS18 (EP) | 2018 | Snavs brings a Trap-music influence to Future House, resulting in a sound with heavy sub-bass and more complex, syncopated rhythms. This release demonstrates the genre's ability to absorb elements from other electronic styles while maintaining its 4/4 house core. |
| Dirty Swift | Future Anthems | 2017 | This compilation-style project highlights the "Mainstream" side of the genre, focusing on pop-remix aesthetics and the "standardized" Future House drop that dominated radio airwaves during the late 2010s. |
