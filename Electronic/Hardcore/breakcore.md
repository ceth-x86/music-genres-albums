# Breakcore

Breakcore is an extreme electronic music genre that emerged in the mid-1990s from the intersection of jungle, hardcore techno, and digital hardcore, defined by its use of hyper-edited, chopped, and distorted breakbeats — most iconically the Amen break — pushed to blistering tempos often exceeding 200 BPM. The genre's production aesthetic revolves around granular synthesis, rapid-fire sample manipulation, and the aggressive deconstruction of rhythmic patterns, creating compositions that feel simultaneously chaotic and meticulously programmed. Breakcore draws from an extraordinarily wide range of sources including classical music, metal, noise, gabber, ragga, and even anime soundtracks, with artists frequently employing plunderphonic techniques and absurdist humor in their sample choices. Key figures such as Venetian Snares, Igorrr, and Alec Empire established distinct sub-approaches ranging from orchestral complexity and "baroquecore" to industrial aggression and lo-fi noise. The genre is closely tied to underground DIY culture, thriving on netlabels, small-run vinyl releases, and intense live performances at squat parties and alternative festivals across Europe, North America, and Japan. In the 2020s, a new wave of internet-native artists like Sewerslvt and Machine Girl revitalized the genre by fusing breakcore with atmospheric textures, emo aesthetics, and footwork rhythms, introducing it to a new generation through platforms like Bandcamp and SoundCloud.

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Venetian Snares | Rossz Csillag Alatt Szuletett | 2005 | This masterpiece blends complex, frantic 7/4 breakbeats with mournful orchestral strings and horns, defining the "modern" breakcore sound. It showcases Aaron Funk's peerless drum programming; other essential works include Huge Chrome Cylinder Box Unfolding and Cavalcade of Glee and Dadaist Happy Hardcore Pom Poms. |
| Alec Empire | The Destroyer | 1996 | A foundational bridge between digital hardcore and breakcore, this album utilizes distorted 909 kicks and shredded breaks to create a high-velocity, riotous atmosphere. Empire's work here, and on Futurist, established the genre's early aggressive, anti-authoritarian sonic palette. |
| Bogdan Raczynski | Samurai Math Beats | 1999 | Raczynski combines manic, "math-y" drum patterns with nostalgic, almost childlike melodies and Braindance sensibilities. His erratic sequencing and sudden tempo shifts on this record, along with Boku Mo Wakaran, represent the genre's playful yet technical side. |
| Dev/Null | Lazer_Wrecker | 2006 | This album is a masterclass in "ravecore," utilizing old-school jungle stabs and hardcore techno tropes accelerated to impossible speeds. It captures the frantic, neon-soaked energy of mid-2000s breakcore; he is also known for the equally chaotic Bust Out EP. |
| Enduser | Form Without Function | 2006 | Mixing industrial textures and hip-hop sensibilities with punishing breakbeats, Lynn Standafer creates a cinematic yet brutal soundscape. This release, along with Pushing Back, exemplifies the "industrial breakcore" sub-style that found a home on the Ad Noiseam label. |
| Igorrr | Hallelujah | 2012 | Gautier Serre pioneered "Baroquecore" by meticulously slicing operatic vocals and harpsichords into extreme metal riffs and glitchy breakbeats. The sheer absurdity and technicality of this album, and its successor Savage Sinusoid, pushed the genre into avant-garde territory. |
| Shitmat | Full English Breakfast | 2004 | Henry Collins is the king of "mashcore," layering humorous pop samples and dancehall vocals over pulverizing, distorted Amen breaks. This record and Kill Everyone define the irreverent, plunderphonic spirit of the UK breakcore scene. |
| Bong-Ra | Bikini Bandits, Kill! Kill! Kill! | 2003 | This album introduces a heavy "Ragga-core" influence, fusing Jamaican toastings with massive, distorted jungle rhythms and B-movie aesthetics. Jason Kohnen's work here, and on Monolith, solidified the link between breakcore and heavy dub culture. |
| Ruby My Dear | Remains | 2012 | Characterized by lush, melodic arrangements and incredibly crisp, organic-sounding drum edits, this album represents the more sophisticated, "IDM-adjacent" side of the genre. Other notable releases include Brame and Balloon. |
| Doormouse | Stanley | 2003 | A Midwest hardcore legend, Dan Doormouse blends polka, folk, and noise with blistering, high-BPM breakbeats. This album and Freaked Out Mess are essential for understanding the American "distorto-breakcore" movement. |
| V snares (vs Speedranch) | Making Orange Things | 2001 | Representing the absolute extreme of the genre, this collaboration is a relentless assault of metallic, industrial noise and hyper-edited percussion. It is widely considered one of the harshest albums in the genre, alongside Speedranch's other abrasive works. |
| FFF | 20.000 Hardcore Members Can't Be Wrong | 2010 | FFF brings a purist jungle perspective to breakcore, focusing on intricate Amen break manipulation and heavy sub-bass. This release and Keep the Fire Burning highlight the genre's deep roots in pirate radio and rave culture. |
| Otto von Schirach | Chopped Meat | 2004 | Fusing Miami Bass with glitch and extreme breakcore, von Schirach creates a surreal, "alien" atmosphere filled with squelchy textures. His work on this album and Escalo Frio is uniquely theatrical and bizarre. |
| Drumcorps | Grist | 2006 | This album is the definitive fusion of grindcore and breakcore, featuring live-recorded metal guitars chopped into granular electronic rhythms. Aaron Spector's work here won an Ars Electronica award for its innovative genre-mashing. |
| Stazma the Junglechrist | Shapeshifter | 2017 | A modern torchbearer of the genre, Stazma utilizes acid-techno 303 basslines and aggressive, modern production techniques. This album and Smashed Head demonstrate the evolution of breakcore into the high-fidelity era. |
| Christoph de Babalon | If You're Into It, I'm Out of It | 1997 | A dark, atmospheric masterpiece that pairs cavernous ambient pads with sparse, violent drum-and-bass structures. This record, along with his Excrement EP, is the blueprint for the "darkcore" and "doomcore" overlap. |
| Sickboy | Swing Style | 2005 | This Belgian artist is known for high-energy, distorted rave-core that often incorporates swing and jazz samples. Swing Style and Musical_Diseases are cornerstone releases of the European breakcore festival circuit. |
| The Flashbulb | Flexing Habitual | 2004 | Benn Jordan's early work is a highly technical blend of jazz instrumentation and rapid-fire breakbeats. While he moved toward ambient music later, this album and Red Extensions of Me are quintessential melodic breakcore. |
| Kid606 | Kill Sound Before Sound Kills You | 2003 | A chaotic blend of glitch, techno, and breakcore, this album captures the "everything-at-once" energy of the early 2000s digital scene. Kid606's Down with the Scene is another essential example of his genre-defying approach. |
| Rotator | Dissident Sound Maniaks | 2007 | High-octane "Soundboy" breakcore that focuses on massive distorted kicks and dancehall-inspired aggression. This album and Jump Up and Die are designed for maximum impact on a sound system. |
| Ladyscraper | The Death of Civilisation | 2009 | Known for extremely dense, noisy, and chaotic arrangements, Ladyscraper pushes the genre into near-unlistenable levels of intensity. This record and Whore are the pinnacle of the "noisecore" breakcore sub-strain. |
| Pryapisme | Hyperion | 2012 | While leaning into "avant-prog," this album uses breakcore as its rhythmic backbone to jump between video game music, metal, and jazz. It represents the "maximalist" philosophy of modern breakcore. |
| Machine Girl | WLFGRL | 2014 | A pivotal bridge between the old-school breakcore sound and the modern internet "vibe" scene, blending footwork, jungle, and digital hardcore. This album and Gemini helped revive interest in high-speed breakbeat music for a new generation. |
| Goreshit | Semantic Compositions on Death and Its Meaning | 2011 | A leading figure in the "Lolicore" and "Mashcore" internet subcultures, Goreshit combines anime aesthetics with incredibly brutal, high-speed drum programming. This album and GNF are definitive of the DIY, Bandcamp-era breakcore sound. |
| Sewerslvt | Draining Love Story | 2020 | Representing the "Atmospheric Breakcore" or "Webcore" resurgence, this album uses heavy reverb and melancholic pads over rapid jungle breaks. It shifted the genre's focus toward emotional catharsis, seen also on We Had Good Times Together. |
| Xanopticon | Liminal Space | 2003 | This is the gold standard for "glitch-breakcore," featuring microscopic drum edits that feel like a precision-engineered machine malfunctioning. It is a cold, technical, and highly influential record in the IDM/Breakcore crossover. |
| Passenger of Shit | Putrid Phlegm Pile | 2006 | The extreme "Speedcore" end of the spectrum, featuring scatological humor and abrasive, distorted beats exceeding 300 BPM. This Australian artist's work is the absolute fringe of the genre's "shock" value. |
| Datach'i | Mauseum | 2004 | A technical marvel of modular synthesis and breakbeat manipulation. Joseph Fraioli creates an organic yet alien percussive language on this album and The Elements, bridging the gap between academic electronic music and breakcore. |
| Rykard | Arrive At Dawn | 2010 | A rare example of "chill" breakcore, focusing on cinematic atmospheres and slower, though still complex, breakbeat structures. It offers a meditative contrast to the genre's usual violence; Nightfall is also a key work. |
| Squarepusher | Go Plastic | 2001 | While often labeled IDM, this specific album is Tom Jenkinson's most "breakcore" statement, featuring hyper-processed Amens and aggressive synthesis. Tracks like "My Red Hot Car" influenced the genre's technical evolution immensely. |
