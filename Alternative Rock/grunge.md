# Grunge

| Artist | Album | Year | Why it defines the genre |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Nirvana | Nevermind | 1991 | This record perfected the "quiet-loud" dynamic, blending punk aggression with melodic pop sensibilities and angst-ridden lyrics. It transitioned the genre from an underground movement to a global phenomenon; other essential works include In Utero and Bleach. |
| Alice in Chains | Dirt | 1992 | Characterized by Jerry Cantrell's sludgy, down-tuned guitar riffs and Layne Staley's haunting vocal harmonies, this album explores themes of addiction and depression. The band's dark, heavy-metal-adjacent sound is also captured on Facelift and the Jar of Flies EP. |
| Soundgarden | Badmotorfinger | 1991 | Combining odd time signatures with Chris Cornell's soaring multi-octave range, this album represents the genre's more technical and heavy side. It anchors the "Seattle Sound" alongside their massive follow-up Superunknown and the earlier Louder Than Love. |
| Pearl Jam | Ten | 1991 | This album brought an anthemic, classic-rock-influenced grandeur to the genre, driven by Mike McCready's bluesy leads and Eddie Vedder's soulful, baritone delivery. The band's raw energy and stadium-ready sound continued through Vs. and Vitalogy. |
| Mudhoney | Superfuzz Bigmuck | 1988 | This compilation of their debut EP and singles defined the "dirty" sound of the era, utilizing heavy fuzz pedals and a cynical, garage-punk attitude. It remains a foundational text for the genre's aesthetic; Every Good Boy Deserves Fudge is another key release. |
| Stone Temple Pilots | Core | 1992 | Though hailing from California, this debut captured the quintessential mid-tempo crunch and vocal grit associated with the movement. Scott Weiland's versatile performance and the DeLeo brothers' thick riffs are also prominent on their follow-up, Purple. |
| Screaming Trees | Sweet Oblivion | 1992 | Mark Lanegan's gravelly voice and the band's blend of psychedelic rock and hard-hitting rhythms created a uniquely weathered atmosphere. Their blend of Northwest gloom and 60s influence is also evident on Uncle Anesthesia and Dust. |
| Temple of the Dog | Temple of the Dog | 1991 | A tribute to the late Andrew Wood, this supergroup collaboration features a slower, more soulful and blues-influenced take on the Seattle sound. It is a landmark emotional touchstone for the scene, featuring early vocal contributions from Eddie Vedder. |
| Melvins | Houdini | 1993 | Known for their slow, crushing tempos and experimental structures, the Melvins provided the sludge-heavy DNA that many other bands adopted. This album is a masterclass in heavy distortion and rhythmic weight, similar to Bullhead and Lysol. |
| Mother Love Bone | Apple | 1990 | This album bridged the gap between 80s glam and the burgeoning alternative scene with Andrew Wood's charismatic, flamboyant delivery. The band's legacy served as the precursor to Pearl Jam; their Shine EP is equally vital to the genre's history. |
| L7 | Bricks are Heavy | 1992 | Produced by Butch Vig, this album features sludge-heavy guitar tones and a defiant, punk-rock snarl that defined the "Riot Grrrl" intersection of the genre. Their gritty, no-nonsense approach is also showcased on Smell the Magic. |
| Local H | As Good as Dead | 1996 | A duo that achieved a massive wall of sound using specialized guitar pickups, this album captures the cynical, suburban frustration of the post-boom era. Their high-energy, riff-centric style is further explored on Ham Fritter and the Hollow Bodied Chicken. |
| Tad | 8-Way Santa | 1991 | Led by Tad Doyle, the band utilized a massive, lumbering sound that was far heavier and more abrasive than their mainstream contemporaries. This record is a prime example of the "lumberjack" aesthetic and sonic density found on God's Balls. |
| Silverchair | Frogstomp | 1995 | Released when the band members were teenagers, this album proved the genre's international reach with its heavy, post-Black Sabbath riffs and earnest vocals. It serves as a definitive bridge into the mid-90s era, alongside their later work Freak Show. |
| Babes in Toyland | Fontanelle | 1992 | Characterized by Kat Bjelland's "babydoll" vocals shifting into visceral screams, this album offers a violent, rhythmic intensity. The jagged guitar work and raw emotionality are also central to their debut, Spanking Machine. |
| Green River | Dry As a Bone | 1987 | Often cited as the creators of the first true "grunge" record, this band featured future members of Mudhoney and Pearl Jam. Their sound was a messy, high-energy collision of punk and Aerosmith-style rock, also heard on Rehab Doll. |
| Hole | Live Through This | 1994 | This album combines polished production with jagged, searing lyrics and Courtney Love's abrasive vocal delivery. It remains one of the most critically acclaimed and commercially successful female-led records of the movement, alongside the raw Pretty on the Inside. |
| The Gits | Frenching the Bully | 1992 | Mia Zapata's powerful, blues-soaked vocals brought a sense of soul and street-level urgency to the Seattle punk scene. The band's driving rhythms and authentic lyricism are also captured on the posthumous release Enter: The Conquering Chicken. |
| Bush | Sixteen Stone | 1994 | Bringing the sound to a massive UK and global audience, this album features thick, distorted textures and Gavin Rossdale's hushed-to-heavy vocal dynamics. It stands as a commercial pillar of the genre's peak, followed by the more experimental Razorblade Suitcase. |
| Skin Yard | Hallowed Ground | 1988 | Featuring producer Jack Endino, this band was pivotal in shaping the muddy, distorted production style that defined the era. This album showcases an experimental, art-rock edge to the heavy Seattle sound, also present on 1000 Smiling Knuckles. |
| Love Battery | Dayglo | 1992 | This band leaned more into the "psychedelic" side of the branch, using swirling guitar effects and melodic hooks without losing the signature distortion. Their unique, hazy atmosphere is also prominent on the album Far Gone. |
| 7 Year Bitch | ¡Viva Zapata! | 1994 | This album is a raw, aggressive tribute to their fallen friends, characterized by sharp punk-metal riffs and uncompromising vocal delivery. It is a vital document of the Seattle underground's resilience, alongside their debut Sick 'Em. |
| Paw | Dragline | 1993 | Hailing from Kansas, this band incorporated a distinct Southern-rock/country influence into the heavy, distorted framework of the genre. Mark Hennessy's powerful vocals and the band's rural imagery are also found on Death to Traitors. |
| Veruca Salt | American Thighs | 1994 | Blending sugary pop harmonies with heavy, distorted guitars, this album captured the "indie" spirit of the movement's later years. The dual-vocal attack of Nina Gordon and Louise Post is also featured on the Blow It Out Your Ass It's Veruca Salt EP. |
| Candlebox | Candlebox | 1993 | This self-titled debut leaned into a more accessible, blues-oriented rock sound that helped bring the genre to mainstream radio. Kevin Martin's soaring vocals and the band's slicker production are also seen on their follow-up, Lucy. |
| Hammerbox | Numb | 1993 | Carrie Akre's powerful, melodic vocals set this band apart from the more screaming-focused acts of the time. The album features driving, mid-tempo alt-rock rhythms that are also prevalent on their self-titled debut. |
| Grunted | Inside Yours | 1990 | Often considered the first "grunge" band from outside the US (Vancouver), they utilized a heavy, Sabbath-esque groove and deep, resonant vocals. Their slow, punishing style helped bridge the gap to sludge metal, also seen on Ashes to Ashes. |
| The U-Men | Step on a Bug | 1988 | A foundational influence on the entire scene, this band combined swampy blues, jazz-influenced rhythms, and erratic punk energy. Their chaotic, avant-garde approach to heavy rock is also preserved on their various self-titled EPs. |
| Blood Circus | Primal Rock Speech | 1989 | One of the earliest Sub Pop signings, this band played an incredibly stripped-back, primitive form of heavy rock. Their rough-around-the-edges sound is a pure distillation of the genre's early "unwashed" ethos, also heard on their Six-Pack EP. |
| Failure | Magnified | 1994 | While later known for "space rock," this album is a definitive example of the genre's more cerebral and meticulously produced side. It features heavy, fuzzed-out basslines and dissonant guitar textures, a style they refined on the masterpiece Fantastic Planet. |
